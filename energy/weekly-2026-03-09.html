Edward's Take

The market spent the first week of this conflict pricing a short war. Goldman's implied 4-week duration, the modest CDS widening for Saudi and UAE, the S&P only down 2% — all of that says "this resolves quickly." I think the market is wrong about the timeline and possibly about the resolution mechanism. The physical reality at Hormuz is not a pricing problem the DFC can solve with a $20B backstop; it's a safety problem created by active military operations, drone strikes on LNG facilities, and at least four tankers hit. Insurance is available (Bloomberg confirmed this) — but no rational shipowner sends a VLCC through waters where tankers are being struck by drones, regardless of the coverage terms. The binding constraint isn't financial, it's kinetic. Meanwhile, the second-order effects are compounding: Iraq's production collapse means even if Hormuz reopens tomorrow, it'll take weeks for output to recover from forced shut-ins. Qatar's LNG restart will take a month. The force majeure cascade is locking in supply gaps that persist well beyond a ceasefire. Goldman's $150/bbl scenario sounds extreme, but their precondition — "no signs of a solution by next week" — looks increasingly likely. The Gulf SWF divestment review story (FT, Mar 7) is the one that should scare portfolio managers most, because it signals that the geopolitical damage is extending well beyond energy markets into the structural relationship between Gulf capital and US assets. That's not a one-week trade. That's a regime shift.
