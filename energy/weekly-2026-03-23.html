Edward's Take

The market dropped Brent below $100 today and I think it's wrong. Not about the direction — de-escalation is the most likely resolution — but about the timing. The OFAC waiver, the SPR release, and the 48-hour ultimatum are three moves designed to do one thing: push oil prices down before the political cost of $4+ gasoline becomes untenable. The waiver is the most interesting of the three. The US government is, during an active military campaign against Iran, issuing licenses for Iranian oil to flow to market. Read that twice. It means the administration has decided that the price problem is more dangerous than the sanctions architecture. That's a significant signal about priorities. The real test arrives in hours, not weeks. Trump's 48-hour ultimatum expires Monday evening. Iran's counter-threat — close the strait completely and strike Gulf neighbors' energy and water systems — is either a bluff or a preview of the next phase. If it's a bluff, the strait begins reopening and crude has another $15 to fall. If it's not, the $135 Goldman scenario isn't a tail risk anymore; it's a base case. What the consensus is getting wrong: every full-year average forecast I can find ($65–$85 Brent) assumes recovery. Nobody on the Street is publishing a model where oil stays above $100 for the rest of 2026. But United's CEO is planning for it. The insurance market is pricing it. The IEA is calling it the worst energy crisis since the 1970s. There's a gap between the desk forecasts and the operational reality, and when those gaps close, they close violently. The analysts are pricing the most likely scenario; the risk managers are pricing the most dangerous one. I'd watch the risk managers.
