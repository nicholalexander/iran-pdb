OPEC+ & Supply Dynamics

Saudi production collapse: Saudi Arabia has cut oil production by approximately 2 million bpd to around 8 million bpd, after reducing output from two major offshore fields amid the conflict. This is involuntary — driven by inability to export safely and infrastructure protection, not quota management. (Reuters)

Total Gulf production loss: The IEA estimates Gulf countries have cut production by 10 million bpd since hostilities began — equivalent to roughly 10% of global supply. This is the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market. (CBS News, IEA)

IEA record stockpile release: On March 14, 30+ IEA member nations agreed to release 400 million barrels from strategic reserves — the largest coordinated release ever. The US contributes 172M barrels, structured as an exchange (barrels must be returned). Analysts are blunt: at 105M bpd global consumption, 400M barrels covers 3.8 days of world demand or ~20 days of normal Hormuz flows. Patrick De Haan (GasBuddy): "It's kind of like trying to replace a water main with a straw." (CNBC, Bloomberg, Al Jazeera, CBS News)

Russian crude lifeline — limited: Russian crude on tankers totalled ~245M barrels as of Mar 12 (Kpler), roughly equivalent to lost Middle Eastern supply. But China, India, and Turkey were already buying the bulk of Russia's output under sanctions, so the incremental relief from any waiver is far smaller than the headline suggests. (Reuters)

Iran still exporting through its own blockade: Iran has exported 16+ million barrels since March 1, with ~90 ships crossing the strait despite the war. China remains the near-exclusive buyer (11.6% of China's seaborne imports). Iran is weaponizing transit access — allowing its own tankers through while blocking everyone else. (PBS, Euronews, CNN, Reuters/Kpler)

Edward's Take

The Qatar LNG strike changed the character of this crisis. Until March 19, you could tell yourself this was a transit disruption — temporary, recoverable, a function of kinetic risk that degrades as Iran's capabilities are degraded. The destruction of two liquefaction trains at Ras Laffan isn't a transit problem. It's a capacity destruction event with a 3–5 year recovery timeline. Force majeure on long-term contracts with Italy, Belgium, South Korea, and China isn't a week-to-week trade. It's a structural repricing of global LNG supply. Goldman's base case — Brent back to the $70s by Q4 — rests on a gradual April recovery in oil flows. That assumption was already strained by the insurance withdrawal and the 97% collapse in Hormuz transit. Now it also requires ignoring that Qatar, the world's largest LNG exporter, has lost 17% of its capacity for years. The IEA's 400M barrel release is the right response and entirely insufficient. It covers 20 days of Hormuz flows. The conflict is three weeks old with no ceasefire in sight. The math doesn't close. The release will moderate the spike — it already has, pulling Brent from $119 back to $112 — but it can't substitute for 10 million missing barrels per day. What I'd watch most closely: the insurance market. If US/Israeli military operations degrade Iran's anti-ship capability to the point where P&I Clubs reinstate Gulf coverage, traffic could resume rapidly and the entire risk premium unwinds. That's the bull case for normalization, and it's plausible — but it requires military success that hasn't been demonstrated yet. Until then, the binding constraint remains kinetic, not financial, and no amount of SPR releases or reinsurance facilities changes that.
