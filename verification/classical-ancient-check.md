## Section I: Thucydides and the Trap

### Claim 1: Graham Allison's "Thucydides Trap" drawn from Thucydides 1.23.6
- **Quote:** "It was the rise of Athens, and the fear that this inspired in Sparta, that made war inevitable"
- ✅ VERIFIED — This is the standard rendering of Thucydides 1.23.6 as popularized by Allison. Note: "inspired" vs. "instilled" varies by translation. The essay uses "inspired"; the Allison version typically uses "instilled." Minor translation variant.
- **Book:** *Destined for War: Can America and China Escape Thucydides's Trap?* (Houghton Mifflin, 2017) — ✅ VERIFIED

### Claim 2: Sicilian Expedition launched summer 415 BCE
- ✅ VERIFIED

### Claim 3: Segesta requested Athenian aid due to local conflict
- ✅ VERIFIED — Segesta was in conflict with Selinus.

### Claim 4: Athenians "ignorant of the size of the island" (Thuc. 6.1)
- ✅ VERIFIED — Confirmed in multiple translations of Thucydides 6.1.

### Claim 5: Segesta's envoys borrowed gold/silver plate to deceive (Thuc. 6.46)
- ⚠️ PARTIALLY CORRECT — The deception is described, but in Thucydides the Segestans themselves collected plates from neighboring towns and presented them at banquets — not exactly "borrowed gold and silver plate from neighboring cities" as envoys in Athens. The deception happened when Athenian envoys visited Segesta, not the other way around. The essay's version is broadly correct but slightly conflates the details. The citation 6.46 is correct.

### Claim 6: Expedition force — 134 triremes, 5,100 hoplites, approximately 30,000 total personnel
- ✅ VERIFIED — 134 triremes and 5,100 hoplites match Wikipedia/Thucydides exactly. The "30,000 total" is a reasonable estimate including crews (~200 per trireme = ~26,800 rowers alone, plus soldiers and support).

### Claim 7: *Eros* — "A passionate desire (*eros*) to sail fell upon everyone alike" (6.24)
- 📝 PARAPHRASE — Thucydides 6.24 does describe collective enthusiasm using the concept of *eros*, but the exact English wording is a paraphrase/composite of different translations. The reference to eros at 6.24 is well-established in scholarship.

### Claim 8: Sparta fortified Decelea in Attica, disrupted agriculture, cut off silver mines at Laurion (Thuc. 7.27-28)
- ✅ VERIFIED — Thucydides 7.27 discusses the fortification of Decelea and its effects on Athenian agriculture and the Laurion mines.

### Claim 9: Athens oligarchic coup of 411 BCE followed military disaster
- ✅ VERIFIED — The oligarchic revolution of the Four Hundred occurred in 411 BCE, after the Sicilian disaster of 413 BCE.

## Section II: Rome and Regime Change

### Claim 10: Jugurtha, king of Numidia, defeated 105 BCE
- ✅ VERIFIED — Jugurtha was captured/betrayed in 105 BCE.

### Claim 11: Bocchus was Jugurtha's father-in-law who betrayed him
- ✅ VERIFIED — Bocchus I of Mauretania was indeed Jugurtha's father-in-law.

### Claim 12: Jugurtha delivered to Roman general Sulla
- ✅ VERIFIED — Sulla negotiated Bocchus's betrayal.

### Claim 13: Jugurtha paraded in Marius' triumph, executed by strangulation in the Tullianum prison
- ✅ VERIFIED — Confirmed. (Plutarch *Marius* 12; Sallust *BJ* 113-114 citations plausible.)

### Claim 14: Mithridates VI of Pontus fought three wars against Rome between 88 and 63 BCE
- ✅ VERIFIED — First Mithridatic War (88-84), Second (83-81), Third (73-63 BCE).

### Claim 15: Mithridates' suicide in 63 BCE after betrayal by son Pharnaces
- ✅ VERIFIED — Pharnaces II rebelled; Mithridates attempted suicide in 63 BCE. (Appian *Mithridatic Wars* 111-112 citation is plausible.)

### Claim 16: Battle of Carrhae, 53 BCE
- ✅ VERIFIED

### Claim 17: Carrhae = modern Harran, in southeastern Turkey
- ✅ VERIFIED

### Claim 18: Crassus was "the wealthiest man in Rome"
- ✅ VERIFIED — Standard characterization.

### Claim 19: Crassus led seven legions across the Euphrates
- ✅ VERIFIED — Plutarch and other sources confirm seven legions.

### Claim 20: Parthian general Surena destroyed Roman force using mounted archers with "Parthian shot"
- ✅ VERIFIED — Surena (Suren) commanded; the mounted archer tactics are well-documented.

### Claim 21: Crassus killed during negotiations
- ✅ VERIFIED — Plutarch describes Crassus killed during a parley/negotiations.

### Claim 22: 20,000 Romans died; 10,000 captured (Plutarch *Crassus* 25-31; Dio Cassius 40.21-27)
- ✅ VERIFIED — Standard casualty figures for Carrhae. Citations are plausible.

### Claim 23: Trajan conquered Armenia, Mesopotamia, Assyria in 114-116 CE, reaching the Persian Gulf
- ✅ VERIFIED — Confirmed by Britannica and multiple sources.

### Claim 24: Trajan reportedly wept at the shore, saying he was too old to follow Alexander's path to India (Dio Cassius 68.29)
- ✅ VERIFIED — Dio Cassius records this. The citation 68.29 is standard.

### Claim 25: Jewish diaspora revolts in Cyrenaica, Egypt, and Cyprus during Trajan's campaign
- ✅ VERIFIED — The Kitos War (115-117 CE) involved Jewish revolts in those regions.

### Claim 26: Trajan died on return journey; Hadrian abandoned eastern conquests
- ✅ VERIFIED

### Claim 27: Septimius Severus captured Ctesiphon in 198 CE
- ✅ VERIFIED — Oxford Reference gives 28 January 198 CE. (Wikipedia says campaign began in 197, capture on Jan 28, 198.)

### Claim 28: Carus captured Ctesiphon in 283 CE
- ✅ VERIFIED — Carus sacked Ctesiphon in 283 CE.

### Claim 29: Julian attempted to capture Ctesiphon in 363 CE
- ✅ VERIFIED — Julian fought the Battle of Ctesiphon in 363 CE but did not capture the city (won the battle but couldn't take the walls, then retreated and died).

### Claim 30: Ctesiphon = modern Salman Pak, near Baghdad
- ✅ VERIFIED — Ctesiphon ruins are at Salman Pak, ~35 km southeast of Baghdad.

### Claim 31: Julian "attempted to rally Persian Christians against the Sassanid state"
- ❌ FALSE — Julian was a pagan who attempted to *restore* paganism and was hostile to Christianity. He would not have rallied Christians against the Sassanids. This appears to be a fabricated or confused claim. Julian's campaign against the Sassanids was military, not ideological in this way.

### Claim 32: "Crassus against Orodes II" as an example of attempted decapitation
- ⚠️ PARTIALLY CORRECT — Crassus invaded Parthia while Orodes II was king, but Crassus was not attempting to "decapitate" Orodes specifically — he was seeking conquest and glory. The framing as "decapitation" is an interpretive stretch.

### Claim 33: Perseus of Macedon defeated 168 BCE, Macedonia partitioned into four republics which rebelled requiring direct Roman annexation (Livy 45.29; Polybius 36.17)
- ✅ VERIFIED — Perseus defeated at Pydna 168 BCE; Macedonia divided into four republics; later annexed after Andriscus's revolt (149-148 BCE). Citations plausible.

### Claim 34: Cleopatra eliminated, Egypt annexed 30 BCE; emperor prohibited senators from visiting without permission
- ✅ VERIFIED — Egypt became an imperial province; senators were indeed barred from visiting without imperial permission. (Tacitus *Annales* 2.59 and Dio 51.17 are standard references.)

### Claim 35: Roman client kings — *reges socii et amici populi Romani*
- ✅ VERIFIED — Standard Latin term for Roman allied client kings.

## Section III: The Achaemenid Perspective

### Claim 36: Cyrus the Great founded Achaemenid Empire in 550 BCE
- ✅ VERIFIED — Cyrus overthrew the Median kingdom in 550 BCE.

### Claim 37: Alexander conquered Achaemenid Empire between 334 and 323 BCE
- ✅ VERIFIED — Alexander's campaigns ran 334-323 BCE.

### Claim 38: Alexander burned Persepolis in 330 BCE
- ✅ VERIFIED

### Claim 39: Parthians replaced Seleucids in second century BCE, adopted Persian titles and patronized Zoroastrianism
- ✅ VERIFIED — The Arsacid dynasty (Parthians) rose in the mid-3rd to 2nd century BCE and adopted aspects of Persian culture.

### Claim 40: Ferdowsi's *Shahnameh* completed c. 1010 CE
- ✅ VERIFIED — Completed 8 March 1010 CE per Wikipedia.

### Claim 41: Mongol invasions of thirteenth century killed "perhaps ninety percent of the population in some regions"
- 🔍 UNVERIFIABLE — This is a commonly cited but debated figure. Some scholars cite extreme depopulation in specific regions (e.g., Khorasan, Transoxiana), but "ninety percent" for some regions is at the high end of estimates. Not clearly false but not easily verified.

### Claim 42: Iran-Iraq War 1980-1988 killed approximately 500,000 Iranians
- ⚠️ PARTIALLY CORRECT — Most standard sources cite ~500,000 total deaths on BOTH sides (Britannica: "perhaps 500,000" total). Some datasets (Correlates of War) give higher figures for Iran specifically (up to 750,000). The essay's claim of "500,000 Iranians" is defensible under some estimates but conflates the most commonly cited total-both-sides figure with Iranian-only casualties.

### Claim 43: Iran-Iraq War remembered as "Sacred Defense" (*Defa-e Moqaddas*)
- ✅ VERIFIED — Standard term in Iran.

### Claim 44: Safavid conversion to Shi'a Islam in sixteenth century
- ✅ VERIFIED — Ismail I established Twelver Shia as state religion starting 1501.

### Claim 45: Martyrdom of Imam Hussein at Karbala in 680 CE
- ✅ VERIFIED — Battle of Karbala, 10 October 680 CE.

### Claim 46: Behistun inscription of Darius I (c. 520 BCE): "By the favor of Ahura Mazda, I am of such a sort that I am a friend to right, I am not a friend to wrong"
- 📝 PARAPHRASE — The Behistun inscription does contain similar language about Darius's relationship with Ahura Mazda and his opposition to the lie (*drauga*). The specific English rendering is a paraphrase that captures the spirit of the inscription. The date of c. 520 BCE is correct.

### Claim 47: Nowruz celebrated continuously for at least 3,000 years
- ⚠️ PARTIALLY CORRECT — Nowruz has very ancient origins, but "at least 3,000 years" is at the high end. UNESCO recognizes it as having origins going back at least 3,000 years, so this is a common claim, though exact dating is uncertain.

### Claim 60: Melian Dialogue — "the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must" (Thuc. 5.89)
- ✅ VERIFIED — Famous passage from the Melian Dialogue. The citation 5.89 is correct.

## Section V: Democratic War-Making

### Claim 61: Arginusae 406 BCE — Athenian assembly executed six of its own generals for failing to rescue drowning sailors
- ✅ VERIFIED — Six generals were tried and executed after the Battle of Arginusae (406 BCE). The charge was failure to rescue survivors.

### Claim 62: "Including the son of Pericles"
- ✅ VERIFIED — Pericles the Younger was among the executed generals.

### Claim 63: Socrates served as epistates and refused to put illegal collective trial to vote (Plato *Apology* 32b)
- ⚠️ PARTIALLY CORRECT — Plato's *Apology* 32b confirms Socrates' role and his refusal. However, Socrates served as a member of the prytany (his tribe held the presidency), not necessarily as the single epistates on that particular day. Some sources say he was epistates; others say he was among the prytaneis. The claim is close but the specific role is debated.

### Claim 64: Alcibiades' trajectory — championed Sicilian Expedition, recalled for sacrilege, defected to Sparta/Persia/returned/exiled again (Thuc. 6.53-61; Xenophon Hell. 1.4-5)
- ✅ VERIFIED — The broad outline is confirmed. Thucydides 6.53 covers the recall. The full trajectory across Thucydides and Xenophon is accurate.

### Claim 65: Marius' army reforms of 107 BCE opened service to propertyless
- ✅ VERIFIED — Standard account of the Marian reforms.

### Claim 66: Mytilene debate 428 BCE — assembly voted to destroy city, reversed next day by narrow margin (Thuc. 3.36-49)
- ✅ VERIFIED — The Mytilenean debate is in Thucydides 3.36-49. The revolt was in 428/427 BCE.

### Claim 67: Thucydides 3.82.4 — "words changed their ordinary meaning and took that which was now given them" (Corcyra revolution)
- ✅ VERIFIED — Famous passage from Thucydides' account of the revolution at Corcyra. The reference 3.82 is correct.

## Errors Requiring Correction

### ❌ Error 1: Julian rallied Persian Christians (Section II)
**Essay says:** Julian "attempted to rally Persian Christians against the Sassanid state"
**Correct:** Julian (the Apostate) was a pagan revivalist who persecuted Christians. He would not have rallied Christians for anything. His campaign against Persia was a conventional military invasion. This claim is factually incorrect and should be removed or corrected.

### ❌ Error 2: "Samian ambassadors" protested Parthenon funds (Section IV)
**Essay says:** "the Samian ambassadors protested that Athens was 'acting like a vain woman...'"
**Correct:** Plutarch *Pericles* 12 attributes the criticism to Pericles' political opponents in the Athenian assembly (led by Thucydides son of Melesias), not to Samian ambassadors. The "vain woman" metaphor is real but the attribution is wrong.

---

## Notable Partial Errors

1. **Iran-Iraq War casualties:** "500,000 Iranians" — most standard sources cite ~500,000 as TOTAL deaths both sides. Iranian-specific figures vary widely (200,000 to 750,000 depending on source). Recommend qualifying as "hundreds of thousands of Iranians."

2. **Septimius Severus Ctesiphon date:** Essay says 198 CE — technically correct (Jan 28, 198) though some sources shorthand it as 197.

3. **Yarshater as sole editor of Cambridge History of Iran:** He edited vol. 3; the series had many editors.
