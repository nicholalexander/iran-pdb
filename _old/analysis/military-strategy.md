## Abstract

Six days into the most significant US military engagement since the 2003 invasion of Iraq, the combined US-Israeli campaign against Iran — designated **Operation Epic Fury** by the Pentagon — has achieved rapid air superiority, eliminated the supreme leader and 49+ senior officials, degraded approximately 80% of Iran's integrated air defense system, and reduced Iranian ballistic missile launch rates by 90%. Yet the campaign has also opened a second front in Lebanon, triggered a selective closure of the Strait of Hormuz affecting global energy markets, drawn European militaries into defensive deployments, and produced civilian casualties — including 175 children at a school in Minab — that complicate the coalition's political position. This paper provides a preliminary campaign analysis across eight dimensions, drawing on open-source intelligence, ISW-CTP assessments, and multilingual media reporting through March 5, 2026.

---

## 1. Campaign Design & Objectives

### 1.1 The Operational Plan: Decapitation-Degradation Hybrid

The combined US-Israeli campaign represents a doctrinal innovation: a **simultaneous decapitation and degradation strike** that merges two traditionally sequential approaches. On February 28, at approximately 06:00 UTC, the combined force struck across all 24 Iranian provinces in what appears to have been a meticulously pre-planned operation. Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed "with first shots before air defense [was] disabled" (White House statements, March 5) — a remarkable operational sequencing decision that prioritized leadership elimination over the conventional SEAD-first approach.

ISW-CTP identified three campaign priorities that emerged from the opening strikes:

1. **Suppressing Iranian air defenses** — systematic rollback from western Iran toward Tehran
2. **Degrading Iran's capacity to retaliate** — specifically ballistic missile launchers, production facilities, and launch infrastructure
3. **Disrupting Iranian command and control** — targeting IRGC headquarters complexes, intelligence organizations, and internal security apparatus (ISW Evening Report, March 3, 2026)

The IDF outlined a **three-phase air superiority plan**: Phase 1, dismantling air defenses and missile launchers in western Iran; Phase 2, achieving air superiority over western Iran and "paving the way to Tehran"; Phase 3, achieving air superiority over Tehran itself (ISW Morning Report, March 1, 2026). By March 5, IDF Chief of Staff Lt. Gen. Eyal Zamir declared the force had achieved "almost complete aerial superiority" and was transitioning to a "next phase" targeting underground sites and "regime foundations" (Israel Hayom, Reuters, March 5).

### 1.2 Historical Comparisons

**Iraq 2003 (Shock and Awe):** The parallels are instructive but limited. Operation Iraqi Freedom's "shock and awe" phase (March 19-April 9, 2003) used approximately 29,000 munitions over 21 days against a country with degraded air defenses after 12 years of no-fly zone enforcement. Epic Fury has delivered 6,000+ munitions in 2,500 strikes over 6 days against a peer-level IADS — a far more compressed timeline against a more capable adversary. Unlike Iraq 2003, there is no apparent ground invasion component for Iran proper, though Israeli ground forces have crossed into Lebanon.

**Libya 2011 (Odyssey Dawn/Unified Protector):** The Libya campaign began with SEAD and evolved into a protracted air support operation for rebel ground forces over seven months. Epic Fury's decapitation-first approach inverts this model — killing the head of state on Day 1 rather than after months of attrition. The CIA's arming of Kurdish groups (reported by Trump to Walla, March 5; denied by multiple parties) suggests a potential Libya-style proxy ground component, though evidence remains disputed.

**Israel-Hezbollah 2006:** The 34-day Second Lebanon War is perhaps the most relevant negative example. Israel's air campaign failed to stop Hezbollah rocket fire — launches actually increased in the war's second week. In contrast, the combined force's design explicitly addresses this lesson: ISW-CTP noted the campaign was "designed to destroy Iran's ballistic missile capabilities before the force depletes its interceptor stockpiles" (ISW Evening Report, March 3). The 90% reduction in Iranian ballistic launches by Day 6 suggests this attrition calculus is, so far, favorable.

**June 2025 Israel-Iran War:** The most direct precedent. That 12-day conflict saw Israeli strikes on Iranian nuclear and missile facilities, but Iran gave advance warning before retaliatory launches at Al Udeid Air Base — "a signal calibrated to facilitate de-escalation" (War on the Rocks, February 28, 2026). The current conflict is qualitatively different: Iran's retaliation has been maximalist, not signaling.

### 1.3 Stated vs. Actual Objectives

Official US statements have evolved dramatically in six days. Initial framing emphasized defensive counterproliferation. By March 5, Trump's rhetoric had shifted to explicit regime change: "I have to be involved in the appointment [of Iran's next leader], like with Delcy in Venezuela" (Axios, March 5). The White House offered Iranians "immunity" or "absolutely guaranteed death" and called on IRGC forces to "lay down arms" and diplomats to "seek asylum" (BBC Persian, BFM TV, March 5). This represents the most overt regime-change language from a US president since the 2003 Iraq invasion, and it raises the question of whether campaign objectives have expanded beyond the military's initial planning assumptions.

## 2. Force Employment

### 2.1 US Force Structure

The US force package centers on **two carrier strike groups** (including USS Gerald R. Ford, deployed to the region in mid-February), land-based aviation operating from regional bases, and submarine forces. Key platforms and operations:

- **Air Force:** B-2 and likely B-1B bomber operations, now confirmed as staging through RAF Akrotiri, Cyprus, after UK granted basing access (Hegseth, CENTCOM briefing, March 5). F-15E Strike Eagles deployed to Kuwait (three lost to Kuwaiti friendly fire — CENTCOM, March 2). PrSM (Precision Strike Missile) employed operationally for the first time (Sputnik, multiple sources).
- **Navy:** Submarine operations confirmed — IRIS Dena sunk by Mk 48 torpedo (Pentagon, March 4). CENTCOM reports 30+ Iranian naval vessels sunk, including a drone carrier "roughly the size of a World War II aircraft carrier" (Adm. Brad Cooper, March 5). This represents the largest naval engagement since the Falklands War (1982).
- **Cyber/EW:** Israel Health Ministry restricted AI tool use citing cyber attack fears (Mako, March 5). AWS data centers in UAE remain offline. Morgan Stanley assessed AI as "deeply embedded in military contest" (via Chinese media). Sputnik reported first broad operational use of AI in combat.
- **New weapons debuts:** LUCAS autonomous drones, PrSM missile, Khorramshahr-4 interception operations, and Mk 48 heavyweight torpedo in combat (Sputnik, Pentagon).

### 2.2 Israeli Force Employment

The IAF has conducted the majority of strike operations inside Iran — 2,500 strikes delivering 6,000+ munitions as of March 5 (IDF Chief Zamir). Key operational milestones:

- **13 waves of strikes** through March 5, including nighttime operations against Parchin (BBC Persian, March 5)
- **F-35I Adir** shot down an Iranian YAK-130 over Tehran — the first confirmed F-35 air-to-air kill (March 4)
- **Intelligence-driven targeting:** IDF killed MOIS Deputy Minister Yahya Hosseini Panjaki (Israel Affairs directorate) via precision strike in Tehran, and Daoud Ali-Zadeh (role unclear) on March 3 (Iran International)
- **Lebanon second front:** IDF ordered blanket evacuation of Dahiya (500,000 residents) and launched strikes on Hezbollah infrastructure; UNIFIL confirmed ground combat at Kfar Kila with Israeli troops crossing the border (Guardian, NYT, March 5)

### 2.3 Coalition Structure

The coalition is a **hub-and-spoke model** centered on the US-Israel bilateral axis, with concentric rings of participation:

- **Tier 1 (Active belligerents):** United States, Israel
- **Tier 2 (Direct military support):** United Kingdom (Akrotiri basing for bomber operations; 4 Typhoons to Qatar; Wildcat helicopters to Cyprus)
- **Tier 3 (Defensive deployments):** Netherlands (frigate HNLMS Evertsen), Spain (frigate to Cyprus, despite refusing US base access), Italy (ship to Cyprus), Portugal (KC-46 tankers at Lajes)
- **Tier 4 (Logistical/passive):** France (base access with "guarantee" not used offensively — El País), Qatar (shot down 2 Iranian fighters; hosting UK Typhoons)
- **Tier 5 (Aligned but non-participating):** Gulf states (joint condemnation statement: Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE — State Department, March 5)

France's position is particularly nuanced — providing base access with explicit restrictions on offensive use, a political compromise reflecting European ambivalence about the campaign's objectives.

## 3. Iranian Response Assessment

### 3.1 Retaliatory Doctrine in Practice

Iran activated its pre-war doctrine of **distributed retaliation across multiple axes simultaneously**. Since February 28, Iran has launched 500+ ballistic missiles, 2,000+ drones, and an undetermined number of cruise missiles (Fars News Agency via Wikipedia, March 5). The targeting priorities reveal Iran's strategic calculus:

- **~40% directed at Israel**, including strikes on Beit Shemesh (9 dead, March 1), producing 12 dead, 11 missing, and 777 hospitalized in Israel overall
- **~60% directed at US targets and Gulf states**, exploiting what ISW-CTP assessed as "relatively softer GCC defenses compared to Israel or US bases" (ISW Evening Report, March 2). The UAE alone absorbed 1,000+ attacks (UAE official statement, March 5).

This distribution reveals a strategic choice: Iran is attempting to **fracture the coalition by imposing costs on Gulf bystanders**, forcing them to pressure Washington for a ceasefire. The BAPCO refinery strike in Bahrain (March 5) — the first successful hit on a Gulf oil facility — represents an escalation in this approach.

### 3.2 Pre-Delegated Authority

The campaign's opening decapitation strike tested Iran's reported pre-delegated authority structure. The IRGC Ground Forces had decentralized its command in the 2000s-2010s, establishing 32 provincial units designed to operate independently following a leadership decapitation (ISW Evening Report, March 4). The continued — though declining — pace of Iranian missile launches suggests these pre-delegation mechanisms functioned at least partially. However, the shift from ballistic missiles (high-value, complex launch sequences) to drones (simpler, pre-positioned) suggests that the more sophisticated retaliatory capabilities require centralized coordination that has been disrupted.

### 3.3 Missile Inventory Depletion

The declining launch rate tells a clear story of attrition:

| Metric | Day 1 (Feb 28) | Day 6 (Mar 5) | Reduction |
|--------|----------------|---------------|-----------|
| Ballistic missile launches | Baseline | -90% | CENTCOM confirmed |
| Drone launches | Baseline | -83% | CENTCOM confirmed |
| Ballistic launchers destroyed | 0% | 60% | IDF stated |
| Air defenses destroyed | 0% | 80% | IDF stated |

Pre-war Israeli intelligence estimated Iran's ballistic missile inventory at 2,500-3,000 medium-range missiles (BBC Russian). With 500+ ballistic missiles launched and 60% of launchers destroyed, the remaining operational inventory is likely in the low hundreds — a trajectory toward exhaustion within 1-2 weeks at current rates.

The deployment of **Khorramshahr-4 heavy missiles** (1-ton warhead) in IRGC Communiqué #21 (March 5) signals Iran is now deploying strategic reserve assets — typically a sign of desperation rather than escalation dominance.

### 3.4 Proxy Activation

- **Hezbollah:** Entered the conflict on March 1 with rocket strikes and an attack on RAF Akrotiri. By March 5, Lebanon suffered 123 dead and 683 wounded. However, Lebanese PM Nawaf Salam banned Hezbollah military activities (Iran International, March 5) — an unprecedented political move that, if enforceable, would undermine Hezbollah's operational freedom.
- **Houthis:** The Houthi leader gave a televised speech declaring readiness "to act at any moment," but as of March 5, there has been **zero confirmed kinetic action** from Yemen (multiple sources). This is the most significant gap between rhetoric and action in the Axis of Resistance.
- **Iraqi militias:** Iraq has imposed a total communications blackout and cut oil production by 1.5M bbl/day, but no confirmed militia strikes against US forces in Iraq have been reported.

The proxy response has been **notably weaker than pre-war assessments predicted**. The Axis of Resistance's much-theorized "unity of fronts" doctrine has partially activated (Lebanon) but failed to materialize across all axes simultaneously.

## 4. Battle Damage Assessment

### 4.1 Coalition Claims vs. Independent Verification

The reliability of battle damage assessments varies significantly by source:

**Leadership Decapitation:**
- 49+ senior leaders killed (coalition claim). Named and confirmed kills include Khamenei himself, MOIS Deputy Minister Yahya Hosseini Panjaki, and Daoud Ali-Zadeh. The Assembly of Experts was struck during a succession vote (March 3). **Confidence: HIGH** — multiple independent confirmations, succession crisis observable.

**Air Defense Suppression:**
- 80% destroyed (IDF, March 5). BBC Verify satellite imagery confirmed damage to multiple missile sites and Natanz entrance (BBC Russian). The achievement of "almost complete aerial superiority" is indirectly confirmed by the IAF conducting 13 strike waves over Tehran with apparently minimal losses. **Confidence: MODERATE-HIGH** — the operational tempo itself serves as partial verification.

**Naval Destruction:**
- 30+ ships sunk (CENTCOM, March 5). BBC Verify satellite analysis independently confirmed 11 vessels destroyed/damaged (BBC Chinese) — significantly lower than the CENTCOM claim. Named vessels include IRIS Dena (sunk by Mk 48 torpedo, 87 dead), IRGC Shahid Bagheri drone carrier, Jamaran frigate, Bayandor corvette, Naghdi corvette, and Kurdistan forward base ship (ISW Evening Report, March 2). **Confidence: MODERATE** — the 11 vs. 30+ discrepancy suggests either BBC Verify examined limited imagery or CENTCOM's count includes smaller vessels not visible in satellite imagery.

**Missile Capacity:**
- 60% of launchers destroyed (IDF), 90% launch rate reduction (CENTCOM). These metrics are self-reinforcing but not independently verified. The declining launch rate could reflect launcher destruction, ammunition depletion, crew casualties, or command disruption — likely a combination. **Confidence: MODERATE** — the trend is clear, but precise percentages should be treated as estimates.

**Nuclear Facilities:**
- IDF claimed destruction of a "secret facility" (March 3); Witkoff stated Iran had "material for 11 bombs." IAEA confirmed Natanz entrance damaged but initially assessed no nuclear installation damage (ISW Morning Report, March 2). Parchin was struck at nightfall on March 5 (BBC Persian). **Confidence: LOW-MODERATE** — contradictory reporting between IDF claims and IAEA assessments. Parchin's nuclear relevance remains unconfirmed.

### 4.2 Coalition Losses

Coalition losses have been remarkably light relative to the scale of operations:
- **US:** 6 KIA (all Kuwait, 103rd Sustainment Command), 10 seriously wounded
- **Israel:** 12 dead, 11 missing, 777 hospitalized
- **Aircraft:** 3 F-15Es lost to Kuwaiti friendly fire (not enemy action)
- **No confirmed US aircraft losses to enemy fire**

The absence of significant US combat losses six days into a campaign against a peer-level IADS is historically anomalous and suggests either highly effective standoff operations or incomplete reporting. The 3 F-15E friendly-fire losses are notable — fratricide remains a persistent coalition warfare problem.

### 4.3 Iranian Military Losses

- **Personnel:** 1,230 dead (Foundation of Martyrs), 6,000+ wounded (Emergency Services) — both Iranian government figures, likely underestimates
- **Naval:** 30+ vessels sunk (CENTCOM) / 11 confirmed (BBC Verify)
- **Air:** At least 1 YAK-130 shot down; 2 fighters shot down by Qatar; Iran Air fleet losses unreported
- **Infrastructure:** Mehrabad Airport struck; Azadi Stadium destroyed; military bases across provinces hit

### 4.4 Civilian Casualties

- **Iran:** Minab school strike — 175 children killed (updated). Qazvin: 15 dead, 106 wounded from military base strike. 100,000+ fled Tehran.
- **Lebanon:** 123 dead, 683 wounded, 83,000+ displaced, 500,000 evacuated from Dahiya
- **Gulf states:** UAE 3+ dead/94+ wounded; Kuwait 3 dead/32+ wounded (including 11-year-old Iranian national); Bahrain 1 dead/6 wounded
- **Maritime:** IRIS Dena — 87 dead; 2 Indian sailors killed on tanker Skylight; 23,000 Indian sailors stranded

## 5. Escalation Dynamics

### 5.1 The Escalation Ladder (Modified Kahn Framework)

Mapping the conflict against Herman Kahn's escalation ladder, the war has climbed rapidly through multiple rungs:

- **Rungs 1-8 (Subcrisis maneuvering → Conventional crisis):** The January 2026 ultimatum (three demands including permanent enrichment cessation) through February carrier deployments.
- **Rung 9 (Dramatic military confrontations):** February 28 opening strikes — skipping several intermediate rungs.
- **Rungs 12-16 (Large conventional war):** Current state — 2,500 strikes, 500+ ballistic missiles, 2,000+ drones, 30+ ships sunk, 6 US KIA.
- **Rung 17+ (Deliberate nuclear attacks):** Not reached, but the targeting of Natanz and Parchin, combined with Witkoff's "11 bombs" claim, suggests the coalition is deliberately degrading this option for Iran.

**Critical observation:** The conflict **skipped multiple rungs** — there was no graduated escalation, no limited demonstration strikes, no tit-for-tat exchange. The combined force went from diplomatic ultimatum to maximum conventional force in a single step. This is consistent with a pre-planned operation designed to deny Iran escalation dominance by overwhelming its response capacity from the outset.

### 5.2 Red Lines Crossed and Remaining

**Already crossed:**
- Decapitation of head of state (Khamenei killed Day 1)
- Strikes across all 24 provinces
- Nuclear facility targeting (Natanz entrance, possibly Parchin)
- Gulf state territory struck (UAE, Kuwait, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia)
- NATO member territory impacted (Turkey — missile debris in Hatay)
- Strait of Hormuz disrupted
- Second front opened (Lebanon)

**Remaining red lines:**
- **US ground invasion of Iran** — Araghchi warned this would be "a big disaster" (NBC, March 5); no indicators of preparation
- **Nuclear weapon use** — Iran reportedly had material for 11 bombs but no confirmed weaponization
- **NATO Article 5 invocation** — Hegseth explicitly stated Turkey debris "does NOT trigger Article 5"; Russia called any invocation "blatant hypocrisy"
- **Houthi kinetic action in Red Sea** — still at zero despite rhetoric
- **Iranian strikes on Israel's nuclear facilities** — not attempted

### 5.3 The Lebanon Front

The March 5 blanket evacuation of Dahiya (500,000 residents) and IDF ground operations at Kfar Kila represent a **qualitative escalation** — Israel is now fighting a two-front war while conducting deep strikes into Iran. Smotrich's threat that "Dahiya will look like Khan Younis" invokes the 2024 Gaza destruction model. This front expansion raises force allocation questions: every squadron over Lebanon is one not over Iran.

### 5.4 European Entry

The European military response has been calibrated as defensive but is escalating:
- UK providing offensive basing (Akrotiri for bomber runs) — crossing from defensive to active participation
- Spain, Netherlands, Italy deploying naval assets to Cyprus — defensive but forward-positioned
- France providing base access with non-offensive guarantees — an attempt to split the difference

Lavrov's warning that "NATO is being drawn into war against Iran" (EADaily, March 5) reflects Russian anxiety about this trajectory. The European deployments are individually modest but collectively represent the largest European military mobilization in the Middle East since the 2003 Iraq War.

## 6. Strategic Vulnerabilities

### 6.1 Coalition Vulnerabilities

**Force protection in the Gulf:** The 6 US KIA in Kuwait demonstrate the vulnerability of forward-deployed logistics and support personnel. Iranian targeting of "softer" Gulf state territories rather than hardened US bases is strategically rational — it imposes political costs on Gulf hosts without directly engaging America's strongest defenses.

**Interceptor depletion:** ISW-CTP explicitly noted the campaign was designed to "destroy Iran's ballistic missile capabilities before the force depletes its interceptor stockpiles" (ISW Evening Report, March 3). This framing reveals a critical vulnerability: coalition missile defense is a **wasting asset**. The 90% reduction in Iranian launches has eased this pressure, but a prolonged campaign (Politico reports preparations for up to 6 months) could reverse the calculus if Iran reconstitutes launch capability.

**Political will and war aims creep:** Trump's escalation from counterproliferation to explicit regime change ("I have to be involved in the appointment") within six days mirrors the Iraq War's mission creep compressed into days rather than months. Congressional war powers challenges failed (Senate 47-53, House 212-219), but the margin in the House (7 votes) suggests limited political depth for a protracted campaign.

**Coalition cohesion:** France's conditional base access, Spain's base refusal (while deploying a frigate), and the EU's simultaneous condemnation of Iran and call for "dialogue and diplomacy" reveal a fragmented European position. The joint US-Gulf statement is notable for what it is: condemnation of Iran, not endorsement of US operations.

**Friendly fire:** Three F-15Es lost to Kuwaiti air defense represents a serious coalition interoperability failure. In a compressed battlespace with multiple air forces operating simultaneously (US, Israeli, UK, Qatari), IFF (identification friend or foe) challenges will intensify.

### 6.2 Iranian Vulnerabilities

**Command and control collapse:** The decapitation of Khamenei plus 49+ senior officials, combined with strikes on IRGC headquarters complexes (Ground Forces, Navy, Quds Force, Intelligence Organization, Basij, Electronic Warfare — all struck March 4 per ISW), has degraded Iran's ability to coordinate operations. The shift from ballistic missiles to drones likely reflects this C2 disruption — drones can be launched with pre-positioned, pre-programmed autonomous guidance; ballistic missiles require more complex targeting coordination.

**Missile inventory exhaustion:** At pre-war estimates of 2,500-3,000 ballistic missiles (Israeli intelligence via BBC Russian), with 500+ launched and 60% of launchers destroyed, Iran's residual capacity is approaching a critical threshold. The deployment of Khorramshahr-4 strategic reserves confirms this assessment.

**Internet blackout as double-edged sword:** The 130+ hour communications blackout suppresses domestic dissent (Basij forces fired on buildings in Tehran's Sattarkhan neighborhood after hearing anti-regime chanting — ISW Evening Report, March 2) but also degrades military coordination and civilian resilience. The Health Ministry's defensive insistence of "no shortage" of medicine and the Tehran Governor's warning against "panic buying" suggest the blackout is amplifying rather than containing social disruption.

**Regime survival:** The succession to Mojtaba Khamenei (56) — rejected by Trump as "a lightweight" — faces immediate legitimacy challenges. The Interim Leadership Council (Pezeshkian + Mohseni Ejei + Arafi) is a committee structure ill-suited to wartime decision-making. Iran contacted the CIA via a third country on March 1 (NYT) while publicly denying all contact — suggesting a split between diplomatic and military leadership on war termination.

**Naval annihilation:** With 30+ vessels sunk (CENTCOM) or 11+ confirmed (BBC Verify), Iran's navy has ceased to exist as a conventional force. The selective Hormuz closure is being maintained through mines and shore-based missiles rather than naval surface action — a capability that degrades as the air campaign continues.

## 7. Endgame Scenarios

### 7.1 Scenario A: Regime Collapse (15-25% probability)

The combination of leadership decapitation, military degradation, internet blackout, economic isolation, and internal dissent could produce regime collapse within weeks to months. Indicators: anti-regime protests emerging despite blackout (Basij already responding to chanting), IRGC factional splits, mass military surrenders, or provincial fragmentation. The CIA Kurdish operation (if real) could accelerate this in western provinces.

**Obstacles:** The IRGC's 32 provincial units were designed for exactly this scenario — decentralized operations following decapitation. The Basij's social control apparatus remains functional. Iran's population of 88 million and mountainous terrain make external regime change without ground forces virtually impossible.

### 7.2 Scenario B: Negotiated Ceasefire (25-35% probability)

Iran's March 1 CIA contact suggests interest in negotiation despite public defiance. Potential terms: Iran accepts enrichment limits and missile constraints in exchange for ceasefire and sanctions relief. The declining missile inventory creates a "last card" dynamic — Iran may negotiate while it still has something to bargain with.

**Obstacles:** Trump's "I said Too Late!" and regime-change rhetoric closes diplomatic off-ramps. Araghchi's "not asking for ceasefire" and "ready for ground invasion" posture suggests Iran is not yet at its pain threshold. No mediator has emerged (Oman's Sultan remains silent; Witkoff "cut contacts").

### 7.3 Scenario C: Frozen Conflict / Attrition (30-40% probability — most likely)

The campaign continues for weeks to months (Politico: possibly until September) at declining intensity as Iran's retaliatory capacity is exhausted. The combined force achieves its military objectives (missile degradation, nuclear disruption) without forcing regime collapse. Iran enters a period of internal instability, contested succession, and economic crisis while maintaining nominal sovereignty. Hormuz gradually reopens under US naval escort. Hezbollah is degraded but not destroyed.

**This is the Iraq 1991 model** — military objectives achieved, regime survives weakened, long-term instability follows.

### 7.4 Scenario D: Wider Regional War (10-15% probability)

Houthi activation in the Red Sea, Turkish NATO mechanisms, or an Iranian "use it or lose it" nuclear/chemical escalation could widen the conflict beyond current bounds. The triple chokepoint crisis (Hormuz + Red Sea + Qatar LNG disruption) has already produced global economic effects (Brent $84, European gas +70%, gold $5,400 ATH).

**Indicators to watch:** Houthi kinetic action (currently zero), Turkish Article 5 discussion (Hegseth denied), Iranian WMD indicators (Witkoff's "11 bombs" claim), and Russian/Chinese material support (contradictory signals — Iran claims help, Russia denies).

### 7.5 What "Victory" Looks Like

**For the United States:** Elimination of Iran's nuclear breakout capability, destruction of ballistic missile infrastructure sufficient to guarantee Gulf and Israeli security, and a successor regime willing to accept US terms. Trump's statements suggest nothing less than a compliant successor government will satisfy stated aims — a maximalist position historically associated with protracted commitments.

**For Iran:** Regime survival with deterrent capacity intact, US withdrawal under political pressure, and enhanced legitimacy from having "resisted" the world's superpower. Even a severely degraded Iran that survives this campaign can claim victory in the same way Saddam Hussein claimed victory after 1991. Araghchi's "quagmire" framing signals this is the Iranian theory of victory.

## 8. Lessons Learned (Preliminary)

### 8.1 Decapitation Strikes in the 21st Century

Operation Genesis — the killing of Khamenei "with first shots before air defense disabled" — represents the most significant leadership decapitation since the US killed Qasem Soleimani in 2020, but at a categorically different scale. The operation demonstrates that modern ISR and precision strike can eliminate a head of state despite extensive security measures. However, the Iranian system's pre-delegated authority and decentralized command structure has prevented total military collapse — the lesson being that **decapitation degrades but does not immediately defeat** a state that has planned for this contingency.

### 8.2 Ballistic Missile vs. Air Defense Attrition

The central operational equation of this war — destroying Iranian launchers before depleting coalition interceptors — is a **new paradigm in modern warfare**. ISW-CTP explicitly identified this as the campaign's design logic. The 90% reduction in Iranian launches by Day 6 suggests the offense currently has the advantage in this equation, but the result is not preordained: a larger missile inventory, better-concealed mobile launchers, or decoy operations could have reversed the calculus. This war will become the primary case study for missile offense-defense balance for decades.

### 8.3 Cloud Infrastructure as Military Target

The continued outage of AWS data centers in the UAE highlights a novel vulnerability: **commercial cloud infrastructure in conflict zones becomes a de facto military target** whether or not it hosts military applications. The Israeli Health Ministry's restriction of AI tools due to cyber fears (Mako, March 5) further demonstrates how digital dependencies create attack surfaces. Morgan Stanley's assessment that AI is "deeply embedded in military contest" and the reported Pentagon-Anthropic dispute over AI weapons use signal that the civil-military AI boundary has effectively dissolved.

### 8.4 THAAD and Missile Defense Performance

While specific interception rates remain classified, the overall picture suggests missile defense has performed adequately but not perfectly: 12 Israeli dead and 777 hospitalized despite 200+ ballistic missiles aimed at Israel; Bahrain's BAPCO refinery struck; Kuwait taking multiple hits. The UAE's reported interception of 6/7 ballistic missiles (CBS) — an 86% rate — is consistent with published THAAD performance claims but demonstrates that even high interception rates allow leakers against large salvos. The lesson: **missile defense buys time; it does not provide immunity.**

### 8.5 The Selective Strait: Economic Warfare in the Hormuz Era

Iran's selective Hormuz closure — letting Chinese ships through while blocking Western-aligned traffic — represents a **new model of economic warfare**. Chinese SOE executives negotiated exemptions directly (WSJ via Chinese media). This weaponization of a global chokepoint on a discriminatory basis creates a two-tier global economy that advantages China and disadvantages US allies. The ~200 ships anchored in the Gulf, Maersk's suspension of all Gulf bookings, and Japan's emergency request for strategic reserve release demonstrate the systemic fragility.

### 8.6 Coalition Warfare and Friendly Fire

The loss of 3 F-15Es to Kuwaiti friendly fire — the only confirmed US aircraft losses — is a stark reminder that **coalition operations in compressed airspace remain inherently dangerous**. With US, Israeli, UK, Qatari, and potentially other air forces operating over or near the same battlespace, alongside active Iranian air defenses and retaliatory launches, the IFF challenge is immense.

### 8.7 Information Operations and Narrative Warfare

The information environment features at least five competing narratives: US/Israeli military confidence messaging (phase transitions, percentage claims), Iranian defiance and false-flag accusations (claiming US/Israel are striking Gulf states with Iranian-looking drones), Gulf state victim positioning (UAE: "1,000+ attacks, did not participate"), Russian wedge-driving (Lavrov: "NATO drawn into war"), and Chinese strategic positioning (selective Hormuz passage). Saudi state TV's explicit warning about disinformation campaigns targeting Gulf states represents a remarkable meta-level acknowledgment of information warfare.

### 8.8 The Speed of Escalation

Perhaps the most significant lesson is the **velocity of modern conflict escalation**. In six days, this campaign has killed a head of state, opened two fronts, involved a dozen countries, disrupted global energy markets, created a triple chokepoint crisis, triggered European military deployments, and produced over 1,500 confirmed fatalities across multiple countries. The pre-war assumption that escalation would follow a graduated ladder has been invalidated — both sides jumped to near-maximum conventional intensity within hours of the first strike.

---

## Conclusion

Six days into Operation Epic Fury, the combined US-Israeli force has achieved remarkable tactical and operational success: air superiority over Iran, 90% reduction in retaliatory fire, destruction of the supreme leader and senior leadership, and near-elimination of the Iranian navy. Yet strategic success remains elusive. The campaign's objectives have expanded faster than its capabilities — from counterproliferation to regime change in less than a week — while the conflict has generated second-order effects (Hormuz disruption, Lebanon front, European entanglement, global energy crisis) that extend far beyond the original casus belli.

The critical question for the coming weeks is whether tactical military dominance can be converted into a durable strategic outcome, or whether — as in Iraq 2003, Libya 2011, and Afghanistan 2001 — the phase transition from military operations to political objectives proves to be the campaign's undoing. Iran's theory of victory does not require military success; it requires survival. The combined force's theory of victory increasingly requires something approaching regime change. History suggests the latter is far harder to achieve through airpower alone.
