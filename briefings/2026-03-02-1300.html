§16. 🦉 Edward's Analysis

Three things happened today that matter more than the bombs.

First, Lebanon broke with Hezbollah. Nawaf Salam banning Hezbollah's military activities and ordering weapon surrender is, on paper, the most consequential political development since the strikes began. It won't be enforced — the Lebanese Armed Forces couldn't disarm Hezbollah if they tried — but that's not the point. The point is that the Lebanese state just said, on the record, that Hezbollah's armed wing is illegal. That's a lever that will be pulled for years. It destroys the fiction that Hezbollah operates with state consent. Whether Salam did this under pressure, out of genuine alarm, or as political theater — the words can't be unsaid.

Second, Qatar shot down manned fighter jets. Intercepting missiles is defensive. Destroying piloted aircraft is something else. Qatar just crossed a threshold that could make it a target of directed Iranian retaliation, while hosting Al Udeid. If Iran treats Qatar as a combatant, the geometry changes.

Third, the nuclear thread is unraveling in both directions. NRC's reporting that 400kg of enriched uranium may have been moved to Isfahan before the strikes is, if true, devastating to the stated rationale. Trump's "last best chance" framing assumes the nuclear threat is being eliminated. If Iran secured its fissile material, the strikes destroyed facilities but not capability. Fresh explosions at Isfahan suggest both sides may be racing toward the same underground complex.

What concerns me most: Trump saying "the big wave hasn't even happened" after three days of the most intensive air campaign since Iraq 2003. Combined with the explicit refusal to rule out ground troops and the 4-5 week timeline, the rhetoric is being set for a much larger operation. The MAGA fracture tells me domestic political cost is registering. The war powers vote is theater — it won't survive a veto — but the count will tell us if this war has a ceiling.

Oil retreating from morning highs is the market betting this stays contained. Goldman's 130% model is the market admitting it might not. Both can't be right.
