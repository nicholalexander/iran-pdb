16. 🦉 Edward's Analysis

Three things happened this cycle that, taken together, paint a picture nobody in Washington is going to like.

First, Trump told Jonathan Karl the US had identified candidates to replace the Iranian regime — and then killed them in the initial strikes. The United States government identified the people it wanted to install after regime change and then bombed them to death. This is not a policy failure in the conventional sense. This is what happens when strike planning and political planning happen in different buildings with different priorities. The military optimized for maximum leadership decapitation; the political side wanted specific people alive. Nobody reconciled the two. The result is an administration that has explicitly admitted — on the record, to ABC News — that it destroyed its own post-war plan on Day 1.

Second, the Larijani denial is more significant than it looks. Larijani is the SNSC secretary — the closest thing to a national security advisor in the interim structure. If Trump's backchannel existed, Larijani would know. His public denial either means the backchannel was fabricated by Trump (to project control), or it existed and Iran has now closed it to avoid looking weak under bombardment. Either way, the diplomatic off-ramp that Trump announced 12 hours ago is gone.

Third, the AMAN vs Mossad missile discrepancy is deeply concerning. Two arms of the same government giving numbers that differ by 150%. AMAN says 2,500; Mossad says 1,000. If AMAN is right, Iran has substantial retaliatory capacity left and weeks of missile fire to go. If Mossad is right, Iran is nearly spent. War planning presumably depends on one of these numbers.

The Lebanon front opening changes everything. This is no longer US-Israel vs Iran. Hezbollah is conducting sustained operations — 13 attacks in 24 hours. 31 dead in Lebanon in one night. The November 2024 ceasefire is dead. Israel is fighting on three fronts, the US on two. The geographic scope doubled overnight.

Oil markets are doing something unexpected: not panicking as predicted. Brent pulled back to $78 from above $80. Either the market is pricing in a short war, or it's pricing in Hormuz being less closed than announced — which the FM's contradictory statement supports.

The broader picture: 72+ hours into an operation whose stated goal (regime change) has been undermined by its own success (killed the replacement candidates), whose diplomatic channel has been publicly denied, whose intelligence agencies can't agree on the enemy's remaining capacity, and which has expanded from one theater to three. The think tank consensus — that aerial bombing has a "terrible record at fomenting regime change" — is being proved correct in real time, and we're not even through Week 1.
