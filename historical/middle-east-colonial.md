## Introduction

On February 28, 2026, American and Israeli forces launched Operation Epic Fury against the Islamic Republic of Iran — the largest American military operation since the 2003 invasion of Iraq. In the opening minutes, a precision strike killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. Within six days, 2,500 strikes had delivered over 6,000 munitions across all twenty-four Iranian provinces, 175 children lay dead at a school in Minab, and the President of the United States was publicly demanding to be "involved in the appointment" of Iran's next leader.

To Washington, this was a preemptive counterproliferation campaign — surgical, justified, forward-looking. To Tehran, and to much of the broader Middle East, it was something far older: the latest iteration of a pattern that stretches back through the 1953 CIA coup, through the British and Russian carve-up of Iran in 1907, through the Sykes-Picot Agreement of 1916, through a century and more of Western powers deciding who rules the lands between the Mediterranean and the Hindu Kush, and what those rulers may or may not possess. The question this essay poses is not whether the 2026 strikes were militarily effective — they manifestly were — but what they mean when read against the long arc of Middle Eastern history, and what that history suggests about what comes next.

History does not repeat, as the misattributed Twain quote insists, but it does rhyme with a regularity that should concern anyone paying attention. The rhymes between 2026 and 1953, between the fall of the Ottoman Empire and the potential fragmentation of Iran, between the Sykes-Picot partition and Trump's demand to choose Iran's next leader, are not coincidences. They are consequences of structural forces that have operated in this region for over a century: the strategic importance of hydrocarbon resources, the asymmetry of military power between Western and Middle Eastern states, and the persistent Western conviction that the region's political arrangements are subject to external revision.

This essay attempts to place the events of late February and early March 2026 in that longer context — not to excuse or justify any party's actions, but to understand why this particular war, in this particular place, carries a weight of historical meaning that neither precision-guided munitions nor presidential Truth Social posts can dispel.

## I. Iran's Historical Memory: Seventy Years of Interference

### The Ghost of Mossadegh

No event in modern Iranian political consciousness carries the weight of the 1953 coup. When Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (AIOC) in 1951, he was asserting a principle that would seem unremarkable in any other context: that a sovereign nation's natural resources belong to its people. The AIOC — predecessor to British Petroleum — had operated under a concession granted in 1901 to William Knox D'Arcy, paying Iran a pittance while extracting enormous wealth. Mossadegh's nationalization was wildly popular, democratically enacted, and legally defensible. It was also intolerable to London and, by extension, to Washington.

Operation Ajax, executed jointly by the CIA and MI6 in August 1953, overthrew Mossadegh and restored Mohammad Reza Shah to absolute power. The operation cost approximately $1 million — a trivial sum even by 1953 standards — and its architects, Kermit Roosevelt Jr. and Donald Wilber, regarded it as a masterpiece of covert action. In Washington and London, it was filed away as a Cold War success. In Iran, it became the foundational trauma of modern political identity.

The significance of 1953 is not merely that a democratically elected government was overthrown. It is that the overthrow foreclosed a democratic path for Iranian political development. Mossadegh represented a secular, nationalist, parliamentary tradition that had deep roots in the Constitutional Revolution of 1905-1911. His removal did not merely change a government; it discredited an entire mode of politics. If democracy could be erased by a foreign intelligence service in a matter of days, then democracy was not a viable vehicle for Iranian sovereignty. This lesson was absorbed by the generation that would make the 1979 revolution — and it has never been unlearned.

### The Shah's Quarter-Century

The twenty-six years of the Shah's restored rule (1953-1979) confirmed every suspicion that the coup had planted. Mohammad Reza Pahlavi modernized Iran's infrastructure while building one of the most feared secret police forces in the world. SAVAK — the Organization of Intelligence and National Security — was trained by the CIA and Israel's Mossad. Its methods included systematic torture: electric shock, nail extraction, the insertion of heated objects, and mock executions. SAVAK operated with impunity, and its relationship with American intelligence was an open secret. When President Jimmy Carter toasted the Shah in Tehran on New Year's Eve 1977, calling Iran "an island of stability in one of the more troubled areas of the world," he was toasting a man whose security apparatus had tortured thousands of his own citizens with American technical assistance.

The revolution of 1979 was, at its deepest level, a repudiation of this arrangement. Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini's genius — and it was a form of genius, however one judges its consequences — was to fuse anti-imperialism with Shia Islamic identity into a revolutionary ideology that could not be co-opted by either superpower. The phrase "neither East nor West, only the Islamic Republic" (*na sharqi, na gharbi, faqat jomhuri-ye eslami*) was not merely a slogan. It was an ontological claim: that Iran's political identity existed independent of the Cold War binary, rooted in a civilizational tradition older than either the United States or the Soviet Union. This principle — sovereignty as theological imperative — has governed Iranian strategic thinking for forty-seven years, through war, sanctions, and now bombardment.

### The Iran-Iraq War: The West's Chosen Silence

If 1953 planted the seed of Iranian mistrust, the Iran-Iraq War of 1980-1988 made it flower into something close to civilizational conviction. When Saddam Hussein invaded Iran in September 1980, the international community's response was instructive. The United Nations Security Council did not condemn the invasion. The United States, despite its official neutrality, provided Iraq with satellite intelligence, military equipment through third-party transfers, and — most damningly — looked the other way when Saddam deployed chemical weapons against Iranian soldiers and Kurdish civilians.

The scale of chemical warfare was staggering. Iraq used mustard gas, tabun, and sarin against Iranian forces in at least forty documented engagements. The attack on Halabja in March 1988, which killed between 3,200 and 5,000 Kurdish civilians, was carried out with chemicals whose precursors were supplied by Western European firms, a fact well known to Western intelligence services. The United States initially attempted to blame Iran for the Halabja attack — a piece of disinformation that the Defense Intelligence Agency helped construct and that was not formally corrected for years.

For Iranians, the Iran-Iraq War is not ancient history. It is living memory. One million Iranians were killed or wounded. Every city has its martyrs' cemetery. Every family has its story. The war ended with UN Resolution 598, which Khomeini accepted by comparing it to "drinking poison." The phrase has entered the Iranian political lexicon as shorthand for the anguish of accepting an unjust peace imposed by a world order that had watched Iran bleed for eight years while supplying its enemy.

### The Nuclear Program as Sovereignty

It is in this context that Iran's nuclear program must be understood — not as a weapons project (though it may have been that too), but as an assertion of technological sovereignty. The right to enrich uranium under the Non-Proliferation Treaty became, in Iranian political discourse, a synecdoche for the right to exist as an independent state. When Mahmoud Ahmadinejad displayed centrifuge cascades as national achievements, he was speaking to an audience for whom mastery of the nuclear fuel cycle represented proof that Iran could not be reduced to a client state again.

The 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) represented a rare moment of diplomatic possibility — Iran accepted constraints on enrichment in exchange for sanctions relief and implicit acceptance of its right to a civilian nuclear program. The Trump administration's withdrawal from the JCPOA in 2018 confirmed what Iranian hardliners had always argued: that agreements with the United States were not worth the paper they were printed on. The subsequent "maximum pressure" campaign, which drove Iran's economy into crisis without producing either regime change or a better deal, vindicated the most cynical elements of Iran's political establishment.

### How Iranians Understand 2026

Against this backdrop, the events of February 28, 2026 do not arrive as a surprise. They arrive as a confirmation. For seventy-three years — from Ajax to Epic Fury — the pattern has held: when Iran asserts sovereignty in ways that challenge Western interests, force follows. The specific trigger changes (oil nationalization, revolution, nuclear enrichment), but the structural dynamic remains constant. An Iranian watching the Minab school footage or listening to Trump declare his intention to choose Iran's next leader does not need a historian to explain what is happening. The history is written in their family's bones.

This is not to say that all Iranians support their government — many manifestly do not, as the 2009 Green Movement, the 2017-2018 protests, and the 2022 Woman, Life, Freedom movement demonstrated. But there is a critical distinction between opposing one's own government and welcoming foreign bombardment. The Islamic Republic has survived for forty-seven years in part because every time internal opposition reaches a critical threshold, external aggression provides the regime with its most potent argument: that the real threat is not domestic tyranny but foreign domination. Operation Epic Fury is, in this sense, the greatest gift the United States could have given to the very ideology it claims to oppose.

## II. The Colonial Pattern: Sykes-Picot and Its Successors

### Drawing Lines in the Sand

In May 1916, Sir Mark Sykes of Britain and François Georges-Picot of France drew lines on a map of the Ottoman Empire's Arab provinces, dividing them into spheres of influence with a casualness that still staggers. The agreement, later modified by the inclusion of Russian and Italian claims, created the template for the modern Middle East: Iraq (a British creation joining three Ottoman provinces with little in common), Syria and Lebanon (French mandates carved to maximize Christian-Muslim tensions that could be exploited for colonial control), Palestine (promised simultaneously to Arabs and Jews in contradictory commitments), and Transjordan (invented as a consolation prize for the Hashemite dynasty expelled from the Hejaz).

The mandate system that formalized these arrangements after World War I was colonialism wearing a League of Nations mask. Article 22 of the League Covenant described the mandates as a "sacred trust of civilization" for peoples "not yet able to stand by themselves." The language of civilizational tutelage — the assumption that Middle Eastern peoples required Western guidance to achieve political maturity — persisted long after the mandates formally ended. It persists, one might argue, in Trump's declaration that he must be "involved in the appointment" of Iran's next leader.

### Oil and the Architecture of Control

The discovery and exploitation of Middle Eastern oil transformed the colonial relationship from one of strategic positioning to one of existential economic interest. The 1901 D'Arcy Concession in Iran, the 1928 Red Line Agreement that carved up Ottoman oil territories among Western companies, the 1933 Saudi concession to Standard Oil of California (later ARAMCO) — these agreements established a pattern in which the region's primary natural resource was extracted under terms set by foreign powers and enforced, when necessary, by foreign arms.

The 1953 Iranian coup was, at its core, an oil dispute. The 1991 Gulf War was fought to restore the regional oil order disrupted by Saddam's invasion of Kuwait. The 2003 Iraq invasion, whatever its stated rationale, occurred in a country sitting atop the world's fifth-largest proven reserves. And now, in 2026, the war unfolds against a backdrop of Hormuz closure, Ras Tanura shutdown, and Qatar LNG force majeure — the entire global energy architecture shuddering because Western military power has once again been deployed in the region that fuels the world.

### Where Does 2026 Fit?

Is Operation Epic Fury the final act of the colonial era, or something new? The answer, uncomfortably, is both. The structural elements are colonial: a Western power using military force to determine the political arrangements of a Middle Eastern state, justified by the claim that the target state's sovereign choices (nuclear enrichment) pose an unacceptable threat to international order. The method — precision strikes rather than territorial occupation — is new, but the underlying logic is Sykes-Picot logic: the region's political arrangements are subject to external veto.

What is genuinely novel is the absence of even the pretense of multilateral legitimacy. Sykes-Picot had the fig leaf of wartime necessity. The mandates had the League of Nations. The 1991 Gulf War had UN Security Council Resolution 678. Even the 2003 Iraq invasion was preceded by months of UN inspections and a (fabricated) case presented to the Security Council. Operation Epic Fury has none of these. It represents colonial prerogative stripped of colonial ceremony — the raw assertion that military power confers the right to determine who rules and what they may possess, without the need to justify that assertion to any international body.

## III. The Sunni-Shia Dimension: Fourteen Centuries of Division

### Karbala as Living Memory

On the tenth of Muharram in 61 AH (October 10, 680 CE), Husayn ibn Ali — grandson of the Prophet Muhammad and the central figure of Shia Islam — was killed with most of his family and companions on the plains of Karbala in what is now Iraq. His small band, denied water for days, was massacred by the forces of the Umayyad caliph Yazid I. Husayn's severed head was carried to Damascus. His surviving family members were paraded through the streets.

Karbala is not, for Shia Muslims, a historical event in the way that Gettysburg is a historical event for Americans — something studied, commemorated, and filed under the past. It is a living wound, ritually re-experienced every year during Ashura, when millions of Shia mourn Husayn's martyrdom with processions, passion plays (*ta'ziyeh*), and acts of devotion that blur the boundary between past and present. The theological lesson of Karbala — that the righteous may be outnumbered, outpowered, and killed by the forces of tyranny, but that their sacrifice redeems history — is the emotional foundation of Shia political consciousness.

When Iranian state media frames the 2026 war as a new Karbala, this is not mere propaganda. It draws on a reservoir of meaning that is fourteen centuries deep. The role of Yazid — the unjust ruler who deploys overwhelming force against the righteous — is assigned to the United States. The role of Husayn — the martyr who refuses to submit — is claimed for Iran. The theological framework transforms military defeat into spiritual victory: to be struck down by a superior force is not humiliation but sanctification.

### Saudi Arabia at the Gates

The entry of Saudi Arabia into the anti-Iranian alignment — formalized in the joint US-Gulf condemnation statement and operationalized through the basing and overflight permissions that enable the air campaign — carries a sectarian charge that transcends geopolitics. Saudi Arabia is the custodian of Mecca and Medina, the birthplace of Wahhabism, and the most powerful Sunni state in the Muslim world. Its alignment against Shia Iran in a war of regime change activates historical memories that predate the modern state system entirely.

The Saudi-Iranian rivalry has been framed in many ways: as a struggle for regional hegemony, as a proxy for the Arab-Persian ethnic divide, as a competition for leadership of the Muslim world. All of these framings capture something real. But the sectarian dimension is irreducible. When Saudi-aligned clerics have described Shia as *rafidha* (rejectors) or questioned their status as Muslims, and when Iranian clerics have framed Saudi Wahhabism as a deviant corruption of Islam, they are drawing on theological disputes that date to the succession crisis after the Prophet's death in 632 CE. The 2026 war does not create this sectarian fault line, but it widens it to a degree not seen since the Iran-Iraq War, when Saddam — a secular Ba'athist — was nevertheless framed as the champion of Sunni Arab civilization against Persian Shia expansionism.

The potential consequences are severe. If the war is perceived across the Muslim world as a Sunni-Western alliance destroying a Shia state, the sectarian radicalization will outlast any ceasefire. The Shia communities of Iraq, Bahrain, Lebanon, Saudi Arabia's Eastern Province, and Pakistan will draw conclusions about their place in a regional order that has just bombed the only state that claimed to represent their interests. The historical parallel is the Crusades — not in the simplistic way that term is sometimes invoked, but in the sense that a military campaign framed by one side as strategic necessity will be remembered by the other as civilizational aggression for generations.

## IV. Ottoman Collapse as Template

### The Sick Man Dies

When the Ottoman Empire finally dissolved after World War I, it ended six centuries of political order across a vast territory spanning southeastern Europe, Anatolia, the Arab world, and North Africa. The post-Ottoman settlement created the modern Middle East — and the modern Middle East has been unstable ever since. The reasons are instructive for anyone contemplating the consequences of regime change in Iran.

The Ottoman system, for all its flaws, provided a framework for managing diversity. The *millet* system granted religious communities a degree of autonomy. Provincial governance, while often corrupt, maintained a recognizable chain of authority. The empire's collapse produced not liberation but fragmentation: mandatory powers imposed artificial boundaries on ethnically and religiously diverse populations, installed client monarchs with no local legitimacy, and created states whose primary function was to serve the economic interests of their colonial patrons.

Iraq is the paradigmatic case. The British joined the predominantly Shia south, the Sunni center, and the Kurdish north into a single state, then installed a Hashemite king from the Hejaz to rule it. The resulting polity required continuous coercion to maintain — from the British RAF's aerial bombing of Kurdish and Shia rebellions in the 1920s, through the Ba'athist police state, through the American occupation that followed 2003. A century later, Iraq remains a state whose internal coherence depends on external pressure or internal repression.

### The Iranian Difference — and the Risk

Iran is not an artificial state. Its civilizational continuity stretches back to the Achaemenid Empire of the sixth century BCE. Persian identity — linguistic, cultural, literary — has survived Arab conquest, Mongol invasion, and Turkic dynasty. The modern Iranian state, despite its multi-ethnic composition (Persians, Azerbaijanis, Kurds, Baluchis, Arabs, Lurs, Turkmen), possesses a national coherence that Iraq, Syria, and Lebanon never achieved.

But this coherence is not indestructible. The Islamic Republic has maintained it through a combination of ideological mobilization, economic distribution (however corrupt), and security force control. If the regime collapses under the weight of bombardment — Scenario C in the strategic synthesis, assessed at 10-15% probability — the centrifugal forces that have been contained for forty-seven years could emerge with devastating speed. Kurdish separatism in the northwest, Baluchi insurgency in the southeast, Arab unrest in Khuzestan, Azerbaijani nationalism in the northwest — these movements have been suppressed, not resolved.

The Ottoman precedent suggests that the collapse of a multi-ethnic, religiously legitimated imperial order produces not democratic transformation but prolonged fragmentation, foreign intervention, and sectarian violence. The post-Ottoman Middle East took decades to stabilize — and the "stability" it achieved was the stability of authoritarian states maintained by foreign patronage. If Iran follows the Ottoman template, the result will not be the liberal democracy that regime-change advocates imagine. It will be a generation of chaos in a country of ninety million people, bordering Iraq, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Turkey, and the Caspian states — every one of which has its own ethnic and sectarian fault lines that Iranian fragmentation would activate.

## V. The Mossadegh Parallel: The Law of Unintended Consequences

### The Cycle

The trajectory from 1953 to 2026 traces a pattern that should give any strategist pause.

In 1953, the United States and Britain overthrew a democratic government to secure oil interests and Cold War positioning. The intended consequence — a pliant, pro-Western Iran — was achieved. The unintended consequence — a quarter-century of authoritarian rule that discredited secular democracy and created the conditions for revolutionary Islamism — was not foreseen.

In 1979, the revolution that overthrew the Shah was in significant part a consequence of the 1953 coup. The Islamic Republic that emerged was not what most revolutionaries intended — the coalition that overthrew the Shah included liberals, leftists, nationalists, and Islamists, and it was only through a series of political maneuvers in 1979-1981 that Khomeini's faction consolidated power. But the anti-Western, anti-democratic character of the resulting regime was a direct product of the historical experience: democracy had been tried and destroyed by foreign intervention; therefore, the new order must be resistant to foreign intervention above all else.

If the 2026 campaign produces regime change — through direct collapse or through the long-term destabilization of the succession process — what does the 1953→1979→2026 cycle suggest about the next iteration? Each intervention has produced a successor regime more hostile to Western interests than the one it replaced. Mossadegh was a parliamentary democrat who sought to renegotiate oil terms. The Shah was an authoritarian modernizer aligned with the West. The Islamic Republic is a theocratic state hostile to the entire Western-led international order. If the pattern holds, what follows the Islamic Republic will be worse — not better — from the perspective of Western interests.

This is the law of unintended consequences operating at civilizational scale. Each intervention is designed to solve the problem created by the previous intervention, and each creates a new problem that is larger, more complex, and more resistant to external manipulation than the last. The 1953 coup created the conditions for 1979. The 1979 revolution created the conditions for the nuclear program. The nuclear program created the justification for 2026. What will 2026 create the conditions for?

The honest answer is: no one knows, and anyone who claims to know is selling something. But the historical pattern is clear enough to constitute a warning. The United States has intervened in Iran three times — covertly in 1953, through proxy in 1980-1988 (supporting Iraq), and directly in 2026. Each intervention has been tactically successful and strategically catastrophic. There is no reason to believe the fourth iteration will break the pattern.

## VI. Regional Proxy Systems in Historical Context

### Clients, Vassals, and the Patron's Dilemma

Iran's network of regional allies — Hezbollah in Lebanon, the Houthis in Yemen, Kata'ib Hezbollah and other militias in Iraq, various Palestinian factions — is routinely described in Western media as a "proxy network" or "axis of resistance." The framing implies something illegitimate: puppets on strings, subordinate actors executing Tehran's will. The reality is more complex, and the historical precedents more illuminating.

Every great power in the Middle East has maintained client relationships. The Ottoman Empire governed through a system of vassal states, tributary principalities, and semi-autonomous provinces that preserved local power structures while extracting loyalty and revenue. The British Empire operated through client monarchies — the Hashemites in Iraq and Transjordan, the Gulf sheikhdoms bound by exclusive treaties, the Egyptian khedivate that was nominally Ottoman but functionally British. The United States inherited and extended this system: the Shah's Iran, Saudi Arabia, Jordan, the Gulf states, Israel — all have functioned, to varying degrees, as American client states whose security depends on Washington's commitment.

Iran's proxy network is thus not an aberration but a regional norm pursued by a state with limited conventional military options. Hezbollah was created in 1982 in response to Israel's invasion of Lebanon, with Iranian funding and IRGC training. Over four decades, it has evolved from a militia into a state-within-a-state, providing social services, running hospitals, and fielding a military force more capable than most national armies in the region. The Houthi movement in Yemen, while aligned with Iran, has its own domestic roots in Zaydi Shia marginalization and the failures of the post-2011 political transition. Iraqi Shia militias emerged from the post-2003 power vacuum and the existential threat of ISIS. None of these movements is simply a puppet; all are products of local conditions that Iran has cultivated but did not create.

### When the Patron Falls

The historical record of what happens to client networks when their patron state is weakened is sobering. When the Ottoman Empire collapsed, its vassal and tributary relationships dissolved overnight. Former Ottoman provinces were absorbed by European colonial powers or left to fend for themselves. Local power brokers who had operated within the Ottoman framework became warlords, separatist leaders, or clients of the new colonial masters. The transition from one patronage system to another was not peaceful — it produced the Arab Revolt, the Turkish War of Independence, the Iraqi and Syrian revolts against mandatory rule, and decades of political instability.

If Iran's capacity to sustain its proxy network is destroyed — a plausible outcome given the 90% reduction in missile launch rates, the destruction of the navy, and the potential collapse of the regime — the consequences for the region will be severe but not in the way that proxy-network critics imagine. Hezbollah will not simply disarm and integrate into Lebanese politics. The Houthis will not lay down their weapons. Iraqi militias will not dissolve. These movements have their own domestic constituencies, their own revenue streams (however diminished), and their own ideological commitments. What they will lose is strategic coordination, advanced weapons supply, and the deterrent value of Iran's retaliatory capacity.

The most likely outcome is not the disappearance of these movements but their fragmentation and radicalization. A Hezbollah deprived of Iranian strategic guidance but retaining its arsenal and social base becomes more, not less, dangerous — a wounded animal rather than a coordinated strategic actor. The post-patron trajectory is one of splintering: factions competing for diminished resources, freed from the patron's moderating influence (such as it was), and driven toward more extreme action to maintain relevance. This is what happened to Palestinian factions after the PLO's expulsion from Lebanon in 1982 — the result was not peace but the rise of Hamas and Islamic Jihad, movements more radical than the ones they partially replaced.

## VII. What the Region Remembers That Washington Forgets

### The Asymmetry of Memory

The most consequential feature of the US-Iranian relationship is not the asymmetry of military power — that is obvious and overwhelming. It is the asymmetry of historical memory. Americans and Iranians remember entirely different histories, and this divergence shapes the conflict in ways that no military strategy can address.

For most Americans, the story of US-Iranian relations begins on November 4, 1979, when Iranian students stormed the US Embassy in Tehran and took fifty-two Americans hostage for 444 days. The hostage crisis was a genuine trauma: it dominated American media for over a year, ended Jimmy Carter's presidency, and established Iran as an enemy in American popular consciousness. Everything that followed — the "Death to America" chants, the Marine barracks bombing in Beirut (1983), the Khobar Towers bombing (1996), the IED campaign against American forces in Iraq — reinforced an image of Iran as an irrational, fanatical adversary motivated by nothing more than hatred of America.

For Iranians, the story begins earlier and contains chapters that most Americans have never heard of. It begins with the 1907 Anglo-Russian Agreement that divided Iran into spheres of influence without consulting any Iranian. It continues through the 1941 Allied invasion that deposed Reza Shah and occupied the country as a supply corridor to the Soviet Union. It reaches its defining moment in 1953, when the CIA destroyed Iranian democracy to preserve British oil profits. It encompasses twenty-six years of the Shah's US-backed dictatorship, during which SAVAK — trained by the CIA — tortured and killed thousands. It includes eight years of war during which the United States supplied intelligence and diplomatic cover to Saddam Hussein's Iraq, even as Iraq used chemical weapons against Iranian soldiers. It includes the USS Vincennes' shooting down of Iran Air Flight 655 in July 1988, killing all 290 passengers and crew — an act for which the United States has never formally apologized, and for which the commanding officer received a Legion of Merit.

These are not competing narratives about the same events. They are narratives about different events entirely. The American narrative begins in 1979; the Iranian narrative begins in 1907 or 1953. The American narrative centers American suffering (hostages, Marines, soldiers); the Iranian narrative centers Iranian suffering (coups, dictatorship, chemical warfare, civilian aircraft). Neither narrative is false. Both are radically incomplete. And the incompleteness is not symmetric: the American narrative omits American actions that caused Iranian suffering, while the Iranian narrative, whatever its distortions, at least acknowledges the existence of the other side's grievances.

### The Consequences of Forgetting

This asymmetry has direct operational consequences. When American policymakers frame Iran's nuclear program as an unprovoked act of aggression, they do so in genuine ignorance of the historical context that makes the program comprehensible to Iranians. When the Trump administration demands the right to choose Iran's next leader, it does so without apparent awareness that the United States chose Iran's last leader-but-one — the Shah — and that the consequences of that choice are why there is an Islamic Republic to bomb in the first place.

The asymmetry also shapes the aftermath. Americans will remember the 2026 war through their own casualties: six killed in Kuwait, the anxiety of Hormuz closure, the economic disruption. Iranians will remember 175 dead children at Minab, 1,200+ killed across twenty-four provinces, 130 hours of internet blackout, a supreme leader assassinated, and an American president who declared he would choose their next ruler. These memories will not fade when the missiles stop. They will harden into the foundation of the next generation's political consciousness, just as the memories of 1953 hardened into the ideology of 1979, and the memories of the Iran-Iraq War hardened into the nuclear program's political justification.

The historian's task is not to adjudicate between these memories but to insist that both be acknowledged. A conflict understood through only one side's historical lens is not understood at all. And a policy that ignores the other side's historical experience — that proceeds as if history began on the date most convenient for the policymaker — is not a policy but a prayer, addressed to a universe that has never answered such prayers favorably.

## Conclusion: The Recurring Tragedy

The Middle East's recurring tragedy is not that history repeats — it is that the powerful never believe history applies to them. Every Western intervention in the region has been conducted in the conviction that this time is different: that the lessons of previous interventions are irrelevant because the current operation is more precise, more justified, more technologically sophisticated, more morally grounded. The British believed this in 1920 when they bombed Iraqi villages from the air to suppress rebellion. The Americans believed this in 2003 when they invaded Iraq with shock and awe. And in 2026, the architects of Operation Epic Fury believe it again — that 6,000 precision-guided munitions can accomplish what a century of intervention has failed to achieve: a Middle East arranged to Western specifications.

The region knows better. The region has watched empires come and go — Achaemenid, Macedonian, Parthian, Sassanid, Arab, Mongol, Timurid, Safavid, Ottoman, British, and now American — and it has learned the lesson that empires never learn about themselves: that military supremacy is temporary, that imposed political orders are fragile, and that the memories of the occupied outlast the attention spans of the occupiers.

Iran will survive this war. It has survived the Arab conquest of the seventh century, the Mongol devastation of the thirteenth, the Afghan invasion of the eighteenth, and the Iran-Iraq War of the twentieth. Whether the Islamic Republic survives is a different question — and one that Iranians, not American presidents, will ultimately answer. But whatever political form Iran takes in the aftermath of 2026, it will be shaped by the memory of what is happening now: the children of Minab, the assassination of a head of state, the demand to choose the successor. These memories will join the memories of Mossadegh, of SAVAK, of Saddam's chemical weapons, of Flight 655 — and they will produce consequences that the architects of Epic Fury have not imagined, because they have not studied the history that would enable them to imagine.

The weight of history is not a metaphor. It is the accumulated mass of lived experience — of suffering, resistance, betrayal, and survival — that shapes how peoples understand the present and choose the future. Iran carries that weight. The Middle East carries that weight. The question is not whether the 2026 war will add to it — it already has. The question is whether anyone in a position of power will learn from it, or whether the next generation of policymakers will once again proceed in the serene conviction that history does not apply to them, repeating the tragedy that their predecessors' amnesia guaranteed.

As the great Iranian poet Hafez wrote six centuries ago: *"The green sea of heaven and the bark of the new moon / Are drowned in the illusion of my love."* The illusion of control over the Middle East has drowned more than moons. It has drowned democracies, peoples, and the possibility of peace — again and again, in waves that each generation believes it sees for the first time.
