# The Democratic Recession: Weimar Echoes, Oligarchic Drift, and the War That Didn't Rally

**An Essay for the Historical Context Roundtable on the US-Iran War of 2026**

*March 6, 2026*

---

## I. The Global Right-Wing Turn in Historical Context

There is a temptation, when democracies begin to fail, to treat each failure as sui generis — a product of local grievances, particular demagogues, unique circumstances. This is how the interwar generation understood what was happening to them, too. Mussolini was an Italian problem. Pilsudski was a Polish one. The NSDAP was a German pathology rooted in Versailles humiliation. It was only afterward — after the camps, after the ruins — that historians recognized the pattern: a global wave of democratic collapse driven by structural forces that transcended any single nation.

We are living through the second such wave, and we have less excuse for failing to recognize it.

The data are unambiguous. The V-Dem Institute's 2025 Democracy Report — titled, with a precision that now reads as prophecy, *25 Years of Autocratization: Democracy Trumped?* — documented 45 countries undergoing autocratization in 2024, up from twelve just twenty years prior. For the first time since the early 2000s, autocracies outnumber democracies worldwide. The "third wave of democratization" that Samuel Huntington celebrated in 1991 has not merely stalled; it has reversed. The institute identified the United States as undergoing "the fastest evolving episode of autocratization the USA has been through in modern history" — a judgment rendered *before* the bombs fell on Tehran.

The roster of the rightward turn reads like a geopolitical roll call. Viktor Orbán in Hungary since 2010, systematically dismantling judicial independence, press freedom, and electoral fairness while remaining inside the European Union — proving that democratic backsliding could proceed within liberal institutional frameworks rather than against them. Narendra Modi in India, presiding over the democratic degradation of the world's largest democracy through Hindu nationalist mobilization, institutional capture, and the systematic marginalization of Muslim citizens. The Brexit referendum of 2016, which married nativist sentiment to economic grievance in the world's oldest parliamentary democracy. Jair Bolsonaro in Brazil, who attempted what Trump later achieved more gradually: the conversion of populist energy into institutional subversion. Giorgia Meloni in Italy, the first post-fascist prime minister of a G7 nation, whose Brothers of Italy descends directly from the Movimento Sociale Italiano, itself the successor to Mussolini's Republican Fascist Party. Javier Milei in Argentina, the libertarian-authoritarian hybrid whose chainsaw politics represented the economic desperation of a continent. And Trump — first in 2016, then again in 2024, each iteration more radical, more openly contemptuous of constitutional restraint, more willing to test the tensile strength of institutions that had been assumed to be load-bearing.

The 1920s-1930s parallel is imperfect but instructive. Then as now, the democratic recession followed a period of rapid globalization and its discontents. Then as now, a catastrophic economic shock (1929; 2008) delegitimized ruling elites and created mass constituencies for radical alternatives. Then as now, new communications technologies (radio; social media) enabled demagogues to bypass institutional gatekeepers and speak directly to mass audiences. Then as now, liberal internationalist institutions (the League of Nations; the post-1945 rules-based order) proved unable to constrain the states they were designed to govern. And then as now, the democracies that survived did so not because their institutions were inherently resilient, but because specific political actors made specific choices to defend them — choices that were never inevitable and that, in many countries, were never made.

The crucial difference is speed. The interwar collapse took roughly fifteen years, from the March on Rome in 1922 to the Anschluss in 1938. The current wave has compressed that timeline. Hungary went from liberal democracy to what Orbán himself calls "illiberal democracy" in a single decade. The United States moved from the norms-testing of Trump's first term to the constitutional crisis of the Iran war in under two years of the second.

And here is where the war fits the pattern with terrible precision. Democracies in crisis have historically launched foreign adventures for domestic reasons. Wilhelmine Germany's naval buildup and colonial ambitions were inseparable from Bismarck's and then Wilhelm's management of domestic social tensions. Argentina's junta invaded the Falklands in 1982 as the economy collapsed and domestic opposition mounted. Russia's annexation of Crimea in 2014 followed the Maidan revolution and served to reconsolidate domestic support. The mechanism is not mysterious: external conflict creates rally effects, justifies emergency powers, delegitimizes domestic opposition as disloyal, and redirects public attention from governance failures to patriotic fervor.

Operation Epic Fury was launched by an administration facing historically low approval ratings, an "affordability crisis" its own supporters identified as their primary concern, and a Department of Homeland Security in its third week of shutdown. The war's timing — following the June 2025 Israel-Iran war that had already tested and validated the operational concept — suggests not spontaneous response to imminent threat but calculated escalation toward a predetermined objective. Trump's demand, six days into the campaign, to be "involved in the appointment" of Iran's next leader is not the rhetoric of defensive counterproliferation. It is the rhetoric of imperial prerogative.

But here is what makes 2026 genuinely novel, and genuinely terrifying: the rally didn't come. Every prior foreign adventure by a democracy in crisis produced at least a temporary surge of patriotic support. Even Iraq 2003, launched on fabricated intelligence, commanded 76% initial approval. Operation Epic Fury sits at 25-27% — the lowest initial support for any major American military operation in recorded polling history. The democracy-in-crisis launched its foreign adventure, and the public refused to play its assigned role in the script.

This is not a minor data point. It represents the failure of a mechanism that has operated reliably for at least two centuries. And its failure raises a question that should keep every democratic theorist awake: What does a government do when it can no longer rally its people? History suggests two answers: it either retreats, or it stops needing their consent.

## II. Weimar Lessons: The Mechanisms of Democratic Erosion

The Weimar Republic did not die on January 30, 1933, when Hindenburg appointed Hitler chancellor. It died incrementally, through a series of constitutional improvisations that each seemed defensible in isolation and proved catastrophic in accumulation. The instrument of death was Article 48 of the Weimar Constitution — the emergency powers provision that allowed the president to "take measures necessary for the restoration of law and order" when "public safety and order are seriously disturbed or endangered." Between 1930 and 1933, Heinrich Brüning, Franz von Papen, and Kurt von Schleicher governed almost entirely through emergency decrees, bypassing the Reichstag. By the time Hitler took office, parliamentary governance had already been suspended for three years. The Nazis did not destroy the Weimar Republic; they moved into a building whose foundations had already been removed.

The parallel to the American war powers crisis is not exact — historical parallels never are — but the structural homology is striking. The US Constitution vests the power to declare war in Congress. Article I, Section 8 is unambiguous. Yet since 1945, presidents have initiated military operations of increasing scale and duration without congressional declarations. The Korean War, the Vietnam War (authorized by the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution, passed under fraudulent pretenses), the invasions of Grenada and Panama, the bombing of Serbia, the intervention in Libya — each established a precedent that the next president could cite. The War Powers Resolution of 1973, passed over Nixon's veto in the aftermath of Vietnam, was designed to reassert congressional authority. It has never once forced the withdrawal of American forces from a conflict.

On March 4-5, 2026, both chambers of Congress voted on War Powers challenges to Operation Epic Fury. The Senate rejected the resolution 47-53. The House defeated it 212-219 — a margin of seven votes. Only two Republicans, Thomas Massie and Warren Davidson, voted to constrain the president's authority. Every other Republican member chose partisan loyalty over constitutional prerogative. The mechanism that Article I was designed to provide — legislative deliberation over the most consequential decision a republic can make — was tested and found wanting.

The Weimar analogy illuminates the specific pathology at work. Article 48 was not a bug in the Weimar Constitution; it was a feature, designed to enable emergency governance in a new and fragile democracy. Its framers — Hugo Preuß chief among them — intended it as a temporary measure, bounded by presidential judgment and parliamentary review. What they did not anticipate was that the emergency would become permanent, that the exception would become the rule, and that the Reichstag, rather than fighting for its authority, would acquiesce in its own marginalization.

This is precisely what has happened to American war powers. The "emergency" of presidential military authority, originally justified by the nuclear age's demand for rapid response, has been normalized into a standing prerogative. Each president has pushed the boundary further. Obama intervened in Libya without even the fig leaf of a congressional authorization. Trump ordered the assassination of Qassem Soleimani in January 2020 without congressional notification. Now Trump has launched the largest American military operation since Iraq — a regime-change war against a sovereign state — and Congress has ratified it after the fact through the negative act of failing to stop it.

The German constitutional theorist Carl Schmitt argued that "sovereign is he who decides on the exception." Schmitt meant this descriptively, but it has functioned prescriptively: the power to declare emergencies is the power to rule. When Congress failed to invoke the War Powers Resolution — when it had the opportunity to reclaim its constitutional authority and chose not to — it conceded Schmitt's point. The president is sovereign because the president decides what constitutes an emergency, and no other institution will say otherwise.

There is a deeper mechanism at work, one that the Weimar scholars identified but that has received insufficient attention in the American context: **legislative abdication as a rational strategy for individual legislators.** Weimar parliamentarians who supported Brüning's emergency decrees did so not because they believed in executive supremacy but because they feared the political consequences of taking responsibility for governance during a crisis. If the Reichstag voted on austerity measures, individual members would be held accountable. If the president decreed them, members could distance themselves from the outcomes.

The identical logic operates in the American war powers context. A vote to authorize war creates accountability. A vote to stop a war already in progress creates political risk — the accusation of "not supporting the troops," of being "soft on Iran," of undermining national security. The path of least resistance for any individual legislator is to let the president act and avoid the vote entirely. The War Powers Resolution's 60-day clock, which expires in late April, creates a future confrontation. But history suggests that when the clock runs, Congress will find a reason not to act — just as the Reichstag found reasons to extend Article 48 authority again and again until there was no authority left to extend.

The erosion of norms accelerates the process. Trump's rhetoric — "I have to be involved in the appointment" of Iran's next leader, "immunity or absolutely guaranteed death" — is not merely bellicose. It is the language of a sovereign who recognizes no constraint. The comparison to Venezuela, the demand for personal involvement in another country's governance — these are not the statements of a president operating within constitutional limits. They are the statements of what the framers feared most: an executive who has absorbed the war power entirely and treats it as personal prerogative.

Hannah Arendt observed that totalitarianism does not begin with concentration camps. It begins with the destruction of the space between citizens in which political action becomes possible. The American constitutional system created that space through the separation of powers — the requirement that war, the gravest act of state, must be a shared decision between the executive and the legislature. When that space collapses, when war becomes a unilateral executive act that the legislature can only ratify or impotently protest, the constitutional structure retains its form while losing its substance. The building still stands. The foundations are gone.

## III. The Oligarchic Turn

In January 1961, Dwight Eisenhower — a five-star general who had commanded the largest amphibious invasion in human history — used his farewell address to warn against "the acquisition of unwarranted influence, whether sought or unsought, by the military-industrial complex." The warning was prophetic, but it was also incomplete. Eisenhower could not have anticipated the fusion of military-industrial power with the concentrated wealth of the technology sector, creating an oligarchic formation without precedent in American history.

The numbers tell part of the story. In 2025, the three wealthiest Americans — Elon Musk, Jeff Bezos, and Mark Zuckerberg — controlled more wealth than the bottom 50% of the American population combined. The top 0.1% of households held more wealth than the bottom 80%. The Gini coefficient of the United States had reached levels not seen since the original Gilded Age of the 1890s. But wealth concentration alone does not produce oligarchy. What produces oligarchy is the conversion of economic power into political power — the moment when wealth stops buying influence and starts buying governance.

That moment arrived. Musk's installation at the head of a government efficiency operation with authority to reshape federal agencies was not lobbying. It was not even the revolving door between industry and government. It was the direct exercise of governing power by a private citizen whose wealth exceeds the GDP of most nations. The tech billionaire class's attendance at Trump's second inauguration — sitting in positions of honor that had traditionally been reserved for elected officials and dignitaries — was a visual text that required no interpretation.

The relationship between oligarchy and war-making is ancient and well-documented. Thucydides recorded how Athenian commercial interests drove the Sicilian Expedition — the catastrophic military adventure that destroyed Athenian democracy. The Venetian Republic's wars were inseparable from the commercial interests of its merchant oligarchy. The British East India Company maintained its own army and conducted its own foreign policy. The American entry into World War I was shaped, as the Nye Committee documented in 1934-1936, by the financial interests of banks that had extended enormous loans to the Allied powers.

In the current conflict, the military-industrial-technology complex operates through several channels. Defense contractors — Lockheed Martin, Raytheon (now RTX), Northrop Grumman — see their stock prices surge with every missile fired. The cost-exchange equation of the Iran war is, from their perspective, not a problem but a business model: each $50,000 Iranian drone that forces the expenditure of a $5-15 million interceptor represents not waste but demand. The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute over AI weapons use — in which the president ordered federal agencies to phase out the technology of a company that drew ethical lines on military applications — demonstrates how the war economy punishes ethical restraint and rewards complicity. The destruction of AWS data centers in the UAE, far from discouraging cloud investment in conflict zones, will drive demand for hardened, militarized cloud infrastructure — a new market measured in tens of billions.

The historical parallel that illuminates most sharply is not the Gilded Age but the late Roman Republic. In the decades before Caesar, the Roman political system was formally democratic — assemblies voted, tribunes exercised veto power, magistrates were elected. But the reality was oligarchic: a small number of wealthy families controlled the Senate, military commands went to political allies, and the spoils of conquest were distributed through patronage networks. The formal mechanisms of republican government continued to function, but they had been hollowed out by wealth concentration that made genuine democratic deliberation impossible. The Republic did not fall to a foreign enemy. It fell because the oligarchy's internal competition escalated from political maneuvering to civil war, and the institutions that should have mediated that competition had been too thoroughly captured to do so.

Yale's research on democratic backsliding, presented at ISPS in 2025, demonstrated empirically what historians have long observed: income inequality increases political polarization, and polarization increases the likelihood of democratic breakdown. The mechanism is straightforward. When economic gains concentrate at the top, the wealthy develop interests that diverge from those of the majority. To protect those interests in a formally democratic system, they must either persuade the majority (increasingly difficult as inequality becomes visible) or capture the institutions that translate majority preferences into policy. Institutional capture — through campaign finance, regulatory revolving doors, media ownership, and direct participation in governance — is the rational strategy of an oligarchy operating within democratic forms.

The Iran war is, among other things, a product of this institutional capture. The 25% of Americans who support it are not the 0.1% who profit from it. The 75% who oppose it lack the institutional channels to translate their opposition into policy restraint. Congress — the institution designed to represent majority preferences on questions of war and peace — voted along lines determined not by constituent opinion but by partisan loyalty, which is itself shaped by donor networks, media ecosystems, and the career incentives of professional politicians. The oligarchic turn does not require a conspiracy. It requires only that the institutions designed to check concentrated power be gradually, incrementally, rationally captured by that power.

## IV. When Democracies Go to War Without Consent

The 25-27% approval figure for Operation Epic Fury is not merely low. It is historically anomalous in a way that demands explanation.

Every major American military operation since scientific polling began has generated a "rally-around-the-flag" effect. Pearl Harbor produced near-universal support. Korea began at 66% approval before declining. The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution passed 88-2 in the Senate and 416-0 in the House; initial Vietnam support exceeded 60%. The first Gulf War commanded 80%+ approval. Even Iraq 2003 — launched on intelligence that many suspected was fabricated, against an enemy that had not attacked the United States, in defiance of the UN Security Council — achieved 76% initial support.

The rally effect has been one of the most robust findings in political science. John Mueller's seminal 1970 work established the pattern; subsequent research by Matthew Baum, Tim Groeling, and others refined it. The mechanism operates through several channels: patriotic identification, threat perception, media deference to executive framing, the desire for national unity in crisis, and the information asymmetry that favors the government in the early stages of conflict. Rally effects are temporary — Vietnam support eroded to 28% by 1971; Iraq fell below 40% by 2005 — but they are, or were, reliable.

The absence of any rally effect for the Iran war breaks the pattern. Understanding why requires understanding what has changed in the relationship between democratic publics and their governments.

The first change is the post-Iraq trust deficit. The Iraq war's fraudulent WMD rationale did not merely discredit a specific policy; it damaged the epistemic authority of the executive branch on questions of war and peace. When the Trump administration cites Iran's nuclear program as justification for preemptive strikes, the public hears an echo of Colin Powell at the UN. Trust, once shattered, does not regenerate within a generation. The boy who cried wolf does not get believed the third time, even if there is a wolf.

The second change is partisan sorting. In the mid-twentieth century, foreign policy retained bipartisan character; wars were national undertakings that transcended party. That era is over. In a political environment where partisan identity determines positions on virtually every issue, a war launched by a Republican president with no Democratic support is, by definition, a partisan project. The pool of persuadable citizens that could generate a 30-point rally effect has shrunk to near zero.

The third change is the information environment, which I address separately below.

The fourth change is the most fundamental: the "peace president" problem. Trump campaigned explicitly as an anti-interventionist. His base — the populist coalition of economically anxious voters who rejected the bipartisan foreign policy consensus — supported him precisely because he promised to end America's "forever wars." Operation Epic Fury is the ultimate betrayal of that promise. When 63% of voters cite gas prices as their primary concern about the war, they are expressing not just economic anxiety but the specific grievance of voters who chose a leader to prioritize domestic welfare and found themselves funding another Middle Eastern war.

The historical precedents for democracies fighting wars their publics oppose from the outset are sparse and instructive. The closest is the Boer War (1899-1902), which divided British public opinion from the beginning and produced the term "pro-Boer" as an epithet for dissenters. The Liberal opposition, much of the press, and significant segments of the public opposed the war on both moral and practical grounds. The result was not immediate policy change — the war continued for three years — but a fundamental realignment of British politics. The Liberal landslide of 1906 was, in substantial part, a delayed referendum on the Boer War and the Conservative government that had prosecuted it.

The more troubling precedent is France during the Algerian War (1954-1962). The war was never popular with metropolitan French opinion; it was sustained by a combination of military momentum, colonial-lobby pressure, settler political power, and the institutional inertia of a state apparatus committed to *l'Algérie française*. The disconnect between public opinion and state action produced the crisis of the Fourth Republic, a military coup attempt, de Gaulle's return to power, and ultimately the creation of the Fifth Republic — a more centralized, executive-heavy constitutional order designed precisely to insulate presidential authority from legislative and popular constraint. The Algerian War ended, but French democracy was permanently restructured in a more authoritarian direction.

This is the pattern that should concern us most. When a democratic state fights a war its public opposes, the resolution is rarely that the public's preferences prevail in real time. More commonly, the disconnect between popular will and state action is resolved either by the eventual exhaustion of the war effort (Vietnam, where it took seven years of declining support to produce withdrawal) or by the restructuring of democratic institutions to reduce popular influence over security policy (France's Fifth Republic). The first outcome requires time and sustained political mobilization. The second is the more permanent danger — and it is the direction in which the American constitutional system is already moving.

## V. The Information Environment and Democratic Deliberation

The relationship between communications technology and democratic governance is the great undertheorized variable in the study of war and democracy. Jürgen Habermas argued that the bourgeois public sphere — the space of rational deliberation among citizens — emerged from the technology of print: newspapers, pamphlets, coffeehouses where printed matter was discussed. This public sphere was the essential precondition for democratic governance, because democracy requires not merely voting but deliberation — the formation of considered public judgment through the exchange of information and argument.

Every subsequent communications revolution has reshaped this public sphere, with consequences for democratic war-making. Radio enabled Roosevelt's fireside chats, which built support for intervention in World War II, but it also enabled Father Coughlin's proto-fascist broadcasts and Goebbels's propaganda machine. Television brought Vietnam into American living rooms — the "living room war" that Walter Cronkite's 1968 broadcast exemplified — and made it impossible to sustain the gap between official optimism and battlefield reality. Cable news created the 24-hour information cycle that shaped public perception of the Gulf War and Iraq.

The social media revolution of the 2010s did something qualitatively different: it destroyed the possibility of a shared information environment. Previous communications technologies, however they distorted reality, at least created a common frame of reference — Americans watching Cronkite or reading the *New York Times* disagreed about the meaning of events but agreed on what events had occurred. Social media shattered that shared reality into algorithmically curated information silos, each with its own facts, its own authorities, its own narrative of events.

For democratic war-making, the consequences are profound and contradictory. On one hand, the collapse of shared reality makes the *manufacture* of consent — the process Edward Herman and Noam Chomsky described in their analysis of media and propaganda — nearly impossible. In 2003, the Bush administration could construct a narrative of Iraqi WMD through a compliant media ecosystem: embedded journalists, patriotic cable news coverage, the institutional authority of the Secretary of State at the UN. That infrastructure of narrative control no longer exists. In 2026, BBC Verify contradicts CENTCOM's ship-sinking claims within hours. Amnesty International verifies civilian casualty footage in real time. OSINT analysts on social media track military movements with commercial satellite imagery. The administration cannot manufacture consent because the information architecture that enabled consent-manufacturing has been destroyed.

On the other hand, the same fragmentation that prevents manufactured consent also prevents informed democratic deliberation. Citizens do not share a common set of facts from which to reason. Supporters of the war inhabit an information ecosystem in which Iran posed an imminent nuclear threat and the strikes are a necessary defensive measure. Opponents inhabit one in which the war is an illegal act of aggression driven by domestic political calculation. Neither group encounters the other's evidence. The public sphere that Habermas described — the space of rational-critical debate among citizens — has been replaced by parallel universes of motivated reasoning.

The specific innovation of the 2026 information environment is real-time OSINT documentation of civilian casualties. The Minab school strike — 175 children killed on Day 1 — was documented by six verified videos (Amnesty International), satellite imagery (BBC Verify), and WHO confirmation of 13 hospital strikes, all within 48 hours. This information reached global audiences unfiltered by military public affairs offices, government spokespeople, or editorial gatekeeping.

The political significance is difficult to overstate. In every prior American war, the government controlled the initial narrative. Civilian casualties were acknowledged gradually, retrospectively, often years after the fact. The Iraq Body Count project documented Iraqi civilian deaths over years; the Lancet studies that estimated 600,000+ excess deaths were published in 2006, three years after the invasion. The time lag between the act and its documentation created political space for the war's advocates.

That space no longer exists. When 175 dead children are documented within hours, the political calculus changes fundamentally. The moral weight of civilian casualties lands on public opinion *during the rally window* rather than after it. This is one reason the rally never came: the public was confronted with the war's human costs before the patriotic narrative could take hold. Democratic legitimacy depends on the capacity of the public to understand and accept the consequences of state action. When those consequences are broadcast in real time to a public that never consented, the gap between democratic form and democratic substance becomes a chasm.

## VI. European Fragmentation and Alliance Dissolution

The Concert of Europe — the system of great-power cooperation established at the Congress of Vienna in 1815 — maintained relative peace in Europe for a century. Its collapse was gradual, driven by the unification of Germany, the rigidification of alliance systems, the arms race, and the inability of existing diplomatic mechanisms to manage crises among increasingly antagonistic blocs. When the system finally failed in July 1914, a regional dispute between Austria-Hungary and Serbia escalated into a world war because alliance commitments transformed local conflicts into systemic ones.

NATO, the post-1945 alliance system that replaced the Concert of Europe's informal arrangements with a formal treaty structure, is now experiencing its own crisis of coherence — and the Iran war is both symptom and accelerant.

The fractures are visible in the coalition's structure. The United Kingdom has crossed from defensive positioning to active co-belligerency by confirming Akrotiri as a basing platform for American bomber operations — a decision that makes British territory a legitimate military target under international humanitarian law without a parliamentary vote authorizing such exposure. France has offered base access with "guarantees" that facilities will not be used for offensive operations — a legal fiction that satisfies neither international law nor domestic politics, but that allows Macron to maintain the appearance of allied solidarity without the substance of co-belligerency. Spain has refused to allow US bases on its territory for the campaign while simultaneously deploying a frigate to Cyprus — a gesture of European solidarity that carefully avoids material contribution to the war effort. The Netherlands, Italy, and Portugal have made contributions calibrated to demonstrate alliance reliability without incurring significant risk.

The most revealing fracture involves Turkey. When an Iranian missile aimed at "Greek Cyprus" deviated and struck Hatay province, Turkey had grounds to invoke Article 5 — the collective defense provision that is NATO's raison d'être. NATO Secretary General Rutte discussed the possible invocation. Pentagon spokesman Hegseth immediately denied its applicability. The message was unmistakable: Article 5 applies when American interests are served by its application, and not otherwise. For Turkey — which has spent decades balancing NATO membership against regional relationships — the lesson is that the alliance's security guarantee is conditional, political, and ultimately unreliable.

This selective application of alliance commitments mirrors the pre-1914 dynamic in which alliance obligations were simultaneously binding and ambiguous. Austria-Hungary expected German support under the Dual Alliance; Germany gave its "blank check." Russia expected French support under the Franco-Russian Alliance; France honored its commitment. Britain's obligations under the Entente Cordiale were less formal, and its entry into the war came later and for different reasons. The system's failure was not that alliances existed but that they were simultaneously rigid enough to transmit escalation and flexible enough to permit miscalculation.

NATO in 2026 exhibits the same pathology. The alliance is rigid enough that member states feel compelled to demonstrate solidarity — hence the British basing, the French base access, the pan-European naval deployments — but flexible enough that each member calibrates its contribution to minimize risk and maximize deniability. The result is not a coherent coalition but a collection of bilateral arrangements, each driven by domestic political calculation rather than strategic logic. Spain's refusal of bases alongside its deployment of a frigate is the perfect expression of this incoherence: solidarity in principle, distance in practice.

The European joint statement calling for "dialogue and diplomacy" while member states provide basing for bomber operations captures a deeper crisis. Europe cannot decide whether it is an American ally participating in a military campaign or an independent actor seeking diplomatic resolution. It is attempting to be both simultaneously, and the contradiction is unsustainable. As the war extends — and every indication suggests it will extend for months — European governments will face increasing pressure to choose. The history of alliance fragmentation during active conflict suggests that the longer the war lasts, the wider the fractures become.

## VII. Where This Leads: Democratic Recovery or Democratic Failure

The most consequential question is not how this war ends but what it does to the democratic institutions that authorized — or failed to prevent — its beginning.

History offers two models of post-crisis democratic recovery. The first is the post-Vietnam/post-Watergate reform era. The combined trauma of an illegal war, a criminal presidency, and domestic surveillance abuses produced a remarkable burst of institutional reform: the War Powers Resolution (1973), the Congressional Budget Act (1974), the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (1978), the Ethics in Government Act (1978), the creation of the Senate and House Intelligence Committees. These reforms were imperfect — the War Powers Resolution, as we have seen, has never successfully constrained a president — but they represented a genuine attempt to reassert democratic control over executive power.

The second model is institutional decay. The Roman Republic's crisis of the late second and first centuries BCE produced not reform but the progressive normalization of extra-constitutional action. Sulla's march on Rome in 88 BCE was shocking; Caesar's crossing of the Rubicon in 49 BCE was merely the logical conclusion of precedents Sulla had established. Each violation of republican norms made the next violation more conceivable. The Republic's formal institutions continued to exist under Augustus, but they had been emptied of meaning — the Senate met, consuls were elected, but power resided in the princeps.

Which model applies to the United States of 2026?

The conditions for a post-Vietnam-style reform are present: a deeply unpopular war, visible institutional failures, public outrage, and an approaching electoral test (the 2026 midterms). If the war produces the kind of political backlash that Vietnam and Iraq eventually generated — and the absence of any rally effect suggests the backlash may come faster — the midterms could produce a Congress willing to reassert its constitutional authority. The 60-day War Powers clock, expiring in late April, creates a specific institutional moment for confrontation.

But the conditions for institutional decay are also present — and they may be stronger. The failed War Powers votes have established a precedent that will be cited by every future president: Congress had the opportunity to constrain the executive and chose not to. The oligarchic capture of political institutions makes reform dependent on the willingness of officeholders whose careers depend on the donors who profit from the status quo. The information environment prevents the formation of the kind of broad, bipartisan consensus that powered the post-Watergate reforms. And the speed of events — the compression of what took years during Vietnam into days during the Iran war — may outpace the capacity of democratic institutions to respond.

The French precedent haunts. The Algerian War produced not democratic renewal but the Fifth Republic — a constitutional order designed to concentrate power in the presidency, insulate security policy from parliamentary interference, and ensure that the military and the executive could prosecute wars without the inconvenience of democratic deliberation. De Gaulle's constitution was, in many ways, the institutional expression of the lesson France's elite drew from Algeria: that democracy is too slow, too fractious, too susceptible to public emotion to manage the serious business of war and peace.

If the American constitutional system follows this trajectory — if the post-2026 settlement is not reform but consolidation of executive war powers — the consequences extend far beyond the United States. American democratic decline does not occur in isolation. The V-Dem data demonstrate that autocratization is contagious: when powerful democracies weaken, the effect cascades through the international system, emboldening autocrats and demoralizing democrats worldwide. The United States has served, however imperfectly and hypocritically, as a reference point for democratic governance. When the reference point degrades, the entire system degrades with it.

There is a third possibility, one that the historical record supports but that is rarely discussed in the literature on democratic decline: **that the crisis produces not reform or decay but a prolonged period of institutional ambiguity** — a gray zone in which democratic forms persist, elections are held, courts rule, and legislatures convene, but the substance of democratic governance — popular sovereignty, government by consent, the rule of law — is progressively hollowed out. This is Orbán's Hungary, Erdoğan's Turkey, and increasingly Modi's India: not dictatorship but democracy without democrats, constitutionalism without constitutional restraint, elections without meaningful choice.

The United States may already be in this gray zone. A war launched without consent, sustained without approval, and unchecked by the legislature — while elections proceed on schedule and courts remain nominally independent — is precisely the kind of institutional arrangement that characterizes competitive authoritarianism. The question is not whether the Republic will fall with a dramatic collapse. The question is whether it has already fallen, quietly, through the accumulation of precedents and the abdication of the institutions designed to prevent precisely this outcome.

Arendt wrote in *The Origins of Totalitarianism* that the most dangerous moment for a society is not when it is confronted by evil but when it loses the capacity to recognize evil as evil — when the monstrous becomes normal, when the exception becomes the rule, when citizens lose the ability to be shocked. The Minab school strike killed 175 children. Six days later, the news cycle has moved on. The war continues. Congress has voted to let it continue. The president demands to choose Iran's next leader. And 75% of Americans oppose what is being done in their name, but the machinery of democratic self-governance has no mechanism to translate that opposition into restraint.

This is where the Weimar parallel reaches its sharpest point. The Weimar Republic's citizens did not lack the desire for democracy. They lacked the institutions capable of defending democracy against those who used democratic forms to undermine democratic substance. The question facing the American Republic is whether its institutions retain that capacity — or whether, like Weimar's, they will be discovered to have been already empty when the crisis demanded they bear weight.

The answer to that question will not be determined by this war alone. But it will be shaped, decisively, by how this war ends, what accountability follows, and whether the democratic public — the 75% who never consented — finds a way to make their dissent consequential. History does not determine outcomes. But it illuminates the stakes. And the stakes, in March 2026, could not be higher.
