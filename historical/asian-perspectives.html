For the Academic Roundtable on the US-Iran War of 2026

March 6, 2026

Introduction

On the morning of February 28, 2026, when American and Israeli precision munitions began falling across Iran's twenty-four provinces, the immediate consequences rippled not westward toward Brussels or Washington but eastward — toward Tokyo, Beijing, New Delhi, Seoul, and Singapore. Within seventy-two hours, Japan was calculating its remaining weeks of LNG reserves. South Korea announced it could exhaust its gas stockpiles in nine days. India activated "war mode" contingency plans for energy imports. Chinese state-owned enterprise executives were negotiating directly with Iranian counterparts for safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz — and getting it.

This asymmetry — a war conceived in Washington and Jerusalem whose most acute material consequences fall on Asian capitals — is not incidental. It is the defining structural feature of the conflict as seen from Asia. And it carries echoes so deep in Asian historical memory that they require excavation to be properly understood.

The view from Asia is not monolithic. It never has been. But it is unified by a single recognition: the US-Iran War of 2026 is, at its core, a test of whether the global order will remain organized around Atlantic power — or whether the disruptions it generates will accelerate the redistribution of strategic weight toward the Indo-Pacific. The answer, as in most things Asian, will emerge not from dramatic confrontation but from the accumulation of patient, self-interested choices made in the fog of someone else's war.

I. China as Strategic Beneficiary: The Echoes of 1914

There is a structural parallel to the current moment that has received insufficient attention in Western commentary, though it is discussed openly in Chinese strategic circles: the position of the United States during the European wars of 1914–1918 and 1939–1945. In both conflicts, the world's rising industrial power — geographically removed from the primary theater of destruction — leveraged its distance into economic advantage, entered the conflict late and on its own terms, and emerged from the wreckage as the dominant global power.

China in 2026 occupies an analogous position. The US-Iran War is, from Beijing's perspective, a conflict between its primary strategic competitor and a significant energy partner, fought in a region critical to global commerce but far from Chinese shores. Every day the war continues, the United States expends military resources, diplomatic capital, and domestic political cohesion. Every day the Strait of Hormuz remains selectively closed, the argument for Chinese-led alternative energy infrastructure — pipelines from Central Asia, overland routes through Pakistan and Myanmar, expanded Russian crude imports — gains material urgency.

The selective Hormuz transit arrangement is the war's most revealing strategic artifact. As Reuters reported on March 5, China is in active negotiations with Iran to ensure continued safe passage for crude oil and Qatari LNG vessels. Chinese VLCCs — very large crude carriers — continue to transit the strait while approximately 200 Western-flagged vessels sit anchored. Chinese SOE executives negotiated these exemptions directly with Iranian counterparts. The arrangement serves both parties: Iran preserves its most important commercial relationship while maximizing economic pressure on the Western coalition. China gains a competitive energy cost advantage while demonstrating, to every watching capital in Asia and beyond, that it alone can negotiate with all sides.

The historical parallel is precise. Between 1914 and 1917, the United States supplied both the Allies and (through neutral intermediaries) the Central Powers, profiting enormously while European industrial capacity was destroyed. American GDP grew by approximately 7% during the First World War years. When Washington finally entered in April 1917, it did so from a position of overwhelming economic leverage, effectively dictating the postwar financial architecture through war loans that made the victors American debtors. The Treaty of Versailles was drafted in Paris, but the money that would determine its implementation flowed from New York.

Beijing's 2026 calculus is similar in structure if not in scale. China purchases most of Iran's 1.6–1.7 million barrels per day of exports. It has expanded strategic petroleum reserves over the past decade — precisely to cushion external shocks — and has increased discounted crude imports from Russia since 2022 as a partial hedge against maritime disruption. China is not invulnerable to the Hormuz crisis (it imports roughly 70% of its crude oil, much of it through the strait), but it is substantially more insulated than Japan, South Korea, or India. Its strategic petroleum reserve, estimated at 500–600 million barrels (though China does not officially disclose the figure), provides a buffer that no other Asian economy can match.

Does this accelerate the "Chinese century" thesis? The answer is complicated. In one sense, yes: every week of selective Hormuz access widens the energy cost differential between Chinese industry and its Japanese, Korean, and European competitors. Chinese manufacturing, already dominant, gains further advantage. The renminbi's appeal as a settlement currency for energy transactions — already advanced through bilateral swap agreements with Gulf states — increases when dollar-denominated energy markets are disrupted by American military action.

But in another sense, the war exposes the fragility of China's strategic position. Beijing's insulation depends entirely on Tehran's continued goodwill — a goodwill that could evaporate if Iran's strategic calculus shifts, if the regime collapses, or if a successor government aligns with Washington. China's $400 billion, 25-year strategic cooperation agreement with Iran, signed in 2021, was premised on the Islamic Republic's survival. Its value declines with every CENTCOM sortie. The "Chinese century," if it arrives, will be built not on the elegant strategems of Zhongnanhai but on the messy, contingent outcomes of other people's wars — much as the "American century" was built on the rubble of Europe, 1945.

II. The Asian Energy Crisis: Three Weeks to Pearl Harbor

Japan has approximately three weeks of LNG reserves. South Korea announced on March 5 that it could exhaust its gas stockpiles in nine days. These are not abstractions. They are the ticking clock at the center of Asian strategic calculation, and they evoke the most dangerous precedent in the region's modern history.

In July 1941, the United States, Britain, and the Netherlands imposed a comprehensive oil embargo on Imperial Japan, freezing Japanese assets and cutting off 80% of Japan's petroleum supply. Japan's strategic petroleum reserve — approximately 48 million barrels at the time — gave the Imperial Navy roughly eighteen months of operations at peacetime consumption rates, far less under wartime conditions. The embargo created what Japanese strategists called jiri-hin — a slow strangulation. The Imperial General Headquarters concluded that Japan must either submit to American demands (withdrawal from China and Indochina) or seize the oil-rich Dutch East Indies before its reserves were exhausted. On December 7, 1941, Japan chose the latter. Pearl Harbor was, at its root, an energy security decision.

The parallel to 2026 is imperfect but instructive. No one is embargoing Japan, South Korea, or India today. But the functional effect of the Hormuz selective closure is analogous: critical energy supplies have been interrupted by great-power conflict, and the affected nations had no voice in the decision. Japan relies on the Middle East for approximately 95% of its oil supplies and 11% of its LNG imports, with roughly 70% and 6% respectively transiting the Strait of Hormuz. As Reuters noted on March 4, Japan has formally requested a strategic oil reserve release — a step not taken since the 2011 Libyan civil war and, before that, the 2005 Hurricane Katrina disruption.

South Korea's situation is more acute. Importing 20% of its gas from the Gulf region, Seoul faces a crisis timeline measured in single-digit days. The country's industrial base — semiconductors, shipbuilding, petrochemicals, steel — is energy-intensive and structurally dependent on uninterrupted LNG supply. A prolonged Hormuz disruption would not merely raise costs; it would force production shutdowns at Samsung, SK Hynix, POSCO, and Hyundai — companies that collectively anchor the global technology and manufacturing supply chain.

India, importing roughly 40% of its crude oil through Hormuz and home to 23,000 stranded merchant sailors as of March 5, has entered what officials describe as "war mode." The resonances for India are different — not Pearl Harbor but the 1973 oil shock, which devastated India's fragile post-independence economy and contributed to Indira Gandhi's declaration of emergency in 1975. Energy vulnerability has historically been the knife's edge on which Indian democratic stability has balanced.

In Southeast Asia, the exposure is less discussed but no less real. Singapore, the region's petrochemical hub, received 45% of its LNG from Qatar in 2025. Thailand depended on Qatar for 28%. The Philippines and Vietnam, with growing but still-limited domestic production, face cascading price pressures that will hit their poorest citizens hardest.

The lesson of 1941 is not that Asian nations will launch military offensives in response to energy deprivation. It is subtler and more dangerous: that energy desperation compresses decision-making timelines, narrows the perceived range of acceptable options, and creates conditions under which previously unthinkable choices become thinkable. Japan in 1941 did not want to attack the United States. It felt it had no alternative. The governments in Tokyo, Seoul, and New Delhi in 2026 do not want to break ranks with Washington, accommodate Beijing, or cut separate deals with Tehran. But with reserves measured in days and weeks rather than months, the political space for principled neutrality shrinks with every cargo that fails to arrive.

The Asia Times captured this dynamic precisely: "Hormuz doesn't need to close to cripple Asia's economies." The selective closure — combined with cancelled P&I insurance, suspended Maersk bookings, and 200+ anchored vessels — has achieved a functional energy embargo on America's most important Asian allies without any formal declaration. The irony is exquisite and bitter: Washington launched this war in part to prevent Iran from acquiring a nuclear deterrent that would make it untouchable. In doing so, it has activated an energy weapon that falls most heavily on the nations whose security Washington is pledged to guarantee.

III. India's Impossible Position: The Ghost of Nehru

When Jawaharlal Nehru addressed the Asian Relations Conference in New Delhi in March 1947 — five months before Indian independence — he articulated a vision of Asian solidarity that would become the intellectual foundation of the Non-Aligned Movement. "Asia, after a long period of quiescence, has suddenly become important again in world affairs," Nehru declared. India's role, he insisted, would be to stand apart from the emerging Cold War blocs, to maintain "strategic autonomy" (swatantra), and to offer moral leadership rooted in anticolonial solidarity.

Seventy-nine years later, Nehru's successor Narendra Modi has maintained a pointed personal silence on the US-Iran War — a silence that, as Asia Times observed on March 4, has become a domestic political issue. Rahul Gandhi, leader of the opposition Congress party, has attacked Modi's silence as a betrayal of India's non-alignment tradition. India, which chairs BRICS in 2026, upgraded its ties with Israel to a "special strategic partnership" just weeks before the war began. Its statements and actions have been interpreted, particularly in the Global South, as de facto alignment with the US-Israeli coalition.

India's position is genuinely impossible in ways that illuminate the structural contradictions of twenty-first century non-alignment. Consider the vectors pulling New Delhi in different directions:

Toward Washington: India's defense procurement is increasingly American — the C-17 Globemasters, P-8I Poseidons, MH-60R Seahawks, and the emerging iCET technology partnership. The Quad (US-India-Japan-Australia) is India's primary Indo-Pacific security architecture. Indian-American economic ties — including a 5-million-strong diaspora — represent the fastest-growing axis of India's global integration. Washington has been explicit in demanding that partners choose sides.

Toward Tehran: India imports significant volumes of Iranian crude (reduced under sanctions but never eliminated through intermediaries). The Chabahar port — India's sole access to Afghanistan and Central Asia that bypasses Pakistan — represents a $500 million investment and a decade of diplomatic effort. The US Treasury granted India a sanctions waiver for Chabahar operations valid until April 26, 2026 — a date now rendered moot by the war itself. India's 2026–27 Union Budget, presented on February 1, conspicuously allocated zero funds for Chabahar, a decision analyst Brahma Chellaney characterized as "a tactical freeze rather than a strategic withdrawal." As Frontline reported on March 2, India has begun winding down its engagement at the port.

Toward its own citizens: Five Indians are dead. Two were killed aboard the tanker Skylight near Oman. Thirty-seven Indian-crewed ships are stuck in the Gulf. Twenty-three thousand Indian merchant sailors are stranded. These are not statistics; they are constituents, and their families vote.

The Nehruvian tradition would counsel a clear moral stance — condemnation of preemptive war, defense of sovereignty, a call for UN mediation. But Nehruvian non-alignment was premised on a bipolar world in which India could stand equidistant between two superpowers. The 2026 landscape is different: India is deeply enmeshed in the American security architecture while simultaneously dependent on energy supplies that flow through a strait controlled by the nation America is bombing. Non-alignment is not an option when the combatants control your weapons supply and your fuel supply respectively.

What Modi has practiced since 2014 is not non-alignment but "multi-alignment" — the simultaneous cultivation of partnerships with Washington, Moscow, Tokyo, Abu Dhabi, and, until recently, Tehran. The Iran war tests this strategy to destruction. Multi-alignment works when conflicts are distant and stakes are low. When the war involves your primary security partner bombing your energy supplier while your citizens die on tankers in the crossfire, multi-alignment collapses into paralysis — which is precisely what Modi's silence represents.

The deeper historical irony: India hosted the Bandung Conference's intellectual precursors and co-founded the Non-Aligned Movement precisely to avoid being forced into such choices. Now, as BRICS chair during a war against a BRICS member (Iran joined in January 2024), India cannot convene a collective response because its own position is incoherent. As Middle East Eye reported, BRICS is "missing in action" — and India's closeness to Israel, combined with Iran's retaliatory attacks on the UAE (where 3.5 million Indians live and work), has made consensus impossible.

IV. The Vietnam Parallel from the Asian Side

When Americans think about the Vietnam War, they think about domestic protest, the Pentagon Papers, Walter Cronkite, and the fall of Saigon. When Asians think about the Vietnam War, they think about something quite different: a great power fought a devastating war in Asia, and Asian nations had to navigate the consequences with minimal agency over the outcome.

From Beijing, the Vietnam War was simultaneously an opportunity and a threat. Mao Zedong provided extensive military aid to Hanoi — an estimated $20 billion in today's terms, including 320,000 Chinese engineering and anti-aircraft troops deployed between 1965 and 1968 — while carefully calibrating Chinese involvement to avoid direct confrontation with the United States. The Korean War's lesson (1950–1953), in which Chinese "volunteers" fought American forces directly, was that direct great-power conflict was ruinously expensive. China's Vietnam strategy — maximum support, minimal direct exposure — anticipated Beijing's current approach to the Iran war with striking fidelity. Chinese ships transit Hormuz; Chinese soldiers do not fire on Americans.

From Tokyo, the Vietnam War presented a different dilemma. Japan was America's most important Asian ally, hosting major US military bases that served as critical logistics hubs for the war effort. Okinawa was the staging ground for B-52 bomber operations. Yet the Japanese public overwhelmingly opposed the war, and Prime Minister Satō Eisaku walked an extraordinary tightrope: providing base access and logistical support while maintaining the fiction that Japan was not a co-belligerent. The parallels to Gulf states in 2026 — hosting American forces, condemning Iranian attacks, but insisting they are not participants — are almost exact.

From New Delhi, the Vietnam War was an opportunity for moral positioning. India under Indira Gandhi criticized American intervention, maintained relations with Hanoi, and used the conflict to burnish its non-aligned credentials. But India paid no material cost for this stance; it was geographically distant, energy-independent (India was not yet a major oil importer), and unconcerned about Southeast Asian security architectures. The luxury of principled neutrality was underwritten by geographical irrelevance.

The critical difference in 2026: Asian nations are not geographically distant from the consequences of the US-Iran War. They are its primary economic victims. Japan's bases may again serve American operations, but Japan's energy supply depends on the strait being bombed. India's moral positioning is constrained by the bodies of its own sailors. China's careful calibration is tested by the possibility that its Hormuz exemption could be revoked at any moment. The Vietnam analogy holds structurally — Asian nations navigating a US war with limited agency — but the stakes are orders of magnitude higher because energy dependence has tied Asian prosperity directly to the Persian Gulf in ways that did not exist during the Vietnam era.

The lesson Asians drew from Vietnam was not the lesson Americans drew. Americans concluded that foreign wars are domestically unsustainable without public support. Asians concluded that when great powers fight in your neighborhood, the most dangerous position is to be dependent on the combatants for your security and your livelihood. Japan spent the next three decades reducing its dependence on any single energy source — diversifying from Middle Eastern oil to Australian coal, Indonesian LNG, and eventually nuclear power. The 2011 Fukushima disaster reversed the nuclear strategy and pushed Japan back toward Middle Eastern gas dependence — a regression now exposed as catastrophic.

South Korea drew the same lesson differently: if you cannot control the supply, control the technology. Korea's investment in nuclear power (which provides roughly 30% of its electricity) and its aggressive pursuit of energy efficiency were downstream consequences of the Vietnam-era recognition that small nations in great-power shadow zones must build resilience against disruptions they cannot prevent. That Korea now faces a nine-day gas stockpile countdown despite decades of diversification illustrates the limits of national strategy against global chokepoint vulnerability.

V. The Silk Road Memory: Two Thousand Years of Asian-Persian Commerce

Western commentary on the US-Iran War tends to frame Iran through the lens of the 1979 revolution, the hostage crisis, the nuclear program, and the "axis of evil." This framing is, at most, 47 years deep. The Asian framing is different — not because Asian leaders are sentimental about ancient history, but because the commercial relationships at stake have structural continuities measured in millennia.

The Silk Road — the network of overland trade routes connecting China to the Mediterranean — was, for most of its 1,500-year active history, an Iranian enterprise. The Achaemenid Empire's Royal Road (circa 500 BCE) predated the Silk Road proper but established the infrastructure. The Parthian Empire (247 BCE–224 CE) served as the primary intermediary for Chinese silk reaching Roman markets. The Sassanid Empire (224–651 CE) controlled the western terminus and much of the transit trade, exchanging Chinese silk for Persian glass, metalwork, and textiles. When the Chinese monk Xuanzang traveled to India in 629 CE, he passed through Central Asian territories — then under the Western Turkic Khaganate — along routes that connected the Sassanid and Chinese commercial spheres.

This is not antiquarian nostalgia. It is the deep structure of Asian economic geography. Iran sits at the physical crossroads of every overland route connecting East Asia to Europe, South Asia to Central Asia, and the Persian Gulf to the Mediterranean. China's Belt and Road Initiative — the $1 trillion infrastructure program launched in 2013 — is explicitly modeled on the Silk Road and explicitly includes Iran as a critical node. The China-Iran 25-year strategic cooperation agreement of March 2021 committed $400 billion in Chinese investment in Iranian energy and infrastructure. The Iran-China railway corridor, connecting Urumqi to Tehran via Central Asia, was designed as a land-based alternative to maritime routes — including the very maritime chokepoint now under selective closure.

The BRI's Iranian dimension reveals a strategic logic that has been obscured by the war's immediate chaos. If the Strait of Hormuz can be selectively closed — weaponized to favor some nations and punish others — then the argument for overland alternatives becomes not theoretical but urgent. Every Asian capital dependent on Hormuz transit is now recalculating the cost-benefit of Chinese-led overland infrastructure. Pakistan's Gwadar port (a Chinese-funded alternative to Hormuz-dependent Gulf shipping), the China-Central Asia-West Asia Economic Corridor, and even the long-stalled Iran-Pakistan-India gas pipeline acquire new strategic rationale.

The war, in this sense, is an argument for Chinese infrastructure with a persuasive force that no diplomatic communiqué could match. If American military action can disrupt the maritime routes that carry 40% of global oil trade, then the nations dependent on those routes have a material interest in alternatives that bypass American naval dominance. This is the strategic subtext of China's careful positioning: not just to benefit from the current disruption but to use the disruption as a permanent argument for a Chinese-centered continental trade architecture.

The Indian perspective on the Silk Road memory is more ambivalent. India's Chabahar port was explicitly designed as a counter to Chinese-funded Gwadar, located just 170 kilometers east along the Pakistani coast. The two ports represent competing visions of Asian connectivity — one Indian-backed, one Chinese-backed — both requiring Iranian stability. The war has damaged both projects, but Gwadar (protected by the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor's political architecture) may prove more resilient than Chabahar (dependent on a US sanctions waiver expiring in April 2026 for a country the US is now bombing).

For the nations of Central Asia — Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan — the Silk Road memory is not memory at all but present reality. Their oil and gas exports depend on pipeline routes that pass through or near Iranian territory. The war threatens not only Persian Gulf energy flows but the stability of the entire corridor from the Caspian to the Chinese border. Astana and Tashkent have been conspicuously silent — the silence of nations calculating which great power will control their economic arteries when the dust settles.

VI. Non-Alignment in the Twenty-First Century: From Bandung to BRICS

The Asian-African Conference at Bandung, Indonesia, in April 1955 was the founding moment of what would become the Non-Aligned Movement. Twenty-nine nations — representing 1.5 billion people, more than half the world's population — gathered to assert their collective refusal to be conscripted into Cold War blocs. Nehru, Sukarno, Nasser, Zhou Enlai, and U Nu of Burma articulated a vision of sovereign equality, peaceful coexistence, and mutual respect that became the Dasa Sila — the Ten Principles of Bandung.

The Non-Aligned Movement (NAM), formally established in Belgrade in 1961, grew to 120 member states. It was, in its best moments, a genuine counterweight to superpower dominance — facilitating decolonization, amplifying Global South voices, and providing diplomatic space for nations that refused to choose between Washington and Moscow. It was also, in its worst moments, a talk shop that provided cover for authoritarian regimes, failed to prevent conflicts among its own members, and lost coherence as the Cold War's binary logic dissolved.

The Iran war of 2026 tests whether the institutional descendants of Bandung — NAM, BRICS, the Shanghai Cooperation Organization — can provide any meaningful alternative to great-power alignment when the stakes are existential.

The evidence, one week in, is discouraging. BRICS, which expanded in January 2024 to include Iran alongside Egypt, Ethiopia, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE, cannot reach consensus on a conflict involving one of its own members. India, the current BRICS chair, has been functionally silent. China has issued measured calls for restraint while securing preferential energy access. Russia — Iran's ostensible strategic partner — has offered diplomatic rhetoric but no material support, with the Kremlin and Iranian foreign ministry publicly contradicting each other about whether Tehran even requested assistance. Saudi Arabia and the UAE, both BRICS members, are signatories to the joint US-Gulf statement condemning Iran. As Ukrinform's diplomatic sources noted, "BRICS countries are unable to develop a coordinated position on Iran" — a failure that "indicates this format is gradually losing its actual geopolitical influence."

The structural problem is that twenty-first century non-alignment faces a different constraint matrix than its Cold War predecessor. In 1955, the superpowers primarily competed through military alliances and ideological influence. A nation could remain non-aligned by refusing to join NATO or the Warsaw Pact, maintaining diplomatic relations with both blocs, and diversifying its arms purchases. The costs of non-alignment were manageable because economic interdependence was limited.

In 2026, the instruments of compulsion are economic, technological, and infrastructural. The United States controls the global financial system (SWIFT, dollar clearing), dominates sea lanes through eleven carrier strike groups, and holds sanctions authority that can crater any nation's economy. China controls critical supply chains (rare earths, solar panels, electric vehicle batteries, telecommunications infrastructure) and is the largest trading partner of more than 120 countries. Non-alignment between these two poles is not a matter of declining to sign a mutual defense treaty; it is a matter of navigating a world in which your financial transactions, energy supply, technology access, and manufacturing inputs are controlled by one or both of the contending powers.

The "multi-alignment" strategy that Asian powers have pursued since roughly 2010 — security ties with Washington, economic ties with Beijing, energy ties with the Gulf, technology ties with everyone — was designed for a world of managed competition, not open conflict. The Iran war reveals its fundamental vulnerability: multi-alignment works only when the aligned powers are not actively at war in a theater that controls your energy supply. When they are, multi-alignment becomes multi-vulnerability.

Japan illustrates this perfectly. It is America's most important Asian ally, hosting 54,000 US troops. It is China's largest regional trading partner. It depends on Gulf energy transiting Hormuz. It has no independent capacity to secure its energy supply lines. Multi-alignment has given Japan prosperity but not resilience. Seoul, New Delhi, Singapore, and Bangkok face versions of the same predicament.

Is non-alignment viable in 2026? Only if "non-alignment" is redefined from a diplomatic posture to an infrastructure strategy — meaning nations that wish to maintain strategic autonomy must build physical alternatives to the chokepoints and supply chains controlled by great powers. This is precisely what China's BRI promises, which is precisely why the war strengthens Beijing's hand.

VII. Asian Lessons for the Post-War Order

Asia's modern history is, in large part, a history of post-war reconstructions — some successful, some catastrophic, none simple. The experiences of Japan, Korea, Vietnam, and others offer lessons for the aftermath of the Iran war that Western planners would do well to study and will probably ignore.

Japan's reconstruction (1945–1952) is the template most frequently invoked by advocates of regime change, and the one most frequently misunderstood. Japan's successful postwar transformation required: (1) unconditional surrender, eliminating factional resistance; (2) an intact bureaucratic class willing to collaborate with occupation authorities; (3) Emperor Hirohito's cooperation in legitimizing the occupation; (4) massive American economic investment (the Dodge Plan, counterpart funds); (5) the Korean War, which provided an industrial stimulus; and (6) seven years of direct military occupation by 350,000 troops.

None of these conditions obtain in Iran. The regime has not surrendered; it is fighting. Iran's bureaucracy is fused with the IRGC at every level. The supreme leader is dead, and his successor lacks legitimacy. No American ground forces are deployed. The US has explicitly demanded a role in choosing Iran's next leader but shown no willingness to commit the resources that regime transformation requires. The Japan model is a fantasy dressed as a precedent.

Korea's division (1945–present) offers a darker but more realistic parallel. Korea was divided not because of any internal logic but because two external powers — the US and USSR — drew a line across a map. The resulting partition produced two states, a devastating war (1950–1953), and seven decades of frozen conflict. The relevant lesson for Iran: external military intervention that destroys a regime without a plan for what follows frequently produces not reconstruction but fragmentation. Iran's ethnic and linguistic diversity — Persians, Azerbaijanis, Kurds, Arabs, Balochis — and the CIA's reported arming of Kurdish groups suggest that a Kurdish separatist movement, an Azerbaijani alignment with Baku, and Arab unrest in Khuzestan could produce not one successor state but several. The Korean precedent suggests that such divisions, once established under great-power patronage, prove extraordinarily durable.

Vietnam's reunification (1975–1986) demonstrates that even "successful" outcomes are measured in decades. After the fall of Saigon in April 1975, Vietnam endured a decade of economic catastrophe, international isolation, a border war with China (1979), and occupation of Cambodia before the Đổi Mới reforms of 1986 began the slow process of recovery. Vietnam's per capita GDP did not recover to its pre-war level until the mid-1990s — twenty years after the war ended. If Iran follows a similar trajectory, the humanitarian and economic costs will extend well into the 2040s.

Afghanistan (2001–2021) is the most recent and most sobering precedent, and it is an Asian one, though rarely framed that way. Twenty years of American occupation, $2.3 trillion in expenditure, and the lives of 2,448 American service members produced a state that collapsed in eleven days when the US withdrew. The Taliban's return demonstrated that regime change without social transformation is regime rental, not regime change. Iran is larger (roughly 90 million vs. 40 million), more urbanized, more industrialized, and more nationalistic than Afghanistan. The notion that it can be reshaped from the air is ahistorical.

What role will Asian powers play in a post-conflict Middle East? The honest answer: whatever role serves their energy security. China will seek to preserve access to Iranian oil and gas under any successor arrangement. It will offer reconstruction investment — as it has in every post-conflict environment from Angola to Sri Lanka — in exchange for resource access, without political conditions. India will attempt to salvage Chabahar or its equivalent. Japan and South Korea will push for guaranteed energy flows and diversified supply arrangements. None of these nations will contribute to political reconstruction because none has an interest in the outcome beyond stability sufficient to keep hydrocarbons flowing.

This is not cynicism. It is the pattern of Asian engagement with the Middle East for the past fifty years, and it has deep historical roots. Asian civilizations have traded with Persia for two millennia without attempting to govern it. The Western compulsion to reshape the region's political order — from Sykes-Picot (1916) to the Iraq invasion (2003) to the current war — has no Asian equivalent. From Beijing, Tokyo, and New Delhi, the Middle East is a source of energy and a market for goods, not a canvas for political experimentation.

Conclusion: The Patience of Civilizations

The great strategic theorist of ancient China, Sun Tzu, is often quoted in Western military academies: "The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting." Less often quoted is the passage that follows: "Hence to fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting."

China is not fighting in the US-Iran War. It is not conquering. It is waiting — as it waited during the Korean War's aftermath, the Vietnam War's escalation, the Gulf War's disruption, and the Iraq War's quagmire. In each case, the United States expended blood and treasure in the Middle East while China built factories, roads, ports, and institutions at home. The compounding effect of this strategic patience, measured across seven decades, is the single most important geopolitical fact of the twenty-first century.

But patience is not passivity, and the 2026 war presents risks that previous conflicts did not. The energy crisis is immediate and severe. The disruption to Asian supply chains is real. The precedent of preemptive war against a sovereign state is alarming to every nation that has ever considered acquiring nuclear weapons — which includes several of China's neighbors. The selective Hormuz closure demonstrates that the global commons can be weaponized, a lesson that applies as readily to the Malacca Strait as to Hormuz.

The view from Asia, ultimately, is one of constrained agency and long memory. Asian nations did not choose this war, cannot stop it, and will bear its heaviest material costs. Their response will be shaped not by the moral frameworks that dominate Western debate — legality, human rights, democratic values — but by the strategic imperatives that have governed Asian statecraft for centuries: secure your energy, diversify your dependencies, avoid other people's wars, and position yourself to benefit from the peace.

Nehru called this non-alignment. Deng Xiaoping called it "hiding your strength and biding your time" (taoguang yanghui). Lee Kuan Yew called it pragmatism. Whatever the label, the strategy is as old as the Silk Road itself: let the great powers exhaust themselves, and trade with whoever wins.

The question the Iran war poses for Asia is whether this ancient strategy remains viable in a world of instantaneous financial flows, integrated supply chains, and energy dependencies that can be weaponized in hours. The answer will not come from any single decision or declaration. It will emerge, as Asian answers always do, over years and decades — in the infrastructure built, the alliances recalibrated, the reserves accumulated, and the quiet choices made while the world watches the explosions.
