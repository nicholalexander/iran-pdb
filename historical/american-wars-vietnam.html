Introduction

On the evening of February 28, 2026, the United States launched Operation Epic Fury — the largest American military operation since the invasion of Iraq in March 2003. Within minutes, precision strikes killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei before Iranian air defenses could respond. Within hours, strikes had expanded across all twenty-four Iranian provinces. Within six days, the stated objective had expanded from counterproliferation to regime change, with President Trump demanding to be "involved in the appointment" of Iran's next leader. The war commands the support of roughly one in four Americans — the lowest initial approval for any major US military operation in the history of modern polling.

None of this is unprecedented. Not the preemptive strike launched without congressional authorization or UN mandate. Not the rapid escalation of war aims. Not the gap between tactical brilliance and strategic incoherence. Not the administration's insistence that military victory equates to political resolution. What is unprecedented is the speed at which patterns that took years to unfold in Vietnam and months in Iraq have manifested in days.

This essay examines what the history of American war-making — specifically Vietnam (1955–1975), Iraq (2003–2011), and Afghanistan (2001–2021) — can tell us about the trajectory, logic, and likely consequences of the US-Iran War. It argues that Operation Epic Fury represents not a departure from the pattern of American wars since 1945, but its most compressed and accelerated expression. The structural dynamics that produced quagmire in Southeast Asia, strategic failure in Mesopotamia, and twenty years of futility in the Hindu Kush are already visible in the Iran campaign — not as distant analogies, but as recurring features of how the United States initiates, escalates, justifies, and ultimately fails to conclude its wars.

I. The Vietnam Parallel: What Maps and What Doesn't

The invocation of Vietnam in American war discourse has become so reflexive as to be almost meaningless. Every conflict since 1975 has been called "another Vietnam" by its opponents and declared categorically unlike Vietnam by its proponents. Serious historical analysis requires more precision.

What maps. The most direct parallel between Vietnam and Iran 2026 is the relationship between pretext and reality. On August 4, 1964, President Lyndon Johnson told the American people that North Vietnamese torpedo boats had launched an "unprovoked" attack on the USS Maddox in the Gulf of Tonkin. The Senate passed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution 88-2 three days later, granting Johnson effectively unlimited authority to use military force in Southeast Asia. We now know, from declassified NSA documents and the testimony of participants, that the August 4 incident almost certainly did not occur — that sonar operators were tracking phantom contacts in heavy seas, that Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara suppressed contradictory intelligence, and that the administration exploited ambiguous evidence to secure authorization for a war it had already decided to fight.

Operation Epic Fury dispensed with even this pretense. There was no precipitating attack on American forces or territory. The Pentagon, when pressed by Congress, acknowledged that Iran posed no imminent threat to the United States. The justification — counterproliferation, the destruction of Iran's missile and nuclear capabilities — rests on the doctrine of preemptive war, which the United States formally adopted in the 2002 National Security Strategy and first applied in Iraq. But where Johnson at least manufactured a casus belli and secured congressional authorization, the Iran campaign launched without either. The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution, for all its dishonesty, was a congressional act. The Iran war began, was challenged under the War Powers Resolution, and survived: the Senate voted 47-53 against constraining the president; the House defeated its own resolution 212-219. Both chambers effectively ratified presidential war-making after the fact. The constitutional trajectory from Tonkin to Tehran is a trajectory of declining democratic constraint.

The escalation dynamics offer a second parallel, though the timescale is radically different. Vietnam's escalation was glacial by comparison. President Eisenhower sent the first military advisors in 1955. Kennedy expanded the advisory mission to 16,300 personnel by 1963. Johnson initiated sustained bombing (Operation Rolling Thunder) in March 1965 and committed ground combat troops that same month. By 1968, 536,100 American troops were deployed. The escalation from advisory mission to full-scale ground war took a decade. At each stage, the expansion of commitment was framed as a modest, necessary adjustment — what David Halberstam called the logic of "one more step."

In Iran, the escalation from counterproliferation to regime change took six days. On February 28, the stated objective was the destruction of Iran's missile and nuclear capabilities. By March 5, Trump was demanding a role in selecting Iran's next leader, comparing the situation to Venezuela, and calling the designated successor Mojtaba Khamenei "a lightweight." Defense Secretary Hegseth declared that American firepower was "about to surge dramatically" and that the campaign was "just getting started." The White House offered Iran's leadership a binary: "immunity" or "absolutely guaranteed death." The structural dynamic is identical — limited objectives giving way to maximalist aims — but the compression is extraordinary. What took Johnson three years (from Tonkin in August 1964 to the commitment of 500,000 troops by 1967) has taken Trump less than a week.

The "credibility" trap, which I will examine at greater length below, operates in both cases. Johnson's national security team — McGeorge Bundy, Dean Rusk, McNamara — consistently argued that American credibility as a superpower required demonstrating resolve in Vietnam. The domino theory provided the strategic rationale, but credibility provided the psychological one. As McNamara wrote in a 1965 memo to Johnson, 70% of the reason for fighting in Vietnam was "to avoid a humiliating US defeat (to our reputation as a guarantor)." The actual welfare of the South Vietnamese people accounted for roughly 10% of his calculus. In Iran, the credibility argument is already audible: Hegseth's language about the campaign being "just getting started," the refusal to acknowledge the CIA back-channel contact that Iran initiated on March 1, and the administration's systematic closure of diplomatic off-ramps all reflect the same logic. To negotiate now would be to admit that the killing of a head of state, 175 children at Minab, and the disruption of the global economy were undertaken for objectives that could have been achieved by other means.

What doesn't map. The differences are equally important. Vietnam was a ground war fought by conscripts against a nationalist insurgency with deep popular support, operating on its own territory with sanctuary across an international border. Iran 2026 is a standoff air and missile campaign fought by an all-volunteer professional military against a state's conventional forces. There are no American ground troops in Iran and, as of Day 7, no indication that any will be deployed. The IRGC, whatever its domestic support, is not the Viet Cong — it is a state military apparatus, not a guerrilla movement rooted in peasant mobilization.

Vietnam's geography — triple-canopy jungle, a 17,000-mile coastline, porous borders — negated American technological superiority. Iran's geography is different but no less challenging: a country of 1.65 million square kilometers with mountainous terrain, 88 million people, and a military doctrine designed precisely for asymmetric resistance to American air power. The coalition has achieved "near-complete aerial superiority" over Iran, as IDF Chief Zamir declared on March 5. But aerial superiority over North Vietnam was never in question either. The United States dropped more bombs on Vietnam than all parties dropped in all theaters of World War II combined. It lost the war.

The most significant difference is the draft. The conscription system that fed the Vietnam War also generated its most powerful opposition. The universal vulnerability of military service — the knowledge that any American family's son could be sent to die in the Mekong Delta — created a constituency for opposition that cut across class, race, and region (though, as Christian Appy documented in Working-Class War, the burdens fell disproportionately on the poor). The all-volunteer force that fights in Iran insulates the majority of Americans from the personal consequences of war. This is not incidental; it is by design, a lesson the national security establishment drew explicitly from Vietnam.

II. Iraq 2003: The Middle Chapter

The US-Iran War cannot be understood without Iraq, and not merely as analogy. The 2003 invasion of Iraq created the material conditions for the 2026 war in ways that are causal, not merely parallel.

Begin with the doctrinal inheritance. The Bush administration's 2002 National Security Strategy formalized the doctrine of preemptive war — the claim that the United States could attack states that posed potential future threats, even absent imminent danger. This doctrine justified the invasion of Iraq on the basis of weapons of mass destruction that did not exist, intelligence that was manipulated (the "Curveball" source, the aluminum tubes, the yellowcake uranium from Niger), and a public case built on fear rather than evidence. Secretary of State Colin Powell's February 2003 presentation to the UN Security Council — which he later called a "blot" on his record — represents perhaps the most consequential act of manufactured intelligence in American diplomatic history.

Operation Epic Fury rests on the same doctrinal foundation. The Pentagon's admission to Congress that Iran posed no imminent threat is the 2026 equivalent of the missing WMDs. The difference is that the Bush administration at least attempted to construct an evidentiary case, however dishonest, and secured congressional authorization through the Authorization for Use of Military Force Against Iraq (October 2002). The Iran campaign skipped both steps. The doctrinal evolution from Iraq to Iran is not a departure from preemptive war doctrine but its logical terminus: if the president can attack a state that poses no imminent threat, the requirement for evidence or authorization becomes purely decorative.

The assumption that military victory produces political outcomes is the second inheritance. The "Mission Accomplished" banner aboard the USS Abraham Lincoln on May 1, 2003 — forty-three days after the invasion began — has become shorthand for the delusion that toppling a regime constitutes winning a war. Baghdad fell on April 9. The insurgency began within weeks. The United States would fight in Iraq for eight more years, at a cost of 4,431 American dead, an estimated 150,000-600,000 Iraqi dead (depending on methodology), and $2 trillion in direct expenditure.

The Iran campaign is reproducing this assumption at accelerated speed. The killing of Khamenei, the destruction of 80% of air defenses, the 90% reduction in ballistic missile launches, the sinking of 30+ naval vessels — all of this represents extraordinary tactical achievement. IDF Chief Zamir's declaration of "near-complete aerial superiority" and the announcement of a "next phase" targeting underground sites and "regime foundations" mirrors the rhetoric of April 2003 with uncanny precision. But the question that Iraq should have taught us to ask — what happens the day after the shooting stops? — remains unanswered.

De-Ba'athification offers the most precise cautionary tale. When L. Paul Bremer issued Coalition Provisional Authority Orders 1 and 2 in May 2003 — dissolving the Iraqi military and banning Ba'ath Party members from government employment — he eliminated the administrative capacity of the Iraqi state at a stroke. An estimated 400,000 armed men were simultaneously unemployed. The insurgency that followed was, in significant part, a direct consequence of this decision. The Sunni officers and officials who had run Iraq's government and military became the cadre of first the Sunni insurgency and later the Islamic State.

Trump's demand to be "involved in the appointment" of Iran's next leader, and the White House's call for IRGC members to "lay down arms" and diplomats to "seek asylum," reproduce the logic of de-Ba'athification with a state apparatus of 88 million people. The IRGC is not merely a military organization; it controls an estimated 20-40% of Iran's economy, manages vast construction and engineering enterprises, and employs millions directly and indirectly. Dismantling it would replicate the Iraqi catastrophe at a scale four times larger.

Most directly, Iraq created the conditions for 2026. The power vacuum left by the destruction of Saddam Hussein's regime empowered Iran as the dominant external actor in Iraqi politics. The sectarian civil war that followed the invasion drove Iraq's Shia majority toward Iranian patronage. The Islamic State, born from the remnants of de-Ba'athification, drew Iran's IRGC and its proxy network deeper into the region. The "Shia Crescent" that American strategists now cite as a threat is, in substantial part, an artifact of the 2003 invasion. We are, in a sense, bombing the consequences of our last bombing.

III. The Domestic Front: From Kent State to Austin

On May 4, 1970, Ohio National Guardsmen fired sixty-seven rounds in thirteen seconds at students protesting the expansion of the Vietnam War into Cambodia. Four students — Allison Krause, Jeffrey Miller, Sandra Scheuer, and William Schroeder — were killed. Nine were wounded. Scheuer and Schroeder were not even part of the protest; they were walking between classes. The Kent State massacre catalyzed the largest student strike in American history: over four million students at 450 universities walked out. One hundred thousand people marched on Washington. Construction workers in New York attacked the protesters with hard hats and wrenches while police watched.

The Vietnam-era antiwar movement was the most sustained and consequential domestic opposition to an American war since the Civil War. The October 15, 1969 Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam brought an estimated two million people into the streets across the country — the largest single-day demonstration in American history to that point. Draft resistance, which began with individual acts of conscience (the burning of draft cards, the flight to Canada) and escalated to organized mass refusal, struck at the material foundation of the war effort. By 1969, the Selective Service System was processing 300,000 inductions per year; by 1972, the system was in crisis, with conviction rates for draft evasion plummeting as juries refused to convict.

The domestic opposition to the Iran war operates in a fundamentally different political and structural environment. The most obvious difference is the absence of conscription. The all-volunteer force, established in 1973 in direct response to Vietnam, was designed to decouple military service from civilian life and thereby reduce the political costs of war. It has succeeded. Six American servicemembers have been killed in Iran as of Day 7 — all in Kuwait, none in combat in Iran itself. The families of the dead are concentrated in the military communities of Iowa and Sacramento, not distributed across every congressional district in the country. The differential exposure to sacrifice that Appy documented in Vietnam — where working-class and minority communities bore disproportionate burdens — has been institutionalized into a system where the vast majority of Americans bear no burden at all.

And yet: 25% approval. The lowest initial support for any major American military operation in polling history. No rally-around-the-flag effect. Trump's overall approval at an all-time low of 44%. How do we explain mass opposition to a war that imposes almost no direct cost on the opposing public?

Three factors distinguish 2026 from both the Vietnam era and the early Iraq period. First, the post-Iraq trust deficit. The American public was lied to about weapons of mass destruction in 2003, and it knows it was lied to. When the Pentagon acknowledges that Iran posed no imminent threat — a concession extracted by congressional questioning, not volunteered — the public maps this onto the WMD template immediately. The credibility of preemptive war justifications has been permanently degraded by Iraq. Seventy-six percent of Americans supported the Iraq invasion in March 2003; by 2007, that figure had fallen below 30%. The Iran war starts where Iraq ended.

Second, the information environment. The Vietnam-era antiwar movement operated through teach-ins, underground newspapers, and network television coverage that, despite government pressure, eventually turned critical (Walter Cronkite's February 1968 editorial is the iconic moment). The Iraq war was fought in an era of embedded journalism and Fox News patriotism, where dissent could be marginalized as unpatriotic. The Iran war unfolds in an information ecosystem where BBC Verify contradicts CENTCOM claims within hours, where Amnesty International verifies the Minab school strike with satellite imagery and six independent videos, and where the gap between official narrative and observable reality is exposed in real time. The administration cannot manufacture consent because the infrastructure of consent-manufacturing — centralized media, information asymmetry, patriotic deference — no longer exists.

Third, the Austin shooting. On March 2, four days into the campaign, a man wearing Iranian flag imagery killed three people at a bar in Austin, Texas. The FBI opened a terrorism investigation. The incident immediately bifurcated the domestic political landscape: for the administration and its allies, it validated the securitization of dissent (Representative Scalise cited the FBI's elevated threat warning); for opponents, it validated the core antiwar claim that the war generates blowback. The cycle that took years during Vietnam — protest, radicalization, violence, repression, deeper polarization — has been compressed into days. Kent State occurred five years into major combat operations. Austin occurred four days in.

The all-volunteer force functions as what the political scientist Andrew Bacevich has called a "political insulator" — it allows wars to be fought without mobilizing the population, and therefore without creating the mass constituency for opposition that conscription generated in Vietnam. But the insulator has limits. It insulates against casualty-driven opposition; it does not insulate against economic opposition. Sixty-three percent of voters are concerned about the gas price impacts of the Iran war. When costs are made explicit, support drops to 18%. The Strait of Hormuz's selective closure, the triple chokepoint crisis, and rising energy prices may accomplish what the absence of a draft prevents: they may make the war's costs visible and personal to Americans who have no family member in uniform.

IV. The Credibility Trap

In November 1967, General William Westmoreland returned from Vietnam to address the National Press Club in Washington. "We have reached an important point," he told the assembled reporters, "when the end begins to come into view." Two months later, on January 30, 1968, the Tet Offensive shattered this assessment. Eighty-four thousand Viet Cong and North Vietnamese troops attacked simultaneously across South Vietnam, penetrating the US Embassy compound in Saigon, seizing the imperial capital of Huế for twenty-five days, and demonstrating that the "light at the end of the tunnel" was, as Senator George Aiken quipped, an oncoming train.

Tet was a military defeat for the North Vietnamese — they suffered catastrophic casualties and failed to hold any major objective. But it was a strategic victory of the first order, because it revealed the unbridgeable gap between the official narrative of progress and the observable reality of a war that was, at best, stalemated. This gap — what journalists called the "credibility gap" — is the defining pathology of American wars.

The credibility trap operates through a specific mechanism. Once a war is initiated, the political costs of admitting failure exceed the political costs of continuing. Each additional commitment of blood and treasure raises the retrospective stakes: if we withdraw now, then the previous sacrifices were "in vain." This logic, which the economist would recognize as the sunk cost fallacy and the psychologist as escalation of commitment, has driven the persistence of every major American war since Korea.

In Vietnam, the trap operated for a decade. Johnson could not withdraw because withdrawal would mean that the 58,000 Americans who would eventually die had died for nothing. Nixon could not withdraw because withdrawal would damage American "credibility" — the belief among allies and adversaries that the United States would honor its commitments. Nixon's "peace with honor" formulation was itself a product of the credibility trap: the "honor" required continued fighting to produce conditions that looked, however implausibly, like something other than defeat. The result was four more years of war, an expansion into Cambodia and Laos, and a final withdrawal in 1975 that produced exactly the outcome Nixon had fought to prevent.

In Iraq, the trap was articulated most clearly by the "surge" debate of 2006-2007. By late 2006, the Iraq Study Group — a bipartisan commission chaired by James Baker and Lee Hamilton — concluded that the situation was "grave and deteriorating" and recommended a drawdown. Instead, President George W. Bush ordered a surge of 20,000 additional troops, justified explicitly in the language of credibility: "If we increase our support at this crucial moment, and help the Iraqis break the current cycle of violence, we can hasten the day our troops begin coming home." The surge produced a temporary reduction in violence but did not resolve any of the political conflicts that had generated the insurgency. The United States withdrew in 2011. The Islamic State seized Mosul in 2014. The sacrifices were, by any honest assessment, in vain.

In Afghanistan, the trap persisted for twenty years. The original objective — the destruction of al-Qaeda's safe haven — was substantially achieved by 2002. The war continued for nineteen more years because no president was willing to accept the political consequences of withdrawal. Obama surged to 100,000 troops in 2010 while simultaneously announcing a withdrawal timeline — a contradiction that satisfied no one. Trump negotiated with the Taliban while maintaining the fiction that the Afghan government was viable. Biden finally withdrew in August 2021, producing the chaotic scenes at Kabul airport that the credibility trap had been designed to prevent.

The Iran war is six days old, and the trap is already forming. Hegseth's declaration that the campaign is "just getting started" and that the United States is prepared to "go far longer" than four to five weeks establishes the rhetorical framework. Trump's "I said Too Late!" — his rejection of the diplomatic overture Iran made through the CIA on March 1 — closes the off-ramp that might have prevented escalation. The White House's ultimatum of "immunity" or "absolutely guaranteed death" eliminates the middle ground where negotiations occur. Six American servicemembers are dead. One hundred seventy-five children are dead at Minab. A head of state has been assassinated. The Strait of Hormuz is functionally closed. To stop now would be to acknowledge that all of this was undertaken without adequate justification — that the Pentagon's own admission of "no imminent threat" means what it plainly says. The credibility trap does not require years to form. It requires only that the costs already incurred be large enough to make honesty politically unbearable.

V. War Aims Creep: The Structural Logic of Expansion

The pattern is so consistent across American wars as to constitute something close to a law: limited objectives expand to encompass regime change and political transformation. The expansion is not accidental. It is driven by structural incentives that are embedded in the American way of war.

Vietnam. The original objective was containment — preventing the fall of South Vietnam to communism as part of the broader Cold War strategy. By 1965, the objective had expanded to include the creation of a viable, democratic South Vietnamese state — a goal that required not merely military defense but political transformation of a society the United States barely understood. The failure to achieve this expanded objective produced further escalation, as military force was applied to problems that were fundamentally political. The strategic bombing campaigns — Rolling Thunder (1965-1968), Linebacker I and II (1972) — reflected the persistent American belief that sufficient application of firepower could compel political outcomes. It could not.

Iraq. The stated objective was the destruction of weapons of mass destruction. Within weeks of the invasion, the objective had expanded to include regime change (achieved with the fall of Baghdad), de-Ba'athification (CPA Orders 1 and 2), and the construction of a democratic Iraqi state (the Coalition Provisional Authority, the transitional government, the 2005 constitution and elections). Each expansion of objectives required deeper commitment and longer timelines. The original war plan envisioned a force drawdown to 30,000 troops by September 2003. Instead, troop levels increased to 170,000 by November 2007.

Iran 2026. On February 28, the objective was counterproliferation — the destruction of Iran's missile and nuclear capabilities. By March 5 — Day 6 — Trump demanded to be "involved in the appointment" of Iran's next leader. The White House called on IRGC members to "lay down arms" and diplomats to "seek asylum." IDF Chief Zamir announced a "next phase" targeting "regime foundations." The progression from counterproliferation to regime change took six days. In Iraq, the equivalent progression took weeks. In Vietnam, it took years.

The structural incentives that drive this expansion are threefold. First, military success generates its own momentum. The destruction of Iran's air defenses, the killing of Khamenei, the degradation of missile capabilities — each tactical success makes the next objective seem achievable. If we can destroy their air force, why not their nuclear program? If we can kill their leader, why not reshape their government? The logic of capability becomes the logic of obligation. Second, the domestic political incentive structure rewards escalation over de-escalation. A president who initiates a war and then accepts a limited outcome invites the charge of having "wasted" the sacrifice. A president who escalates can point to expanded objectives as evidence of ambition and resolve. Third, the military institutional culture rewards operational expansion. Every combatant command, every service branch, every weapons program has an interest in demonstrating its relevance through mission expansion.

The result is a ratchet that turns in only one direction. War aims expand because the political, institutional, and psychological incentives all favor expansion and none favor restraint.

VI. The Pentagon Papers Moment

In June 1971, The New York Times began publishing the Pentagon Papers — a 7,000-page classified study commissioned by Secretary of Defense McNamara documenting the history of US decision-making in Vietnam from 1945 to 1967. The study revealed that the government had "systematically lied, not only to the public but also to Congress, about a subject of transcendent national interest and concern." Four administrations had privately assessed that the war was unwinnable while publicly insisting that victory was imminent. The credibility gap, long suspected, was now documented in the government's own words.

Every American war has its Pentagon Papers moment — the point at which the gap between public narrative and internal assessment becomes undeniable. In Iraq, it was the failure to find WMDs, confirmed by the Iraq Survey Group's final report in September 2004. In Afghanistan, it was the "Afghanistan Papers" published by the Washington Post in 2019, revealing that senior officials had consistently misrepresented the war's progress while privately acknowledging failure.

The Iran war may have reached its Pentagon Papers moment before it began. The Pentagon's admission to Congress that Iran posed no imminent threat — the factual basis for the war's justification — is not a leaked document or a whistleblower's revelation. It is an official concession, extracted through the constitutional process, that the war's stated rationale does not meet the standard the administration implicitly invoked. Combined with the 25% approval rating, the failed but narrow War Powers votes, and the rapid expansion of objectives from counterproliferation to regime change, the credibility gap is not a gap that opened over time. It was present at the moment of initiation.

This is historically unprecedented. The Vietnam credibility gap took years to develop; the Pentagon Papers documented deception spanning four presidencies and two decades. The Iraq credibility gap emerged over months as WMD searches came up empty. The Iran credibility gap is a structural feature of the war's inception. The public does not believe the government's justification, Congress narrowly ratified the war anyway, and the administration has already moved past its original rationale to objectives (regime change) that it did not publicly articulate before the strikes began.

Whether this terminal credibility gap produces political consequences depends on institutional mechanisms that are themselves in crisis. The War Powers Resolution has failed its test. The courts are unlikely to intervene. The media environment prevents consent-manufacturing but does not translate dissent into constraint. The November 2026 midterm elections are the first structural checkpoint — eight months away. The question is whether a war with 25% approval can sustain itself for eight months through institutional inertia alone, or whether the economic costs (rising energy prices, potential recession) create political pressure that operates faster than the electoral calendar.

VII. What Vietnam Actually Teaches

The popular lesson of Vietnam — "don't get into a quagmire" — is so vague as to be useless. No president sets out to create a quagmire. The deeper structural lessons are specific, well-documented, and directly applicable to Iran 2026.

The limits of air power. The United States dropped 7.5 million tons of bombs on Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia — more than twice the total tonnage dropped by all parties in World War II. Operation Rolling Thunder, the sustained bombing campaign against North Vietnam from March 1965 to November 1968, flew 304,000 sorties and dropped 643,000 tons of bombs. The Strategic Bombing Survey conducted after the campaign concluded that it had failed to reduce North Vietnam's capacity to wage war, failed to break the population's will, and failed to interdict the flow of supplies to the south. The CIA's own assessment, delivered to Johnson in 1967, was that the bombing had "not significantly affected North Vietnam's overall strategy."

The Iran campaign has delivered 6,000+ munitions across 2,500 strikes in six days — extraordinary by any measure. It has destroyed 80% of Iran's air defenses, degraded missile launch rates by 90%, and achieved near-complete aerial superiority. These are genuine military achievements that Rolling Thunder never approximated. But the strategic question is the same: can air power compel political outcomes? The destruction of Iran's military infrastructure does not produce a successor government, does not resolve the succession crisis, does not feed 88 million people under internet blackout, and does not create the political conditions for the "new Iran" the administration apparently envisions. Air power can destroy. It cannot build. This was true over Hanoi in 1967, over Baghdad in 2003, and over Tehran in 2026.

The mismatch between military capability and political objectives. The United States military is, by virtually any measure, the most capable fighting force in human history. It has been consistently unable to translate military superiority into political outcomes in adversary states with populations that do not welcome its intervention. This is not a failure of capability but a category error — the assumption that political problems have military solutions. Vietnam's political problem was a nationalist movement with deep popular legitimacy that the United States could not defeat without destroying the country it claimed to be saving. Iraq's political problem was a sectarian society whose internal conflicts could not be resolved by an external occupier. Iran's political problem — an 88-million-person state with a complex political system, a deep nationalist tradition, and a population that may despise its government but also resents foreign intervention — is no more susceptible to military resolution.

The impossibility of destroying an idea with bombs. Ho Chi Minh's revolution drew its power from Vietnamese nationalism and anti-colonial sentiment, not from Soviet or Chinese patronage. The American strategy of attrition — killing enough enemy combatants to break the insurgency — failed because it misidentified the source of the enemy's strength. Each bombing raid that killed civilians generated new recruits. Each village destroyed generated new supporters. The body count — McNamara's preferred metric of progress — measured the wrong thing entirely.

In Iran, the relevant "idea" is not the Islamic Republic's ideology, which commands diminishing domestic support, but Iranian nationalism itself — a force with roots far deeper than the 1979 revolution, extending back through Mosaddegh, the Constitutional Revolution of 1906, and millennia of Persian civilization. The killing of Khamenei, 175 children at Minab, and the bombing of Azadi Stadium (literally "Freedom Stadium") risk achieving what the Islamic Republic's repression could not: unifying a fractured Iranian population against an external enemy. The Tet Offensive demonstrated that military setbacks can become political victories when they expose the gap between narrative and reality. The Minab school strike may prove to be Iran's equivalent — a tactical incident that reshapes the political landscape by making the human cost of the campaign undeniable.

What a serious Vietnam scholar would say about Iran 2026. The honest assessment is that the Vietnam analogy is both illuminating and incomplete. Vietnam was a twenty-year war of incremental escalation fought by conscripts against a peasant insurgency in tropical terrain. Iran 2026 is (so far) a six-day air campaign fought by professionals against a state military in arid and mountainous terrain. The specific military dynamics are different. But the structural pathologies are identical: preemptive war launched on inadequate justification, rapid expansion of war aims, the assumption that military capability translates to political outcomes, the closure of diplomatic off-ramps through escalatory rhetoric, and the credibility trap that makes de-escalation politically impossible.

The deepest lesson of Vietnam is not tactical but epistemological: the American national security establishment consistently overestimates its understanding of the societies it seeks to transform and underestimates the complexity of the political dynamics it seeks to control. McNamara, who spent seven years managing the Vietnam War, wrote in his 1995 memoir In Retrospect that "we were wrong, terribly wrong" — that the war's architects had operated with fundamental ignorance of Vietnamese history, culture, and politics. "We viewed the people and leaders of South Vietnam in terms of our own experience," he wrote. "We totally misjudged the political forces within the country."

There is no evidence that the architects of Operation Epic Fury possess a deeper understanding of Iran than McNamara's team possessed of Vietnam. The demand to be "involved in the appointment" of Iran's next leader suggests, if anything, less understanding — an assumption that an 88-million-person civilization with a 2,500-year history can be reorganized by external fiat. De-Ba'athification in a country of 26 million produced a decade-long insurgency and the Islamic State. The equivalent in a country of 88 million, with a far more sophisticated political culture and a military designed for asymmetric resistance, would produce consequences that current planning does not appear to contemplate.

Conclusion: The Recurring Logic

The US-Iran War is six days old. The administration insists it will last weeks. Military planners reportedly anticipate months. Politico reports it may last until September. The pattern of American wars since 1945 suggests that these estimates are optimistic — not because Iran can win militarily, but because the gap between military victory and political resolution has never been bridged by air power, regime change, or force of arms.

What Vietnam, Iraq, and Afghanistan teach — collectively, at the cost of roughly 7,000 American dead in Vietnam (before the major escalation), 4,431 in Iraq, 2,461 in Afghanistan, and hundreds of thousands of foreign dead — is that the United States excels at initiating wars and struggles to conclude them. The structural dynamics that produce this pattern are not failures of execution but features of the system: the doctrine of preemptive war that lowers the threshold for initiation, the expansion of war aims that raises the threshold for acceptable outcomes, the credibility trap that makes de-escalation costlier than continuation, the all-volunteer force that insulates the public from sacrifice, and the presidential war-making authority that operates without effective institutional constraint.

All of these dynamics are present in the Iran campaign, not as analogies but as active forces shaping decisions in real time. The question is not whether Iran 2026 is "another Vietnam" — the specific circumstances are different enough to make the comparison imprecise. The question is whether the structural logic of American war-making has changed since 1965. On the evidence of the first six days, it has not. It has merely accelerated.
