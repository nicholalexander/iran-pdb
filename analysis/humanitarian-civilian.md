# Humanitarian & Civilian Impact Analysis: US-Iran War
## February 28 – March 5, 2026

**Classification:** OPEN SOURCE | **Assessment Date:** March 6, 2026, 03:00 UTC
**Prepared by:** Independent Humanitarian Analysis Unit
**Methodology:** Multi-source reconciliation drawing on Iranian government figures, international humanitarian organizations (ICRC, WHO, UNHCR, OCHA), independent human rights monitors (Hengaw, HRANA, Amnesty International), satellite verification (BBC Verify), and international media reporting in 15+ languages.

---

## Executive Summary

Six days into the US-Israeli military campaign against Iran (Operation Epic Fury / Operation Roaring Lion), the humanitarian toll is already severe and accelerating. Conservative estimates place Iranian deaths at 1,230–2,400, with at least 310–742 confirmed civilians, including 175 children killed in a single school strike. At least 13 hospitals and health facilities have been struck. Over 330,000 people have been displaced across the region. Iraq's entire power grid has collapsed, leaving 43 million people without electricity. Iran's 90+ million citizens have endured 130+ hours of near-total internet blackout. The WHO's global emergency logistics hub in Dubai is non-operational, jeopardizing humanitarian supply chains for 75 countries. Gulf states have absorbed over 1,000 Iranian missile and drone attacks, with civilian casualties in the UAE, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Qatar. The humanitarian crisis is no longer confined to Iran—it is regional, cascading, and deepening by the hour.

## 1. Casualty Analysis: Reconciling Conflicting Death Tolls

### The Numbers Problem

Every armed conflict produces competing casualty narratives. This war is no different, but the speed and scale of the divergence is striking. Six days in, we have at least five distinct casualty streams, none of which can be independently verified due to Iran's internet blackout:

| Source | Total Dead | Date of Estimate | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| Iran Foundation of Martyrs | 1,230 | March 5 | Official government figure; includes military |
| Iranian Red Crescent | 787 | March 3 | Reported via Amnesty International |
| Iran Emergency Services | 6,000+ wounded | March 5 | First quantification of injuries |
| Hengaw (Kurdish HR org) | 2,400 (310 civilian / 2,090 military) | March 4 | Field-based documentation |
| HRANA (US-based Iranian HR) | 742 civilians specifically | March 3 | Civilian-only count, "hundreds more under review" |
| UN/WHO | 550 civilians | March 2 | Early estimate, rapidly outdated |

**Assessment:** The credible range for total Iranian deaths as of March 5 is **1,200–2,500**, with the higher figure more consistent with Hengaw's field documentation across 163 cities in 24 provinces. The lower Foundation of Martyrs figure likely undercounts military deaths, which Iranian authorities have historically suppressed—Hengaw specifically reports that authorities are "largely refraining from releasing accurate casualty figures, particularly regarding military losses in Kurdistan" (Hengaw, March 4, 2026).

### Civilian vs. Military Breakdown

Hengaw's 13% civilian ratio (310 of 2,400) is the most granular breakdown available. HRANA's 742 civilian figure is higher, likely because it includes cases still under Hengaw's review. The actual civilian percentage is almost certainly higher than 13% for several reasons:

- Many "military" targets are located in or adjacent to residential areas
- Hengaw reports that IRGC units have relocated to schools, dormitories, and mosques—creating future civilian kill zones
- Infrastructure strikes (power, communications, transport) cause civilian deaths not captured in initial tallies
- Iran has not conducted systematic civilian evacuations near military sites (Hengaw, March 4)

**Credible civilian death range: 300–800 as of March 5, trending upward.**

### The Minab School Strike: Shajareh Tayyebeh

The single deadliest civilian incident of the war occurred on its first day. On February 28, a strike hit the Shajareh Tayyebeh primary school in Minab, Hormozgan Province, killing **175 people—predominantly schoolgirls and staff** (Reuters, March 3; the figure rose from initial reports of 150 to 165 to 175). Iran held a mass funeral for 165 victims on March 3 (Al Jazeera).

The UN described the bombing as a "grave violation of humanitarian law" (UN News, March 1). UNESCO warned it violated protections for educational institutions under IHL. The UN Human Rights Office called for a "prompt, impartial and thorough investigation" (Amnesty International, March 5).

Amnesty International verified six videos showing black smoke from a partially collapsed building, with rescuers and excavators searching rubble. Crucially, Amnesty noted that footage shows smoke "from the direction of a nearby Iranian Revolutionary Guards Compound"—suggesting the school may have been collateral damage from a strike on an adjacent military target, or that military and civilian facilities were in dangerous proximity (Amnesty International, March 5).

This is the deadliest single strike on a school in any conflict since the 2014 UNRWA school shellings in Gaza. It exceeds the 2018 Saudi-led coalition strike on a school bus in Dahyan, Yemen (40 children killed), by a factor of four.

### Hospital Strikes

The WHO confirmed on March 5 that **at least 13 hospitals and health facilities have been struck** since the war began, with reports of 4 medics killed and 25 injured (Guardian, March 5; WHO briefing). Iran's ambassador to the UN in Geneva alleged 10 facilities hit (letter to WHO Director-General). The head of Iran's Medical Council reported 10 medical centers damaged (Amnesty International).

**Gandhi Hospital, Tehran** is the most documented case. On March 1–2, a projectile struck a state TV communications tower across the street, causing severe collateral damage to the hospital. Patients in the neonatal ward were evacuated. Debris and shattered glass filled wards. The WHO confirmed evacuation due to "nearby explosions that caused collateral damage" (Reuters, March 2). Al Jazeera noted that the hospital's Motahari wing was also damaged. CNN broadcast footage of patients laid on floors amid debris.

Iran's MFA spokesperson Esmaeil Baghaei stated that the US and Israel "continue to indiscriminately strike residential areas, sparing neither hospitals, schools, Red Crescent facilities" (Al Jazeera, March 3). While the framing is propagandistic, the physical damage to multiple medical facilities is independently verified.

The WHO also reported four ambulances in Iran had been "affected" by strikes (WHO briefing, March 5).

### Historical Comparison

For context against other US-led military campaigns:

- **Iraq (2003 invasion, first 30 days):** Iraq Body Count documented ~7,000 civilian deaths. The Iran war is on a trajectory that could approach this within weeks if the "next phase" announced by IDF Chief Zamir targets regime infrastructure in populated areas.
- **Libya (2011, NATO campaign, 7 months):** HRI estimates 1,000–2,000 civilian deaths. Iran has likely exceeded this in six days.
- **Yemen (Saudi-led coalition, 2015–present):** UNHCR estimates 150,000+ deaths over 8 years, with ~12,000 documented civilian deaths from airstrikes (Yemen Data Project). Iran's daily civilian casualty rate already exceeds Yemen's sustained average.

The combination of strikes across 24 provinces simultaneously, hitting 163 cities, makes this the most geographically dispersed aerial campaign since the 2003 Iraq invasion.

## 2. Infrastructure Destruction

### Healthcare Infrastructure

Beyond the 13 confirmed hospital/health facility strikes, the second-order effects on healthcare are devastating:

- Hospitals near military targets are being preemptively evacuated, reducing treatment capacity
- Two hospitals in Beirut were evacuated due to IDF strikes on Dahiya (Guardian, March 5)
- Iran's Health Ministry issued a defensive statement claiming "no shortage of medicine or baby formula"—suggesting shortages are imminent or already occurring (BBC Dari, EcoIran)
- WHO operations at its **global emergency logistics hub in Dubai are suspended** due to insecurity and airspace closures (WHO briefing, March 5)

The Dubai hub processed 500+ emergency orders for 75 countries in 2025. Its suspension means:
- $18 million in humanitarian health supplies inaccessible
- $8 million in shipments unable to reach the hub
- $1.6 million in **polio laboratory supplies** held up, threatening eradication programs in Afghanistan and Pakistan
- 50+ emergency supply requests from 25 countries stalled
- $6 million in medicines destined for Gaza blocked

This is a humanitarian multiplier effect: the war is not only creating a health crisis in Iran but degrading global health infrastructure.

### Power and Energy

**Iraq's total blackout** is the most severe infrastructure impact outside Iran itself. Iraq's electricity ministry confirmed a nationwide grid collapse affecting all provinces (Al Arabiya, Times of Israel, March 4). With Iraq's population at approximately 43 million, this is the largest single-country blackout caused by a military conflict since the 2003 invasion. Iraq's grid was already fragile, importing Iranian gas for approximately 30–40% of its electricity generation. With Iran under sustained bombardment and cross-border gas supply disrupted, Iraq's grid collapsed.

Second-order effects of prolonged Iraqi blackout:
- Hospital life support and cold chain failures
- Water purification and pumping systems offline
- Sewage treatment failure risking disease outbreaks
- Telecommunications degradation
- Economic paralysis (banking, commerce, industry)

Iran's own power infrastructure damage is unquantifiable due to the internet blackout, but strikes on 163 cities across 24 provinces have almost certainly degraded electrical distribution.

### Communications

Iran has been under **near-total internet blackout for 130+ hours** as of March 5 (Netblocks; state file). This affects 90+ million people. The blackout was imposed by Iranian authorities on February 28—the same day the war began—replicating tactics used during the January 2026 protest crackdowns.

The communications blackout:
- Prevents independent verification of casualties and damage
- Cuts civilians off from emergency information and coordination
- Disrupts family reunification for displaced populations
- Blocks international humanitarian organizations from assessing needs
- Enables both Iranian authorities and attacking forces to control narratives

Amnesty International specifically noted that the shutdown prevents "millions of people from accessing essential communication and life-saving information" (Amnesty, March 5).

### AWS Data Centers and Digital Infrastructure

Iranian drone and missile strikes damaged **three Amazon Web Services data centers** in the UAE and Bahrain (Reuters, BBC, March 2). The ME-CENTRAL-1 region experienced prolonged outages. AWS described recovery as "prolonged" (Business Insider).

This represents a novel category of civilian infrastructure damage: cloud computing facilities serving banking, healthcare, government services, and commercial operations across the Gulf and beyond. The incident demonstrated that modern digital infrastructure—relied upon by hospitals, financial systems, and emergency services—is vulnerable to the physical effects of war.

### Education

Beyond the Minab school strike, Iran's Education Ministry activated the "Shad" online learning platform for virtual schooling (EcoIran). However, with the internet blackout in its sixth day, the platform is inaccessible to most students. An entire generation of Iranian students is out of school with no viable alternative.

### Cultural Heritage

Iran's MFA alleged strikes on 33 civilian sites including the **Tehran Grand Bazaar and the Golestan Palace complex**—a UNESCO World Heritage Site (Al Jazeera, March 5). These claims have not been independently verified, but if confirmed, would constitute violations of the 1954 Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property in the Event of Armed Conflict.

## 3. Displacement and Refugees

### Internal Displacement in Iran

UNHCR reported that approximately **100,000 people fled Tehran in the first two days** following attacks (UNHCR press release, March 5). The Tehran Governor's warning against "panic buying" indicates a population in flight mode (BBC Dari). Destination cities and absorption capacity are unknown due to communications blackout.

Iran hosts **1.65 million refugees**, mostly Afghans (UNHCR). These populations are among the most vulnerable to displacement-within-displacement. UNHCR has emergency supplies pre-positioned in Termez, Uzbekistan, "strategically located near Iran" (UNHCR briefing).

### Regional Displacement

UNHCR's comprehensive figure: **over 330,000 people displaced by recent hostilities** across the region (UNHCR, March 5). This includes:

- **Lebanon:** 83,000+ displaced internally; 38,000 crossed into Syria; 500,000 ordered to evacuate Dahiya alone (March 5). Lebanese death toll: 123 dead, 683 wounded. Schools converted to shelters. UNHCR reported 11,000 crossed into Syria on a single day—20× the daily average (InfoMigrants).
- **Iran internal:** 100,000+ from Tehran; total unknown
- **Gulf residents fleeing:** Tens of thousands of expatriates stranded

### Stranded Travelers

Reuters reported **roughly 30,000 people stranded in the Gulf region** as of March 3, with governments scrambling to organize repatriation flights (Reuters, March 3–4). The Czech Republic, UK, and others organized evacuation flights from Israel, Oman, and the UAE (NYT, March 3). PBS documented individual stories of travelers trapped as Gulf hub airports—Dubai, Doha, Abu Dhabi—saw massive flight cancellations (PBS, March 5).

**23,000 Indian sailors** are stranded near the Strait of Hormuz (NDTV), with 37 Indian-flagged ships unable to transit.

### Historical Comparison

- **Iraq 2003:** ~1 million displaced in the first month
- **Syria 2011–present:** 13.5 million displaced (peak)
- **Yemen 2015–present:** 4.5 million displaced
- **Current trajectory:** If the war continues for months as US officials suggest (Politico: possibly until September), displacement could reach millions, particularly if ground operations commence or nuclear facilities are further damaged.

## 4. International Humanitarian Law Assessment

### Potential Violations by US/Israeli Forces

**a) Proportionality — Minab School Strike**
The strike on Shajareh Tayyebeh school killed 175, predominantly children. Even if the intended target was the nearby IRGC compound (as Amnesty's video analysis suggests), the principle of proportionality under Article 51(5)(b) of Additional Protocol I requires that anticipated civilian casualties not be "excessive in relation to the concrete and direct military advantage anticipated." The death of 175 schoolchildren—whatever the military target—would face intense scrutiny from any future tribunal. The UN has already called it a "grave violation of humanitarian law."

**b) Hospital Protections**
Under Articles 18–23 of the Fourth Geneva Convention and Article 12 of Additional Protocol I, hospitals are protected. The WHO has verified 13 attacks on healthcare in Iran. While some may constitute collateral damage from strikes on adjacent targets (as with Gandhi Hospital), the pattern and scale of hospital damage raises questions about precautionary measures under Article 57 of Additional Protocol I. Were effective advance warnings given? Were strikes on targets near hospitals proportionate?

**c) Infrastructure Targeting**
Strikes on 33 alleged civilian sites—including bazaars, cultural heritage sites, and residential areas (Iran MFA claim, via Al Jazeera)—would, if verified, raise questions under Article 52 of Additional Protocol I, which limits attacks to military objectives. The destruction of the Azadi Stadium, a civilian landmark, also warrants examination.

**d) Internet/Communications Destruction**
While the internet blackout was imposed by Iran itself, US/Israeli strikes on communications towers (including the tower adjacent to Gandhi Hospital) contribute to information deprivation that endangers civilians.

### Potential Violations by Iranian Forces

**a) Indiscriminate Attacks on Gulf Civilian Areas**
Iran has launched **500+ ballistic missiles and 2,000+ drones** at targets across the Gulf since February 28, with the UAE alone absorbing 1,000+ attacks (UAE official statement). These attacks have killed civilians in countries that are not parties to the conflict:
- UAE: 3+ dead, 94+ wounded
- Kuwait: 3 dead (including an 11-year-old Iranian child), 32+ wounded
- Bahrain: 1 dead, 6 wounded
- Qatar: 0 dead, 16 wounded

Under Article 51(4) of Additional Protocol I, indiscriminate attacks—those not directed at a specific military objective—are prohibited. Iran's mass missile and drone salvos against Gulf population centers, targeting countries Iran itself claims "did not participate" in the war, constitute a prima facie case for indiscriminate or disproportionate attack.

**b) Attacks on Civilian Shipping**
Iran's strikes on merchant vessels in the Strait of Hormuz have killed at least 2 Indian sailors (on tanker *Skylight*) and endangered ~200 ships at anchor. Attacks on neutral merchant shipping violate the San Remo Manual on International Law Applicable to Armed Conflicts at Sea.

**c) Use of Civilian Facilities for Military Purposes**
Hengaw documented IRGC units relocating to **schools, dormitories, and mosques** (Hengaw, March 4). Under Article 51(7) of Additional Protocol I, parties are prohibited from using the presence of civilians to shield military objectives. This practice, if widespread, constitutes a violation by Iran and simultaneously complicates the legal analysis of US/Israeli strikes that hit such facilities.

**d) Failure to Evacuate Civilians**
Hengaw specifically reports that "the Islamic Republic of Iran has not evacuated residential areas located near military or security installations. Instead, civilians have been forced to organize their own evacuations" (March 4). Article 58 of Additional Protocol I requires parties to take precautions to protect civilians under their control from the effects of attacks.

### What a War Crimes Tribunal Would Examine

A future International Criminal Court or ad hoc tribunal would likely focus on:

1. **Minab school strike** — intent, target selection, proportionality analysis, precautionary measures
2. **Hospital strikes** — the Gandhi Hospital incident and 12+ others; whether military advantage justified civilian harm
3. **Iran's Gulf bombardment** — indiscriminate attacks on non-belligerent states' civilian populations
4. **IRGC military use of civilian facilities** — schools, mosques, dormitories
5. **Communications blackout** — both Iran's internet shutdown and strikes on communications infrastructure
6. **Failure to facilitate humanitarian access** — all parties
7. **Collective punishment** — Iraq's blackout, Gulf shipping disruption, Hormuz closure affecting neutral states

The International Federation for Human Rights (FIDH) stated on March 2: "All alleged violations—including indiscriminate or disproportionate attacks, deliberate targeting of civilians or civilian infrastructure, and attacks on medical facilities and schools—must be promptly, independently, and transparently investigated" (USA Today, March 3).

## 5. Humanitarian Access

### Current Status: Severely Degraded

Humanitarian access to Iran is effectively **near-zero** for international organizations:

- **Communications blackout** (130+ hours) prevents needs assessment
- **Airspace closures** across the region block humanitarian flights
- **IAEA blocked** from nuclear sites—Natanz entrance damaged (IAEA confirmation)
- **WHO Dubai logistics hub suspended** — the single largest humanitarian supply node for the region
- **Maersk suspended all Gulf bookings** — commercial shipping that normally carries humanitarian cargo is halted
- **~200 ships at anchor** in the Persian Gulf, unable to transit Hormuz

The ICRC president Mirjana Spoljaric warned: "Widening hostilities across the Middle East are putting civilian lives in grave danger. The scale of major military operations flaring across the Middle East risks embroiling the region—and beyond—into another large-scale armed conflict that will overwhelm any humanitarian response" (CBS News, March 2).

### Iranian Red Crescent

The Iranian Red Crescent Society (IRCS) is the primary domestic humanitarian actor. It reported 787 dead as of March 3 (Amnesty International). However, IRCS capacity is constrained by:
- The same communications blackout affecting all organizations
- Infrastructure damage to its own facilities (Iran MFA claims Red Crescent sites struck)
- Fuel and supply chain disruptions
- Security risks from ongoing strikes across 24 provinces

### OCHA Response

Tom Fletcher, UN Under-Secretary-General for Humanitarian Affairs, stated: "The humanitarian fallout from the escalation of violence in the Middle East is increasingly daunting." OCHA is "scaling up operations where necessary and possible, activating contingency plans across Iran and the region, including Lebanon, the occupied Palestinian territory, Syria and Yemen" (The Star, March 4).

### Humanitarian Corridors

No humanitarian corridors have been established. No ceasefire negotiations are underway—Iran explicitly states it is "not asking for a ceasefire" (Araghchi, NBC, March 5). Trump has said "Too Late!" to Iranian outreach. With both sides signaling escalation, the prospect for humanitarian pauses is near zero.

## 6. Gulf Civilian Impact

### UAE

The UAE has absorbed the heaviest Gulf state impact: **1,000+ Iranian attacks** (UAE official), comprising 689 drones (645 intercepted, 44 impacting) and multiple ballistic missiles (7 detected, 6 intercepted per CBS). Cumulative: 3+ dead, 94+ wounded, with the toll rising as interceptor debris falls on populated areas.

Key impacts:
- **AWS data centers damaged** — three facilities across UAE and Bahrain, disrupting cloud services for the region and beyond
- **DP World suspended Jebel Ali** — the world's 9th-largest container port
- **US Consulate Dubai hit by drone** (March 3)
- **Dubai banking systems disrupted**
- **WHO global logistics hub non-operational**
- The UAE officially stated it "did not participate in the war, did not allow use of territory" (Arabi21)—yet it has absorbed more attacks than most active belligerents in prior conflicts

### Kuwait

Three dead including two Kuwaiti troops and an 11-year-old Iranian national killed by shrapnel. 32+ wounded. US Embassy closed indefinitely (March 5). The child's death—an Iranian killed by Iranian ordnance in Kuwait—encapsulates the war's cruel absurdity.

### Bahrain

One dead, six wounded. The BAPCO state oil refinery in Ma'ameer was struck by an Iranian missile—the **first confirmed hit on Gulf oil refining infrastructure** (March 5). Fire extinguished, limited damage, still operational. One dead, six wounded. Bahrain has intercepted 74 missiles and 117 drones.

### Qatar

Zero dead, 16 wounded. Qatar shot down two Iranian fighter jets. QatarEnergy halted all LNG operations. Qatar's PM directly confronted Iran's FM Araghchi, saying Iran was "trying to hurt neighbors and drag them into a war that is not theirs" (BBC Arabic).

### Gulf Food Security

Maersk's suspension of all Gulf bookings, combined with ~200 ships anchored in the Persian Gulf and the effective closure of Hormuz to non-Chinese shipping, threatens food security for **50+ million Gulf residents**. The Gulf states import 80–90% of their food. If shipping disruption continues beyond 2–3 weeks, rationing may become necessary.

## 7. Information Environment and Civilian Protection

### Iran: Information Darkness

130+ hours of near-total internet blackout means 90+ million Iranians have effectively no access to:
- Emergency warnings about incoming strikes
- Information about safe evacuation routes
- Communication with family members
- Independent news about the conflict
- Coordination of civilian self-protection

Iran's Education Ministry activated the "Shad" virtual learning platform—which requires internet that doesn't exist (EcoIran). The Tehran Governor warned against "panic buying" via state media—the only functioning information channel.

The blackout was imposed by Iranian authorities, mirroring tactics from the January 2026 protest crackdowns. However, US/Israeli strikes have also damaged communications infrastructure (the TV tower adjacent to Gandhi Hospital; Mehrabad Airport communications).

### Misinformation and Information Operations

The information environment is saturated with competing narratives:

- **Iran's false flag claims:** Iran's MFA accuses the US and Israel of striking Gulf states with "suicide drones resembling Iranian ones" to frame Iran (RT Arabic, March 5). This claim has been expanded from just Azerbaijan to all Gulf states.
- **Saudi counter-disinformation:** Saudi state TV explicitly warned viewers about "a disinformation campaign to drag Gulf states into war with Iran" (BBC Persian).
- **Conflicting Iranian positions on Hormuz:** Three simultaneous narratives—IRGC says closed; Khatam al-Anbiya deputy says "not blocked"; Chinese ships pass freely.
- **Casualty figure manipulation:** All parties have incentives to inflate or deflate numbers.

For civilians trying to make life-or-death decisions about whether to flee, where to shelter, and who to trust, this information chaos is itself a threat to survival.

## 8. Long-Term Humanitarian Forecast

### Reconstruction

Based on the geographic scope (24 provinces, 163 cities) and intensity (2,500 IDF strikes alone, 6,000+ munitions), reconstruction costs will likely exceed **$100 billion** over a decade—comparable to Iraq's post-2003 reconstruction. Key sectors requiring rebuilding:
- Healthcare infrastructure (13+ facilities damaged)
- Power grid and energy distribution
- Communications networks
- Transportation (Mehrabad Airport struck; multiple military airports destroyed)
- Housing in areas adjacent to military targets
- Educational facilities

### Environmental Damage

**Oil and petrochemical contamination:**
- BAPCO refinery strike in Bahrain caused fires (contained)
- Ras Tanura closure since Monday; Saudi energy infrastructure under repeated attack
- ~200 ships at anchor in the Gulf create spill risk
- Iran's oil infrastructure damage is unknown but likely significant

**Nuclear contamination risk:**
- Natanz entrance damaged (IAEA confirmed)
- Parchin struck at nightfall, March 5 (BBC Persian)
- IDF claims secret nuclear facility destroyed; Witkoff: "material for 11 bombs"
- WHO Director-General Ghebreyesus specifically warned about "the threat of nuclear facilities being affected" with "potential for serious public health consequences" (WHO briefing, March 5)

If enriched uranium or other radioactive materials were dispersed by strikes on nuclear facilities, the public health consequences could persist for decades, affecting groundwater, agriculture, and cancer rates across a wide area. The IAEA's inability to access sites makes independent radiological assessment impossible.

### Public Health

Beyond immediate casualties, the war's public health toll will include:
- **Disrupted vaccination programs** — including $1.6M in polio supplies stuck at the Dubai hub
- **Chronic disease management collapse** — Iran's Health Ministry claims no shortages, but defensive messaging suggests otherwise
- **Waterborne disease risk** — from power grid failures affecting water treatment (especially in Iraq)
- **Malnutrition** — Gulf food supply chain disruption; Maersk suspension
- **Infectious disease** — displacement and shelter overcrowding create transmission environments

### Mental Health

The psychological impact of sustained bombardment on 90 million Iranians—combined with 130+ hours of communications blackout, economic panic, and an uncertain political future after Khamenei's assassination—is incalculable. Tehran residents told BBC Arabic: "Each day feels like a month." An Iranian intellectual published in state media: "This war may lead to millions dead"—unprecedented public pessimism permitted by Iranian censors (BBC Dari).

In Lebanon, 500,000 Dahiya residents were ordered to evacuate simultaneously, creating scenes of "traffic at standstill, thousands walking, families pushing strollers through gridlock, appeals for rescue services for elderly unable to leave" (Guardian, March 5). Finance Minister Smotrich's threat that "Dahiya will look like Khan Younis" compounds the trauma.

Historical research on sustained aerial bombardment—from the London Blitz to the bombing of Baghdad in 2003 to Gaza in 2023–2026—consistently shows elevated rates of PTSD, depression, anxiety disorders, and developmental delays in children lasting decades after the bombing ends.

### War Duration and Humanitarian Implications

Multiple sources now indicate the war may last far longer than initially suggested. Politico reports the US is preparing for operations potentially lasting until September 2026 (Focus.ua, UDN Taiwan, citing Politico). Trump himself said the US is prepared to "go far longer" than 4–5 weeks. IDF Chief Zamir announced a "next phase" targeting underground sites and "regime foundations."

If the conflict extends for months:
- Civilian deaths could reach five figures
- Displacement could reach millions
- Iran's healthcare system could collapse entirely
- Iraq's blackout could trigger a humanitarian emergency in its own right
- Gulf food security could become critical
- Environmental damage from nuclear facility strikes could become irreversible

## Conclusions and Recommendations

Six days into this war, the humanitarian community faces a crisis it cannot adequately respond to. The WHO's own logistics hub is disabled. Communications blackouts prevent needs assessment. No humanitarian corridors exist. Both sides are signaling escalation, not de-escalation.

**The following actions are urgently needed:**

1. **Immediate humanitarian pause** to allow assessment, medical evacuation, and civilian protection—even if a ceasefire is not achievable
2. **Restoration of internet access in Iran** — the blackout is itself a humanitarian violation, imposed by Iranian authorities
3. **Establishment of humanitarian corridors** for medical supplies and evacuation of wounded
4. **Independent investigation** of the Minab school strike and all hospital attacks
5. **IAEA access to nuclear facilities** to assess radiological risk
6. **Protection of Gulf shipping lanes** for humanitarian cargo
7. **Cessation of IRGC use of civilian facilities** — schools, mosques, dormitories
8. **International radiological monitoring** of Natanz, Parchin, and other struck nuclear sites
9. **Emergency food supply coordination** for Gulf states dependent on maritime imports
10. **Mental health surge planning** for the inevitable post-conflict psychological crisis

The trajectory of this conflict—expanding geographically, escalating in intensity, with no diplomatic off-ramp in sight—points toward a humanitarian catastrophe that will define the region for a generation.
