## I. THE CONFLICT IN CONTEXT

### What Kind of War Is This?

Operation Epic Fury defies easy categorization. It is not a ground invasion (Iraq 2003), not a humanitarian intervention (Libya 2011), not a punitive strike (Syria 2017-2018), and not a response to an attack on American soil (Afghanistan 2001). It is, in its design, something genuinely novel: a **preemptive decapitation-degradation campaign** that seeks to eliminate a state's leadership, military capacity, and nuclear infrastructure simultaneously through standoff precision strikes — without ground forces, without UN authorization, without congressional approval, and without majority public support.

The closest historical parallels illuminate by their differences. The 2003 Iraq invasion also featured a decapitation attempt (the failed strike on Dora Farm) and "shock and awe" air campaign, but it was accompanied by a 150,000-strong ground invasion, congressional authorization, UN inspections (however manipulated), and 76% initial public support. Operation Epic Fury has achieved more military destruction in six days than the opening phase of Iraq achieved in three weeks — but with none of the political or legal foundations.

The 1991 Gulf War offers a better structural parallel: coalition air superiority followed by systematic degradation of an adversary's military capacity, with the regime surviving in weakened form. But Desert Storm was authorized by the UN Security Council, supported by a 34-nation coalition, and responded to Iraq's invasion of Kuwait — a clear casus belli that unified international opinion. Epic Fury has none of these.

The June 2025 twelve-day Israel-Iran war is the most direct predecessor. That conflict saw Israeli strikes on Iranian nuclear and missile facilities, but Iran gave advance warning before retaliatory launches — "a signal calibrated to facilitate de-escalation." The current conflict is qualitatively different: Iran's retaliation has been maximalist, not signaling. Both sides jumped to near-maximum conventional intensity within hours, skipping multiple rungs of the escalation ladder.

### What Makes It Novel

Three features distinguish this war from all precedents.

**First, the information environment.** Iraq 2003 was fought in an era of embedded journalists and cable news patriotism. The Iran war unfolds in real-time OSINT, with BBC Verify contradicting CENTCOM's ship-sinking claims within hours (11 confirmed vs. 30+ claimed), social media broadcasting unfiltered footage from Tehran, and AI-generated content complicating verification. The administration cannot manufacture consent because the information architecture that enabled consent-manufacturing no longer exists. This is why 25-27% approval is not a temporary condition — it reflects the structural impossibility of narrative control in the 2026 media environment.

**Second, the technology.** This is the first war in which AI has been broadly integrated into operational military planning, the first in which hyperscale data centers were destroyed as military targets, the first to feature an F-35 air-to-air kill, and the first to test layered missile defense against a peer-level ballistic arsenal at scale. The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute over AI weapons use — in which the president ordered federal agencies to phase out the technology of a company that drew ethical lines on military applications — previews a civil-military technology debate that will define the next generation of warfare.

**Third, the economics.** No prior conflict has simultaneously disrupted three energy chokepoints (Hormuz, Red Sea threat, Qatar LNG). The selective nature of the Hormuz closure — Chinese ships passing safely, Western shipping frozen — represents a new model of economic warfare that weaponizes a global commons on a discriminatory basis, creating a two-tier energy market that advantages America's primary strategic competitor.

### The Speed Problem

Perhaps the most genuinely novel characteristic is the velocity of escalation. In six days, this campaign has killed a head of state, opened two fronts, involved a dozen countries, disrupted 5-6% of global oil supply, created a triple chokepoint crisis, triggered European military deployments, produced over 1,500 confirmed fatalities across multiple countries, destroyed cloud infrastructure serving millions of users, and pushed gold to an all-time high. The pre-war assumption — shared by most analysts — that escalation would follow a graduated ladder has been invalidated. The escalation ladder was not climbed; it was thrown out the window.

This velocity is itself a product of the technologies involved. Precision-guided munitions enable simultaneous strikes across an entire country. Pre-delegated launch authority enables retaliation without surviving leadership. Low-cost drones enable mass attacks that overwhelm defenses through volume rather than sophistication. Social media broadcasts consequences instantly. Financial markets reprice within seconds. Everything that once provided friction — the time to mobilize, to assess, to negotiate, to absorb — has been compressed toward zero.

The result is a conflict that has generated more strategic consequences in less than a week than most wars produce in months.

## II. THE STRATEGIC EQUATION

### The Coalition's Logic

The US-Israeli campaign rests on a strategic logic that is elegant in theory and increasingly strained in practice. The military design is a simultaneous decapitation-degradation strike that addresses the central operational challenge identified by ISW-CTP: **destroying Iran's ballistic missile capabilities before the force depletes its interceptor stockpiles.** This is the launcher-vs-interceptor equation — the defining arithmetic of this war.

By Day 6, the math favors the coalition. Iranian ballistic missile launches are down 90%. Sixty percent of launchers have been destroyed. Eighty percent of air defenses are neutralized. The IAF has achieved near-complete aerial superiority over Iran. The Iranian navy has ceased to exist as a conventional force. The supreme leader is dead, along with 49+ senior officials.

But strategic logic and military arithmetic are not the same thing. The campaign's objectives have expanded from counterproliferation to regime change in six days — Trump's demand to be "involved in the appointment" of Iran's next leader transforms a defensive framing into the most overt regime-change language since Iraq 2003. This expansion of aims mirrors Iraq's mission creep, compressed from months into days.

The coalition structure reveals the political fragility beneath the military success. It is a hub-and-spoke model: the US-Israel bilateral axis at the center, the UK providing offensive basing (crossing from defensive to active participation), France offering base access with non-offensive "guarantees" (a legal fiction), Spain refusing US bases while deploying a frigate to Cyprus, and the Gulf states condemning Iran's attacks without endorsing US operations. This is not a coalition of the willing; it is a coalition of the grudging.

### Iran's Logic

Iran's strategic calculus is not to win militarily — it cannot. Its theory of victory is survival. As Foreign Minister Araghchi's "quagmire" framing signals, Iran's strategy mirrors Saddam Hussein's in 1991: absorb the bombardment, maintain regime coherence, and claim victory by continuing to exist.

Three elements support this strategy. First, the pre-delegated authority structure: the IRGC's 32 provincial units were designed for exactly this scenario, and the continued pace of retaliatory fire (21 communiqués through Day 6) demonstrates that decapitation has degraded but not destroyed Iran's capacity to fight. Second, the distributed retaliation across multiple axes: by striking six countries simultaneously, Iran forces the coalition to defend everywhere while attacking nowhere on the ground. Third, the Hormuz card: the selective closure imposes global economic costs that create pressure for ceasefire from actors the coalition depends on.

But Iran's strategic position is deteriorating rapidly. The shift from ballistic missiles to drones signals C2 disruption and inventory depletion. The deployment of Khorramshahr-4 strategic reserves is a sign of desperation. The navy is gone. The much-theorized "unity of fronts" has partially failed — Houthis have produced zero kinetic action despite rhetoric, and Hezbollah's activation drew a devastating Israeli response that Lebanon's own government has now tried to shut down.

The most revealing indicator: Iran contacted the CIA via a third country on March 1, one day after the strikes began. Publicly, Tehran denies all contact and insists it is "not asking for a ceasefire." Privately, it is already seeking off-ramps. This dual-track approach mirrors Iran's pattern during the Iran-Iraq War, when the Islamic Republic publicly insisted on war to the end while quietly exploring ceasefire terms for years before accepting UN Resolution 598 in 1988.

### The Geopolitical Chessboard

The battlefield dynamics are inseparable from the diplomatic picture. The war has produced a three-way great power reconfiguration.

**Russia is humiliated.** Khamenei is the third Kremlin-backed autocrat to fall in fifteen months, after Assad and Maduro. Iran's foreign ministry claims to be receiving "political and other help" from Russia; the same day, the Kremlin stated Iran had requested no assistance. Moscow lacks the will and capacity to defend its alleged partners. As Chatham House assessed: "If Iran becomes consumed by war, Russia faces a sequential attrition of strategic depth." The multipolar order Russia has championed is dissolving.

**China is the quiet beneficiary.** Chinese ships transit Hormuz safely. Chinese SOE executives negotiated exemptions directly with Iran. Beijing gains leverage over energy markets, demonstrates that US military adventurism creates global disruption, and positions itself as the responsible alternative — all without firing a shot or losing a sailor. The question, as the Atlantic Council's Tuvia Gering frames it, is whether China is "too blinkered by great-power ideology to recognize that opportunity."

**Europe is fracturing.** The EU simultaneously condemns Iran and calls for "dialogue and diplomacy." NATO's Rutte discusses "possible use of articles on collective defense" while Hegseth denies Article 5 applicability for Turkey. The UK has crossed into de facto co-belligerency through Akrotiri basing. France maintains the fiction of non-offensive base access. The European military deployments are individually modest but collectively represent the largest European mobilization in the Middle East since Iraq 2003.

### The Gulf States: From Hedging to Choosing

For a decade, the Gulf monarchies pursued strategic hedging — security partnerships with Washington, economic ties with Beijing, rapprochement with Tehran. That strategy is dead. Iran has launched more missiles at Gulf states in four days than it fired at Israel during the entire June 2025 twelve-day war. The UAE has absorbed 1,000+ attacks. Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura terminal is closed. Bahrain's BAPCO refinery was struck. The joint US-Gulf condemnation statement formalizes what was already operationally true: the Gulf states are aligned with the US-Israeli campaign, whether or not they fire a shot.

Iran's strategy of attacking everyone in range to demonstrate the costs of conflict has backfired catastrophically, converting cautious neutrals into de facto adversaries.

## III. THE ECONOMIC WEAPON

### The Triple Chokepoint

The global economy faces its most severe energy supply shock since 1973. For the first time in history, three critical energy chokepoints are simultaneously disrupted: the Strait of Hormuz (selective closure reducing daily transits from 50+ to approximately 7), Qatar's LNG infrastructure (force majeure on 77 MTPA — 20% of global supply), and the threat to Red Sea shipping (Houthis declaring readiness but not yet acting). Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura terminal adds a fourth pressure point.

The aggregate supply loss is approximately 5-6 million barrels per day of actual production offline — comparable in volume to the 1973 and 1979 shocks. The relatively moderate price response (Brent at $84, not $120) reflects market confidence in a short war. If that assumption proves wrong, Goldman Sachs models $100/barrel oil that would shave 0.4 percentage points off global GDP and add 0.7 points to headline inflation. Bloomberg's full Hormuz closure model projects $108.

The gas shock may prove more consequential than the oil disruption. European TTF futures have surged 70%, approaching levels that triggered industrial shutdowns during the 2022 Russia-Ukraine energy crisis. Goldman estimates a one-month halt of Hormuz LNG flows would push TTF to 74 EUR/MWh; two months or more would exceed 100 EUR/MWh. Japan — 90% dependent on Middle Eastern oil via Hormuz — has approximately three weeks of LNG reserves. India, importing 40% of its oil through Hormuz, has entered "war mode."

### The China Exception

The most strategically significant economic feature of this war is not the disruption itself but its selective application. Chinese cargo ships transit Hormuz safely while Western-flagged vessels are frozen. Chinese SOE executives negotiated exemptions directly with Iran. This creates a two-tier global energy market: one in which China pays modest premiums for continued access, and one in which everyone else faces supply disruption, $423,000/day supertanker rates, and cancelled P&I insurance.

This selective enforcement serves both Iranian and Chinese interests. Iran maintains its most important economic relationship while maximizing pressure on Western economies. China gains a competitive advantage in energy costs while positioning itself as the responsible power that can negotiate with all sides. The strategic implication is profound: the Hormuz chokepoint, long theorized as a symmetric vulnerability, has been revealed as an asymmetric weapon that can be calibrated to favor specific great powers.

### Digital Infrastructure as Economic Weapon

The AWS data center strikes in the UAE and Bahrain represent a new category of economic warfare. The physical destruction of cloud infrastructure cascaded into banking and payment system disruptions, logistics failures (Jebel Ali port suspension), and the effective shutdown of the Gulf's $2 trillion "Pax Silica" AI infrastructure vision. Nvidia, Amazon, and Google closed Dubai offices. The WHO's global emergency logistics hub in Dubai is non-operational, blocking $18 million in humanitarian supplies destined for 75 countries.

The cloud strike precedent is as consequential for the global economy as the Hormuz closure. It demonstrates that the physical infrastructure underlying the digital economy — data centers, fiber routes, oceanic cable landings — is targetable and vulnerable. Every company running production workloads in potentially contested regions now faces a category of risk that did not exist in enterprise planning frameworks one week ago.

### Who Has Leverage?

The economic picture reveals a counterintuitive distribution of leverage. The United States, as a net energy exporter, benefits from higher oil prices domestically even as they create political problems through rising gasoline costs. US shale producers with sub-$50 breakeven costs are printing cash. Russia benefits from higher oil and gas prices funding its war in Ukraine. Saudi Arabia, despite physical damage, benefits from competitor elimination and enhanced pricing power once Hormuz reopens.

The losers are concentrated among US allies: Japan, South Korea, India, and European industrial economies face simultaneous energy cost surges and supply disruption. The Gulf states' diversification visions — Dubai as logistics hub, Abu Dhabi as AI capital, Qatar as LNG superpower — face credibility damage that may take years to repair. The Global South faces a triple blow of higher energy costs, higher food prices, and disrupted supply chains.

The cruel irony: the countries bearing the heaviest economic costs of this war are overwhelmingly countries that had no say in its initiation.

## IV. THE HOME FRONTS

### The Democracy Problem

The US-Iran War presents an unprecedented challenge to democratic war-making: a major military conflict launched without a precipitating attack, without congressional authorization, without UN mandate, and without majority public support. The 25-27% approval rate for the strikes is the lowest initial support for any major US military operation in recorded polling history. No rally-around-the-flag effect has materialized — Trump's overall approval sits at an all-time low of 44%. Among independents, 55% disapprove.

Five structural factors explain the absence of rally sentiment. First, no precipitating attack — every prior rally followed an attack on Americans or the culmination of a long public debate. Second, the "peace president" problem — Trump campaigned as anti-interventionist, and voters who supported him for that posture feel betrayed. Third, economic anxiety — 63% of voters are concerned about gas price impacts, and when costs are made explicit, support drops to 18%. Fourth, the post-Iraq trust deficit — preemptive war justified by nuclear program concerns echoes Iraq's WMD rationale too closely. Fifth, partisan polarization — in an era of near-total sorting, there is simply no pool of persuadable voters large enough to generate 20-30 point swings.

Congress had the opportunity to constrain the president and chose not to. The Senate rejected the War Powers Resolution 47-53; the House defeated it 212-219. But the thin margins — particularly the House's 7-vote margin — reveal limited political depth for a protracted campaign. The failed votes paradoxically strengthen the executive: the administration can now cite them as implicit congressional acquiescence. The War Powers Resolution, already moribund, may now be functionally dead.

### 175 Dead Children in the Age of OSINT

The humanitarian picture feeds directly into the political dynamics through a mechanism that did not exist during prior American wars: real-time, independently verified civilian casualty documentation that reaches domestic audiences without military filtering. The Minab school strike — 175 children killed on Day 1 — is the deadliest attack on a school since Beslan. Amnesty International verified six videos. BBC Verify provided satellite analysis. The WHO confirmed 13 hospital strikes. Hengaw documented IRGC units relocating to schools and mosques. None of this information passed through military public affairs offices.

In Iraq 2003, civilian casualties accumulated over months and were documented retrospectively. The combination of sub-30% approval and 175 dead children and 13 struck hospitals creates a political toxin for which no administration has a remedy.

The Austin shooting — in which a man wearing Iranian flag imagery killed three people at a Texas bar — immediately transformed the domestic political calculus, enabling the securitization of dissent and the conflation of opposition with disloyalty. But the shooting also validated the core antiwar claim: that the war generates blowback. The cycle of protest, radicalization, violence, and securitization that took years during Vietnam has been compressed into days.

### How Democracies Sustain Wars Their Publics Oppose

The historical answer is: they don't, but it takes time. Vietnam support dropped below 30% only after years; the 2006 Iraq midterm wave took three years of declining approval. The Iran war never had majority support. If the conflict extends through the summer — as military planners apparently expect — the November 2026 midterms will be the first electoral test. The Cook Political Report already sees "hard to find much upside for the GOP." Republican freshmen who won on cost-of-living issues now own a war that is driving energy prices higher. Democratic opponents need only quote the administration's own rhetoric: billions on a foreign war while an "affordability crisis rages at home."

The deeper question is constitutional. The failed War Powers votes establish that a president can initiate and sustain large-scale military operations without congressional consent, provided sufficient partisan loyalty exists in either chamber to block a challenge. The 60-day clock expires in late April. If the war continues — and every indication says it will — a second constitutional confrontation is inevitable. But courts have historically treated war powers disputes as political questions, and no judge is likely to enjoin an active military operation. The constitutional restraints on presidential war-making, already eroded by seven decades of practice, may have reached their terminal condition.

The UN Security Council is paralyzed. The IAEA cannot access struck nuclear facilities. International humanitarian law cannot be enforced. The question is no longer whether these norms will be violated, but whether any normative framework will replace them.

### For Nuclear Deterrence and Proliferation

The destruction of Iran's nuclear program paradoxically increases proliferation risk. The lesson every aspiring nuclear state draws: if you don't have nuclear weapons, you can be attacked with impunity; if you do, you can't. Decapitation degrades but does not immediately defeat a state with pre-delegated authority. The launcher-vs-interceptor equation favors the offense at current force ratios, but interceptor production rates cannot sustain extended conflict. The cost-exchange revolution — $50,000 drones forcing expenditure of $5-15 million interceptors at ratios of 100:1 to 300:1 — will be studied by every military planning staff worldwide. ## VII. WHAT WE DON'T KNOW

Six days into this war, the critical unknowns that would most change the picture are:

**Iran's actual remaining military capacity.** The 90% reduction in ballistic missile launches could reflect launcher destruction, ammunition depletion, C2 disruption, or a deliberate conservation strategy. The difference matters enormously: the first three indicate Iranian collapse; the fourth indicates a strategic reserve being held for a decisive moment.

**The state of Iran's nuclear material.** Witkoff claimed Iran had "material for 11 bombs." The IAEA initially assessed no nuclear installation damage, then Natanz entrance was confirmed damaged and Parchin was struck. Whether enriched material has been dispersed, destroyed, or relocated is unknown — and the answer determines whether the campaign's stated primary objective has been achieved.

**What is happening inside Iran.** The 130+ hour internet blackout creates an intelligence void. Anti-regime chanting has been reported (Basij fired on buildings in response), but the scale of domestic opposition, the coherence of the succession process, and the state of civilian infrastructure are all obscured.

**China's actual diplomatic position.** The Hormuz exemptions demonstrate operational coordination with Iran, but whether Beijing is actively mediating, passively benefiting, or preparing to intervene diplomatically remains opaque.

**The Houthi decision calculus.** Zero kinetic action from Yemen despite maximum rhetoric is the war's most significant gap between words and deeds. Whether this reflects Iranian instruction, Houthi calculation, US deterrence, or logistical constraints determines whether the conflict remains contained to its current geography.

**Interceptor stockpile status.** The exact depletion rate of THAAD, Patriot PAC-3 MSE, and other interceptors is classified. Heritage Foundation warned pre-war that high-end interceptors "would likely be exhausted within days of sustained combat." Whether this threshold has been approached is the single most operationally consequential unknown.

**The real casualty toll.** The gap between Iran's Foundation of Martyrs figure (1,230) and Hengaw's field documentation (2,400) suggests significant undercounting, particularly of military deaths. The civilian toll in areas under internet blackout may be substantially higher than any current estimate.

These unknowns are not incidental. Any one of them — if resolved in an unexpected direction — could fundamentally alter the trajectory of this conflict and its consequences.
