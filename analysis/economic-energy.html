Executive Summary

Six days into the US-Israeli military campaign against Iran, the global economy faces its most severe energy supply shock since the 1973 Arab oil embargo. The simultaneous disruption of three critical chokepoints — the Strait of Hormuz (selective closure), Qatar's LNG export infrastructure (force majeure), and Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura terminal (preemptive shutdown) — has created a compound supply crisis without modern precedent. Brent crude has surged 15.3% from $72.87 to $84/bbl; European natural gas (TTF) has spiked 70%; gold has hit an all-time high of $5,400/oz. Goldman Sachs models a tail-risk scenario of $100/bbl oil that could shave 0.4 percentage points off global GDP growth and add 0.7pp to headline inflation. The economic damage extends well beyond energy: Jebel Ali port's suspension disrupts 15.5 million TEU of annual container throughput; AWS data center destruction has cascaded across Middle Eastern digital infrastructure; and Maersk's suspension of all Gulf bookings threatens food security for 50 million people across the Gulf states. This analysis quantifies the damage across nine dimensions and models three forward scenarios.

1. Energy Market Shock

Oil Price Trajectory

Brent crude closed at $72.87/bbl on Friday, February 27 — the last pre-war trading session. By Day 6 (March 5), Brent settled at approximately $84/bbl, with WTI at $81.01 (its highest since July 2024, up 8.51% on the day alone). The price trajectory has been remarkably restrained relative to the scale of supply disruption, reflecting market confidence in a short war — what Goldman Sachs head of oil research Daan Struyven characterized as an implied $13/bbl risk premium above the $65 fair value, pricing approximately four weeks of conflict.

The restraint is fragile. Barclays analysts warned on the first weekend that "Brent could hit $100/bbl." Goldman's upside scenario models $100/bbl under sustained disruption, while Bloomberg's Hormuz blockade model projects $108/bbl under full closure. The current $84 price reflects a market betting on resolution; any signal of protracted conflict would trigger rapid repricing.

Supply losses in aggregate:

  • Iran: 3.5 mb/d crude + 0.8 mb/d condensate production effectively offline (exports already constrained by sanctions to ~1.7 mb/d crude + condensate)
  • Saudi Arabia: Ras Tanura terminal closed since March 3 — 550,000 bpd of export capacity shuttered preemptively
  • Iraq: Production cut ~1.5 mb/d amid total infrastructure blackout
  • Strait of Hormuz transit disruption: ~16 mb/d of non-pipeline oil flows at risk (after accounting for 4.2 mb/d of spare pipeline capacity per IEA estimates)

In total, approximately 5-6 mb/d of actual supply has been removed from the market through a combination of direct destruction, preemptive shutdowns, and transit disruption — roughly 5-6% of global supply of ~100 mb/d.

LNG Crisis

The natural gas shock may prove more consequential than the oil disruption. On March 2, QatarEnergy declared force majeure and halted all LNG production at the world's largest export facility after Iranian drone attacks. Qatar's 77 million tonnes per annum (MTPA) of LNG capacity represents approximately 19-20% of global LNG supply. All of it transits the Strait of Hormuz.

European benchmark Dutch TTF futures surged 70% from their February 27 close of ~31.6 EUR/MWh. Bloomberg reported intraday spikes of up to 54% on the day of the Qatar shutdown alone. European gas prices reached their highest levels since the 2023 energy crisis. Goldman Sachs Research estimates that:

  • A one-month full halt of Hormuz LNG flows could push TTF to 74 EUR/MWh
  • A disruption lasting two months or more would likely lift TTF above 100 EUR/MWh

European gas storage levels are at historically low levels entering March — a structural vulnerability that Reuters flagged as potentially "quickly becoming a major liability for utilities and industry." Japan, which imports over 90% of its crude oil from the Middle East via Hormuz, has seen companies formally request government release of strategic oil reserves. One Japanese oil company has already cancelled March gasoline, jet fuel, and diesel exports to prioritize domestic supply.

2. Strait of Hormuz: Quantifying the Chokepoint Crisis

The De Facto Closure

The Strait of Hormuz carries approximately 21% of global petroleum liquids and 25% of global LNG. Pre-war, 50+ vessel transits occurred daily. By Day 6, daily transits have dropped to approximately 7 — primarily Iranian and Chinese-flagged vessels operating under negotiated exemptions.

The closure is not military in the traditional sense. It is an insurance-driven closure. As one analyst noted, "the insurance market has not withdrawn because every vessel will be attacked. It has withdrawn because the cost of verifying whether any particular vessel will survive any particular transit now exceeds the value of the information." Protection & Indemnity (P&I) clubs — the mutual insurers covering 90% of global shipping — have cancelled war risk coverage for Gulf transits. Without P&I coverage, no commercially insured vessel can legally transit.

Key metrics:

  • ~200 ships anchored in the Persian Gulf (up from 150+ earlier in the week)
  • P&I war risk coverage: cancelled across major clubs
  • VLCC (supertanker) rates: $423,000/day — up 94% and an all-time record
  • War risk insurance for a $100M VLCC: surged to ~$400,000 per single voyage (from ~$250,000 pre-conflict)
  • Maersk: suspended all Gulf bookings
  • At least 3 tankers damaged, multiple seafarers killed (2 Indians on tanker Skylight near Oman)
  • 23,000 Indian sailors stranded near the Strait

The China Exception

A critical nuance: Chinese cargo ships are passing through Hormuz safely. Chinese state-owned enterprise (SOE) executives told the Wall Street Journal that China negotiated exemptions directly with Iran — requesting that Iran not attack tankers or LNG carriers, and not strike Qatar. An Iranian Khatam al-Anbiya deputy explicitly stated: "We have NOT blocked Hormuz" — contradicting IRGC claims of closure. The selective enforcement serves Iran's strategic interest in maintaining its most important economic relationship while maximizing pressure on Western economies.

Persistence Scenarios

According to Kpler's analysis of seaborne export flows, the volumes at risk through Hormuz by commodity class include:

  • Crude/Condensate: 13.37 mb/d via Strait (30.7% of global seaborne crude)
  • Jet fuel/Kerosene: 378 kb/d via Strait (19.4% of global, but 38.9% of European supply)
  • Gasoline/Naphtha: 1.29 mb/d (16% of global)
  • LPG: 85% of India's supply transits Hormuz

If Hormuz remains effectively closed for:

  • 2 weeks: Strategic petroleum reserves (SPR) and pipeline diversions contain damage; oil at $85-90
  • 1 month: Goldman estimates Brent +$15/bbl; TTF approaches 74 EUR/MWh; Asian inflation +0.7pp
  • 3 months: SPR drawdowns become unsustainable; TTF exceeds 100 EUR/MWh; global recession risk materializes
  • 6 months: Structural economic damage; permanent rerouting of trade flows; $120+ oil

3. Global Financial Markets

Equity Markets

The market reaction has been notable for its relative restraint — or, as CNN Business framed it, stocks are "acting weird" about a spiraling war. On the first trading day (Monday, March 3), the Dow plunged 1,200 points at the open before recovering to close down just 400 points (0.8%). The S&P 500 fell 0.9%; Nasdaq dropped 1.0%. By Thursday, March 5, equities fell again as oil surged 8.51%, with the Russell 2000 small-cap index dropping 2.7% — reflecting the disproportionate vulnerability of smaller, domestically-oriented firms to energy cost shocks.

The modest selloff reflects a familiar pattern: markets are pricing the most likely outcome (short war, limited sustained disruption) rather than the tail risk (protracted conflict, Hormuz closure). As Yardeni Research noted: "We wouldn't be surprised if any selloff in the S&P 500 on Monday morning turns into a rally, driven by expectations of lower oil prices once the latest Middle East war ends." Jefferies economist Mohit Kumar took the opposite view: "We see further downside in the coming days."

Sectoral divergence:

  • Energy: US energy stocks rallied sharply; shale producers are the clearest domestic beneficiaries
  • Defense: Lockheed Martin, Raytheon (RTX), Northrop Grumman, and L3Harris all rose
  • Airlines/Transport: Sold off on jet fuel exposure (jet fuel prices up 72% per Indian market data)
  • Tech: Mixed; AWS disruption created targeted selling in cloud-dependent names
  • Small caps: Russell 2000 hit hardest, reflecting vulnerability to energy input costs

Gold and Safe Havens

Gold hit an all-time high of $5,400/oz — a landmark that reflects not just war premium but the cumulative effect of dedollarization trends, central bank accumulation, and inflation hedging demand since 2023. By March 5, gold had pulled back modestly to ~$5,080 as some traders took profits.

Currency Markets

  • Iranian rial: Frozen at approximately 101,650 toman per dollar on the parallel market — remarkably stable given the circumstances, likely reflecting internet blackout suppressing market activity and the Central Bank of Iran's aggressive intervention
  • US dollar: Strengthened on safe-haven flows, with the dollar index climbing as investors fled risk assets
  • Gulf currencies: Pegs holding but under stress; UAE dirham and Saudi riyal pegs backed by massive reserves
  • Emerging market currencies: Oil-importing EM currencies under pressure; Indian rupee, Turkish lira, and South African rand weakening

Credit Markets

Government bonds sold off on Thursday, March 5, as the "stagflationary" nature of the oil shock — simultaneously raising inflation and slowing growth — creates a policy dilemma for central banks. Goldman Sachs notes that central banks "historically have not reacted directly to oil shocks, but tend to tighten policy modestly when inflation is elevated or price shocks are large." Under the baseline, global monetary policy is "mostly unaffected"; under $100 oil, policy turns "more hawkish — potentially through a delay in rate cuts in emerging markets."

Comparison to Prior Geopolitical Shocks

The 2026 equity selloff has been milder than the 2020 pandemic crash (-34% peak-to-trough), the 2022 Russia-Ukraine initial reaction (-13% S&P 500 peak-to-trough over weeks), or even the 2024 Yen carry-trade unwind (-8.5% Nikkei in one day). Markets appear to be applying the post-2001 heuristic: geopolitical shocks produce buying opportunities, not structural bear markets. This assumption will be tested if Hormuz closure persists.

4. Supply Chain Cascades

Jebel Ali Port

Dubai's Jebel Ali port — the world's ninth-largest container port handling 15.5 million TEU annually — suspended operations after drone debris caused fires on March 1. DP World, its operator, has not provided a reopening timeline. Jebel Ali is the logistics hub for re-export trade across the Middle East, East Africa, and South Asia. Its closure disrupts:

  • Re-export trade: Dubai handles approximately $150 billion in annual re-exports
  • Food imports: The Gulf states import 80-90% of food requirements; Maersk's suspension of all Gulf bookings puts an estimated 50+ million people at food security risk
  • Manufacturing supply chains: Jebel Ali Free Zone hosts 8,700+ companies from 100+ countries

AWS Data Center Destruction

Iranian drone strikes physically damaged AWS availability zone mec1-az2 in the UAE, taking it offline. A second availability zone in Bahrain experienced a "localized power issue." The cascade was immediate:

  • Banking and payments services disrupted across the UAE
  • Delivery platform Careem, payments companies Alaan and Hubpay, and Snowflake all reported outages
  • Amazon recommended customers "enact their disaster recovery plans" and recover from remote backups into alternate regions
  • Nvidia, Amazon, and Google temporarily closed Dubai offices
  • Google employees stranded in Dubai

The AWS disruption exposed a structural vulnerability: the Gulf's $2 trillion "Pax Silica" AI infrastructure vision — with massive data center investments by AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, and Oracle — now faces what Rest of World called "a reality check." Chinese tech firms (Alibaba, Huawei, Tencent) with Gulf data centers have not reported disruptions, but the precedent is set.

Most Exposed Industries

  • Aviation: 25-30% of European jet fuel supply originates from or transits the Strait (Kpler data). Airlines face both fuel cost surges (up 72%) and route disruptions.
  • Petrochemicals: Asian petrochemical producers dependent on Gulf naphtha feedstock face production cuts.
  • Shipping/Logistics: Beyond Hormuz, the Red Sea remains under Houthi threat (leader declared readiness to act "at any moment"), creating a dual-chokepoint crisis for Europe-Asia trade.
  • Cloud computing/Digital services: Any Middle East-hosted workload is now at physical risk.
  • Food/Agriculture: Gulf food imports dependent on Jebel Ali; South Asian fertilizer supplies dependent on Gulf LPG.

    • Rerouting Timeline

    Historical precedent from the Red Sea/Houthi crisis (2023-2024) suggests commercial rerouting takes 2-4 weeks to establish alternative patterns and 2-3 months before freight rates stabilize. However, the Hormuz disruption is qualitatively different — there is no "around" route for Gulf oil exports that doesn't involve the limited pipeline alternatives (estimated at 4.2 mb/d spare capacity per IEA). For LNG, there is no pipeline alternative at all.

    5. Sanctions & Financial Warfare

    Existing Architecture

    Iran has operated under one of the most comprehensive sanctions regimes in history since 2018 (reimposed by the Trump administration after JCPOA withdrawal). Key elements include:

    • Oil exports: Sanctioned under maximum pressure; Iran's crude exports fell to under 0.5 mb/d before recovering to ~1.7 mb/d through sanctions evasion (primarily via Chinese "teapot" refineries using ship-to-ship transfers and dark fleet tankers)
    • SWIFT: Major Iranian banks excluded since 2018
    • Central Bank: Sanctioned; foreign exchange reserves largely frozen
    • Individuals/Entities: Thousands designated under OFAC

    Wartime Escalation

    The war has rendered much of the sanctions architecture moot — Iran is now under active military assault, not merely economic pressure. However, the sanctions framework will matter critically for:

  • Reconstruction: Any post-war government will need sanctions relief to rebuild
  • China/India oil flows: China was importing ~1.3-1.5 mb/d of Iranian crude via sanctions evasion. These flows are now physically disrupted by the Hormuz crisis — ironically achieving through kinetic action what sanctions failed to accomplish through economic pressure
  • Russian oil substitution: Carnegie Politika raised the question of whether Russia will replace China's lost Iranian oil supply — potentially strengthening the Russia-China energy relationship at Iran's expense

    • China and India Exposure

    India imports approximately 40% of its oil via the Strait of Hormuz. The government has entered what domestic media describes as "war mode" on energy — with two Russian oil cargoes originally heading to East Asia already diverted to Indian ports. India's LPG dependence is even more acute: 85% of supply transits Hormuz. Former NITI Aayog CEO warned that rising crude "could add billions to India's import bill."

    China's negotiated exemptions for Hormuz transit provide a significant competitive advantage, but Chinese importers still face elevated costs and uncertainty. The selective Hormuz enforcement effectively creates a two-tier global energy market: Chinese-flagged vessels transit relatively freely while Western-allied shipping is frozen.

Saudi Arabia's PIF ($930B+), Abu Dhabi's ADIA ($990B+), and Qatar's QIA ($475B+) can sustain government spending through extended disruption. However, the crisis exposes the fundamental vulnerability of Gulf economic diversification strategies: tourism, logistics, technology, and financial services all require physical security and global connectivity that the war has disrupted.

    The foreign worker dimension is critical: the UAE's population is ~90% expatriate; Qatar ~85%. Any sustained security deterioration could trigger capital flight and workforce exodus that would take years to reverse.

    7. US Economic Impact

    Gas Prices

    US retail gasoline averaged approximately $3.10/gallon pre-conflict. WTI's surge to $81.01 implies retail prices moving toward $3.50-3.70 within 2-3 weeks, with potential to exceed $4.00 if disruption persists. US Energy Secretary Wright dismissed the impact as "temporary" and a "small price" — politically tone-deaf messaging that Democratic opponents will exploit.

    Inflation

    Goldman Sachs models the baseline oil impact as adding 0.2pp to global headline inflation; the $100/bbl scenario adds 0.7pp. For the US specifically, the passthrough to core inflation is typically 0.1-0.2pp per $10/bbl increase in oil with a 3-6 month lag. The Fed faces a classic stagflationary dilemma: energy-driven inflation argues against rate cuts, while growth headwinds argue for them. Under baseline, Goldman expects "mostly unaffected" monetary policy; under the tail scenario, rate cuts could be delayed.

    Consumer Confidence

    The political economy is toxic for the administration. As Representative Jeffries framed it: billions spent on a foreign war while an "affordability crisis rages at home." Gas prices are the single most visible economic indicator for voters. DHS simultaneously shut down entering Week 3, creating a domestic security vacuum — which Graham is leveraging to push DHS funding by invoking the war: "Can we not understand America is under siege?"

    Midterm Narrative

The administration's economic narrative — already strained by tariff policy — now contends with potential $4+ gasoline, rising food prices, and a war with no clear exit timeline. War powers challenges failed in both chambers (Senate 47-53; House 212-219), giving the president full legal latitude but also full political ownership.

    8. Winners & Losers

    Winners

    US Shale Producers: The clearest beneficiaries. Every $10/bbl increase in WTI translates to approximately $30-40 billion in additional annual revenue for the US upstream sector. Permian Basin operators with sub-$50 breakeven costs are printing cash. Shale's ability to ramp production within 3-6 months provides a structural ceiling on long-term prices but a bonanza in the interim.

    Russia: Moscow benefits on multiple fronts. Higher oil prices boost government revenue at a time when war costs strain the budget. The Hormuz disruption creates opportunity to capture displaced Chinese demand — the Carnegie Politika question of whether Russia replaces Iran as China's swing supplier. Diplomatically, Russia positions itself as a responsible voice ("NATO is being drawn into war against Iran" — Lavrov) while benefiting from the chaos.

    Saudi Arabia (with caveats): Saudi Arabia absorbs significant physical damage (11 ballistic missiles, 123 drones, 3 cruise missiles intercepted; Ras Tanura closed) but may benefit long-term from Iranian competitor elimination and higher oil prices. Saudi Arabia's 2-3 mb/d of spare production capacity is the world's only meaningful swing capacity — once Hormuz reopens, Saudi pricing power increases substantially. The caveat: sustained conflict risks Saudi infrastructure and the social contract of security-for-legitimacy.

    Renewable Energy (acceleration thesis): Every oil shock since 1973 has accelerated alternative energy investment. The European gas surge to 70%+ above pre-war levels will intensify EU commitment to renewables, heat pumps, and energy efficiency. The "energy security" argument for renewables — previously abstract — is now viscerally real. However, the acceleration operates on a 2-5 year timeline, not weeks.

    Defense contractors: Raytheon (Patriot systems), Lockheed Martin (F-35s, precision munitions), Northrop Grumman (B-2/B-21 operations), and emerging players in drone/counter-drone technology all benefit from demonstrated demand and accelerated procurement.

    Losers

    Asian Oil Importers: Japan (90%+ ME oil dependence via Hormuz), South Korea, India (40% via Hormuz), and Southeast Asian economies face acute supply disruption and inflationary pressure. Japan is the most vulnerable developed economy — already requesting strategic reserve releases and cancelling fuel exports. Indian jet fuel prices are up 72%.

    European Industry: The 70% European gas price surge recreates the 2022 energy crisis dynamic. Energy-intensive industries (chemicals, steel, ceramics, glass) face margin compression or shutdown. The European recovery from the Russia-Ukraine energy shock had been fragile; this crisis may prove the breaking point for some industrial capacity that had survived the previous shock.

    Global South: Developing nations face a triple blow — higher energy costs, higher food prices (fertilizer/transport inputs), and potential food supply disruption from Gulf port closures. Countries already in debt distress (Pakistan, Egypt, Kenya) face acute fiscal pressure.

    Gulf Diversification Visions: Dubai as global logistics hub, Abu Dhabi as AI capital, Qatar as LNG superpower — all three narratives face credibility damage. The $2 trillion "Pax Silica" AI investment thesis requires physical security that has been demonstrated to be absent.

    Airlines: Global aviation faces simultaneous fuel cost surges and airspace closures. Middle Eastern carriers (Emirates, Qatar Airways, Etihad) are most directly affected.

    Insurers: Marine P&I clubs face claims from damaged/sunk vessels and cargo. The insurance market's decision to cancel war risk coverage — while rational for individual firms — has created the de facto Hormuz closure, demonstrating how insurance markets can amplify geopolitical shocks.
