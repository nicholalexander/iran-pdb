The assassination of a sitting head of state, preemptive war without UN authorization, the selective weaponization of maritime chokepoints, the first broad use of AI in combat, and the invocation of European nuclear deterrence frameworks have collectively shattered norms that took decades to build. I. International Order Impact: The Post-1945 Architecture Under Siege

The killing of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei on February 28, 2026—in the opening minutes of a joint US-Israeli military operation, before Iran's air defenses were even suppressed—represents what international law scholars are calling the most consequential violation of sovereignty norms since the 2003 Iraq invasion. As Marko Milanovic of Reading University has noted, in peacetime "it is extraordinarily rare for a country to deliberately and openly kill the leader of another sovereign nation—even during legally uncontested wars" (NYT, March 2). The closest precedent, the failed 2003 attempt to kill Saddam Hussein, occurred within a conflict that at least had congressional authorization; the current strikes proceeded under executive authority alone, with both chambers of Congress subsequently declining to constrain presidential war powers (Senate 47-53, House 212-219) (Guardian, AP, March 5).

The legal architecture is in tatters. The UN Charter's prohibition on the use of force (Article 2(4)) admits only two exceptions: self-defense under Article 51 and Security Council authorization under Chapter VII. Neither applies here. The administration's legal theory—that Iran's nuclear program and its network of proxies constituted an imminent threat requiring preemptive action—stretches the Bush-era preemption doctrine beyond any previously claimed boundary. As David Crane, former chief prosecutor of the Special Court for Sierra Leone, observed: "International law does not permit states to unilaterally decide which tyrants to remove by force" (KCRA/AP, March 2).

What makes this rupture distinct from Iraq 2003 is its success. The Bush administration failed to kill Saddam in its opening strikes; its regime change required a ground invasion and years of occupation. Operation Epic Fury achieved decapitation within hours. This efficiency makes the precedent more dangerous, not less. It demonstrates that a technologically superior state can eliminate a rival's leadership through standoff strikes alone, lowering the perceived cost of future such operations.

The Brookings Institution's Suzanne Maloney captured the historical resonance: just as the 1979 headline "Shah raft" ("the Shah is gone") marked the end of one era, "Khamenei raft" may mark the beginning of another—though "that hope may be short-lived" given the deeply embedded institutional networks of the Islamic Republic (Brookings, March 2). The regime's radical core is entrenching rather than capitulating, with Itamar Rabinovich noting that "the initial air raid, which killed Khamenei and several of his family members, inflicted a heavy blow on the regime" but "neither toppled the regime nor produced an immediate wave of popular opposition" (Brookings, March 2).

President Trump's subsequent demand to be "involved in the appointment" of Iran's next supreme leader—calling Mojtaba Khamenei "a lightweight" and drawing explicit comparisons to Venezuela (Axios, March 5)—transforms what might have been framed as a defensive operation into unambiguous regime change. This is the first time since at least the 1953 CIA-backed coup against Mosaddegh that the United States has publicly asserted a right to select Iran's leadership. The difference is that in 1953, plausible deniability was maintained for decades; in 2026, the president announced it on camera.

Assessment: The Westphalian principle of sovereign equality—already eroded by decades of humanitarian intervention, the Responsibility to Protect doctrine, and selective enforcement—has suffered what may be a fatal wound for its application to states the United States designates as adversaries. The question is no longer whether sovereignty norms will be violated, but whether any normative framework will replace them.

II. Alliance Dynamics: NATO Fracturing, Europe Mobilizing, the Gulf Aligning

The NATO Fault Line

The war has exposed a three-way split within NATO that was previously papered over by consensus on Ukraine. On one axis: the United States, operating largely unilaterally with Israeli partnership, and invoking no NATO mechanisms. On a second: the E3 (UK, France, Germany) plus the Netherlands, Italy, Spain, and Portugal, mobilizing military assets in what they frame as "defensive" postures while carefully avoiding the label of co-belligerency. On a third: Turkey, a NATO member whose territory has been physically struck by an Iranian missile—debris falling in Hatay's Dörtyol district—yet whose government has been told by US Defense Secretary Hegseth that the incident does not constitute an Article 5 trigger (Reuters, March 4; Hurriyet).

NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte has navigated this by praising the US-Israeli strikes as addressing the threat of Iran obtaining nuclear and ballistic missile capability, while simultaneously stating that "NATO itself would not be involved" (Reuters, March 2). Yet by March 5, Rutte was discussing the "possible use of articles on collective defense"—a significant shift that drew immediate condemnation from Russia's Federation Council as "blatant hypocrisy" (RT Arabic, March 5).

The European military mobilization, while framed as defensive, is substantial:

  • United Kingdom: 4 Typhoon jets deployed to Qatar; Wildcat anti-drone helicopters to Cyprus; RAF Akrotiri confirmed by Hegseth as being used for US "bomber runs"—making the UK a de facto co-belligerent (Navy Times, Sky News, March 5).
  • France: Ceded a base to the US with a "guarantee" it would not be used offensively against Iran—a legal fiction that may not survive scrutiny (El País, March 5). Macron expressed solidarity with Spain's Sánchez against Trump's trade pressure.
  • Spain: Refused US base access but independently deployed a frigate to Cyprus—threading the needle between alliance solidarity and domestic politics (NRC, March 5).
  • Netherlands: Frigate HNLMS Evertsen en route to the Mediterranean to support the French carrier Charles de Gaulle (NRC, Reformatorisch Dagblad).
  • Italy: Relocated its Tehran embassy to Baku; deployed a naval vessel to defend Cyprus (Corriere della Sera, RAI News).
  • Portugal: KC-46 tanker aircraft staging from Lajes in the Azores.

This is not NATO acting collectively. It is a coalition of the willing wearing NATO-member uniforms but operating under national command. The distinction matters legally but may not survive Iranian retaliation. As Lavrov warned: "NATO is being drawn into war against Iran" (EADaily, March 5). The Al-Monitor assessment is that Iran's targeting of Turkey is "unlikely to upend Ankara's strategic balancing act"—but a second incident could change that calculation overnight.

The Gulf Alignment

The joint US-Gulf statement of March 5—signed by Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE—"strongly condemning" Iran's "indiscriminate and reckless missile and drone attacks" represents the most unified Gulf positioning against Iran since the founding of the GCC (State.gov, March 5). Jordan's inclusion is particularly significant; Amman had maintained studied ambiguity until Iranian missiles began falling on its neighbors. The EU-GCC joint statement called separately for "dialogue and diplomacy" while terming Iran's attacks "inexcusable."

Yet this alignment is reactive, not proactive. As Spiegel noted, the Gulf monarchies now face a binary choice: remain targets while claiming neutrality, or formally become co-belligerents with legal and diplomatic consequences (Der Spiegel, March 5). The UAE's position—claiming to have endured "1,000+ attacks" while insisting it "did not participate in the war and did not allow use of territory"—is increasingly untenable (Arabi21, March 5). Iran's first successful strike on a Gulf oil refinery (BAPCO in Bahrain, March 5) suggests Tehran is willing to escalate until the distinction between neutral and belligerent becomes meaningless.

III. Great Power Competition: Beijing's Bind, Moscow's Humiliation

China: The Accidental Beneficiary

The Atlantic Council's Tuvia Gering has framed China's position as "an impossible bind": Iran is simultaneously Beijing's "most reliable anti-Western bulwark and a source of deeply discounted oil" and a liability whose actions are now directly threatening Chinese nationals and investments in the Gulf (Atlantic Council, March 5). Over 400,000 Chinese nationals reside in the UAE alone—a country that has absorbed 1,000+ Iranian attacks.

Yet the operational picture tells a different story. Chinese cargo ships are passing safely through the Strait of Hormuz while European and American-flagged vessels are targeted. Chinese SOE executives confirmed to the Wall Street Journal that Beijing negotiated exemptions directly with Iran—requesting that Tehran spare tankers, LNG carriers, and Qatari infrastructure (Sina, QQ News, WSJ). Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya deputy commander confirmed: "We have NOT blocked Hormuz"—contradicting the IRGC's own declarations (QQ News). The closure is selective, and the selection favors China.

Morgan Stanley's assessment that AI is now "deeply embedded in military contest" and "marks a new geopolitical phase" adds another dimension (Sputnik Chinese). The Pentagon vs. Anthropic dispute over AI weapons use—visible primarily in Chinese and Japanese media—signals that the technological competition underlying great power rivalry is being reshaped in real time by this conflict.

The strategic calculus: China loses a key anti-Western partner if the Iranian regime falls, but gains leverage over energy markets, demonstrates that US military adventurism creates global economic disruption, and positions itself as the responsible alternative. As Gering notes, "whether China is too blinkered by great-power ideology to recognize that opportunity remains the defining question."

Russia: The Third Humiliation

The Atlantic Council's Brian Whitmore delivered the sharpest assessment: "Iran's rulers are now absorbing the same bitter lesson learned by the autocrats of Syria and Venezuela before them. For all its talk of establishing a multipolar world... Moscow lacks the will and the capacity to come to the aid of its alleged partners" (Atlantic Council, March 5). Khamenei is "the third Kremlin-backed autocrat to fall in the past fifteen months"—after Assad (December 2024) and Maduro (January 2026).

The Russia-Iran contradiction is now public. Iran's Foreign Ministry claims to be receiving "political and other help" from Russia and China (Kommersant). The same day, Kremlin spokesman Peskov stated that Iran had requested no assistance whatsoever. This is not coordinated messaging—it is diplomatic friction visible in real time. As Brookings' Pavel Baev noted, Putin's message of condolences decried Khamenei's "assassination" as a "cynical violation of all norms of human morality and international law, which rings hollow for any researcher of the Russian way of war, but betrays his angst" (Brookings, March 3).

Chatham House's analysis is definitive: "If Iran becomes consumed by war, and if its capacity to act as a regional balancer wanes, Russia faces a sequential attrition of strategic depth. The wider geopolitical architecture could shift, from a multipolar balance where Moscow plays off rivals against each other, to a more fragmented environment in which Russia is reactive rather than proactive" (Chatham House, March 2).

Assessment: Beijing emerges as the relative strategic winner—not through action but through restraint. Russia's brand as a reliable partner in the "multipolar" order is now effectively destroyed. The question for the next decade is whether states seeking alternatives to the US-led order will look to China alone, or whether the concept of a coherent anti-Western bloc simply dissolves.

    VIII. Long-Term Geopolitical Forecast

    The Five-Year Horizon (2026-2031)

    Iran with weakened regime: The most likely near-term outcome. Even if regime change fails—and Brookings' Maloney cautions that "the deeply embedded networks and institutions that have underpinned the Islamic Republic for nearly half a century ensure that, at least in the near term, the vestiges of the power structure continue to hold an overwhelming advantage over any challengers"—Iran's conventional military capability is being systematically destroyed. With 80% of air defenses eliminated, 60% of ballistic missile launchers destroyed, 30+ naval vessels sunk, and key nuclear facilities struck, Iran's ability to project power is measured in years of rebuilding, not months.

    Proliferation cascade: The destruction of Iran's nuclear program—paradoxically—increases proliferation risk. As the Stimson Center noted, the lesson every aspiring nuclear state draws from this conflict is: "If you don't have nuclear weapons, you can be attacked with impunity. If you do, you can't" (Stimson Center, March 3). North Korea, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, Egypt, and others will update their calculations accordingly. The RUSI analysis of nuclear proliferation implications will likely dominate arms control discussions for a decade.

    Energy market restructuring: European gas prices up 70% since the war began (Xinhua/WSJ). Brent crude trending toward $84, with Bloomberg modeling $108 if Hormuz fully closes. Japan—90% dependent on Middle Eastern oil via Hormuz—is already requesting strategic reserve releases and cancelling fuel exports (Bloomberg Japan). India, importing 40% of its oil through Hormuz, has entered "war mode" (News18 Hindi). The acceleration of energy transition investment is now not just a climate imperative but a national security one. Countries that reduce fossil fuel dependence reduce their vulnerability to exactly this kind of disruption.

    The post-American Middle East: Whether the US achieves its maximal objectives or not, the war will accelerate the trend toward reduced American engagement in the region.     A new Gulf security architecture: The Gulf states' forced alignment with the US against Iran may paradoxically accelerate their pursuit of autonomous defense capabilities—including nuclear hedging by Saudi Arabia, expanded UAE military capabilities, and deeper cooperation with non-US partners (the FT report of Gulf states purchasing Ukrainian interceptor drones is an early signal).

    The Twenty-Year Horizon (2026-2046)

    The precedent problem: The norms destroyed this week—against assassinating heads of state, against preemptive war without multilateral authorization, against selective chokepoint closure—do not regenerate spontaneously. They must be rebuilt through new agreements, new institutions, or new power structures. The most likely outcome is not reconstruction but fragmentation: a world of competing regional orders, each with its own norms, enforced by whoever has the most capable military in the neighborhood.

    Iran's future: The country of 88 million people, with its ancient civilization, educated population, and resource wealth, will remain a major regional actor regardless of its government's form. The January 2026 uprising—which preceded the war—demonstrated that the Iranian population is capable of revolutionary mobilization. Whether that energy is channeled toward democratic governance or consumed by post-war chaos depends on decisions being made now, primarily by Iranians themselves but shaped by the international environment.

    The AI warfare paradigm: The first broad use of AI in combat operations (confirmed by multiple sources including Sputnik Chinese, Morgan Stanley, Pentagon statements) establishes this conflict as the opening chapter of a new era in warfare. The Pentagon-Anthropic dispute over AI weapons ethics, visible primarily in Asian media, previews a debate that will dominate defense policy for a generation.

    Conclusion: The Order That Was

    The US-Iran War of 2026 is not merely a regional conflict. It is a stress test of the entire post-1945 international order—and the order is failing. The UN Security Council cannot act. International humanitarian law cannot be enforced. Maritime freedom of navigation cannot be guaranteed. Sovereignty norms cannot protect a state from regime change. Nuclear non-proliferation agreements cannot survive the destruction of their verification mechanisms.

    What replaces these institutions and norms is the defining question of the next quarter century. The optimistic scenario—that the shock of this conflict catalyzes reform of international institutions, much as World War II catalyzed the creation of the UN—requires a level of international cooperation that is nowhere in evidence. The pessimistic scenario—that we enter an era of unconstrained great power competition, regional fragmentation, and norm-free military action—is already taking shape.

    As Brookings' Mara Karlin observed: "The Islamic Republic has operated like there is no tomorrow—which is plausible given the breadth, depth, and efficacy of these attacks on its foundations" (Brookings, March 2). The same might be said of the international order itself.
